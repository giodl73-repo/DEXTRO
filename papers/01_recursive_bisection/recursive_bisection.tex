\begin{abstract}
Congressional redistricting in the United States faces persistent challenges from gerrymandering, partisan manipulation, and public distrust. While the Huntington-Hill method has successfully resolved congressional apportionment disputes for over 80 years through mathematical objectivity, district boundary drawing remains subject to political influence. We present an algorithmic approach to redistricting based on recursive bisection and graph partitioning to the boundary design problem. Applied to 2020 Census data across all 50 states, our method produces 435 congressional districts using only population and adjacency data as inputs, with mean population deviation of 2.79\%. We analyze population balance, political characteristics, and demographic representation, demonstrating that mathematical rigor can achieve traditional redistricting objectives while eliminating direct opportunities for partisan manipulation. Our results suggest algorithmic redistricting offers a viable path toward restoring public confidence in electoral system fairness.
\end{abstract}
