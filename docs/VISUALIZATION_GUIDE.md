## Map Types

The pipeline generates several types of maps, each serving a different purpose.

### Full State District Map

**File**: `outputs/us_{year}_{version}/states/{state}/maps/{state}_{N}_districts.png`

**Example**: `california_52_districts.png`

**What it shows**:
- All congressional districts in the state
- Each district colored differently for visual distinction
- Major cities (population > 50,000) labeled with dots
- State boundary in bold
- District numbers labeled inside each district

**How to read it**:

1. **District colors**: Colors are assigned to make adjacent districts visually distinct. Color has no meaning beyond visual separation.

2. **City labels**: Black dots mark city centroids. Cities are labeled with their names. The largest cities have the most prominent labels.

3. **District boundaries**: Thin black lines separate districts. These boundaries follow census tract edges.

4. **Geographic features**: Coastlines, major water bodies visible as district boundaries.

**What to look for**:

- **Compactness**: Do districts look reasonably circular/square, or are they irregularly shaped?
- **Contiguity**: Are all districts connected (no isolated pieces)?
- **Urban vs. Rural**: Small urban districts (dense cities) vs. large rural districts (sparse population)
- **Natural boundaries**: Do districts follow rivers, coastlines, mountain ranges?

**Example interpretation**:

"California's 52 districts show compact shapes in urban areas (San Francisco Bay, Los Angeles, San Diego) and larger geographic districts in rural regions (northern California, Central Valley). All districts appear contiguous."

### Individual District Map

**File**: `outputs/us_{year}_{version}/states/{state}/maps/districts/district_{dd}.png`

**Example**: `district_12.png`

**What it shows**:
- Single district zoomed to its boundary
- District shape highlighted in blue
- Surrounding districts shown in light gray (for context)
- Cities within the district labeled
- District number and population in title

**How to read it**:

1. **Blue area**: This is the district of interest.

2. **Gray context**: Surrounding districts provide geographic reference.

3. **City labels**: Shows which cities are included in this district (centroids inside boundary).

4. **Title**: "District 12 (Population: 761,169)" shows district number and total population.

**What to look for**:

- **Shape quality**: Is the district compact or irregularly shaped?
- **City coverage**: Which major cities does this district include?
- **Boundary details**: Does the boundary follow natural features (rivers, highways) or seem arbitrary?

**Use case**: Share individual district maps in reports, presentations, or public engagement materials.

### Round-by-Round Bisection Maps

**Files**: `outputs/us_{year}_{version}/states/{state}/maps/rounds/round_{dd}.png`

**Example**: `round_03.png`

**What it shows**:
- State at a specific stage of the recursive bisection algorithm
- Each region colored differently
- Round number and region count in title

**How to read it**:

**Round 0** (`round_00.png`):
- Entire state as one region
- Starting point before any splits
- Title: "Round 0: 1 region"

**Round 1** (`round_01.png`):
- State split into 2 regions
- Each region contains roughly half the state's population
- Title: "Round 1: 2 regions"

**Round 2** (`round_02.png`):
- Each of the 2 regions split in half → 4 regions
- Population balanced across regions
- Title: "Round 2: 4 regions"

**Final Round** (`round_06.png` for California):
- All 52 individual districts visible
- Each district is a separate region
- Same as full state map
- Title: "Round 6: 52 districts"

**What to look for**:

- **Balanced splits**: Do regions in each round have similar geographic sizes (correcting for population density)?
- **Contiguity maintained**: Does each split preserve contiguity (no disconnected pieces)?
- **Natural divisions**: Do early splits follow natural geographic or demographic boundaries?

**Example interpretation**:

"Round 1 splits California roughly north-south, separating the Bay Area/Northern California from Southern California. Round 2 further splits each half, creating 4 regions that roughly correspond to: Northern California, Bay Area, Los Angeles region, and San Diego region."

**Use case**: Explain the algorithm's process step-by-step. Show how 1 state becomes 2, then 4, then 8, ..., then N districts.

### National Map

**File**: `outputs/us_{year}_{version}/maps/us_all_districts.png`

**What it shows**:
- All 435 congressional districts across all 50 states
- Each state's districts colored internally
- Alaska and Hawaii shown as insets (not to scale)
- State boundaries in bold

**How to read it**:

1. **State-level view**: Focus on one state at a time - national view is too dense to see individual districts clearly.

2. **Regional patterns**: Compare district sizes across regions (Northeast vs. Great Plains vs. Alaska).

3. **Insets**: Alaska and Hawaii shown separately due to scale differences. Not geographically accurate positions.

**What to look for**:

- **Urban density**: Small districts in cities (NYC, LA, Chicago), large districts in rural areas (Montana, Wyoming).
- **State differences**: How district counts vary (California: 52, Vermont: 1).
- **Coastal complexity**: How districts handle coastlines, islands, peninsulas.

**Use case**: High-level overview, cross-state comparisons, publication frontmatter.

## Dashboard Navigation

The interactive HTML dashboards provide point-and-click access to all visualizations and data.

### Opening the Dashboard

**Single-run dashboard**:
```bash
# Windows
start outputs/us_2020_v1/index.html

# macOS
open outputs/us_2020_v1/index.html

# Linux
xdg-open outputs/us_2020_v1/index.html
```

**Master dashboard** (compare multiple runs):
```bash
open outputs/index.html
```

### Dashboard Layout

**Left Sidebar**:
- State selector dropdown (all 50 states)
- Quick stats for selected state
- Links to download CSVs and maps

**Main Content Area**:
- Tabs: Overview, Districts, Rounds, USA, Political, Demographics, Compactness, Urban Areas
- Visualizations and tables
- Interactive elements (hover, click for details)

**Top Bar**:
- Run information (year, version)
- Navigation breadcrumbs
- Settings/options

### Tab Descriptions

#### Overview Tab

**Content**:
- Full state district map
- Summary statistics table
- Key metrics highlighted

**What to look for**:
- Total districts in state
- Average population per district
- Population deviation range
- Average compactness scores

**Use case**: First stop when exploring a state. Get high-level summary before diving into details.

#### Districts Tab

**Content**:
- Table of all districts with key metrics
- Sortable columns (click header to sort)
- Links to individual district maps

**Columns**:
- District number
- Population
- Area
- Compactness (Polsby-Popper, Reock)
- Major cities

**How to use**:
- **Sort by compactness**: Find most/least compact districts
- **Sort by area**: Identify urban vs. rural districts
- **Click district number**: View individual district map
- **Click city name**: See city details

**Use case**: Compare districts within a state. Identify outliers, investigate specific districts.

#### Rounds Tab

**Content**:
- Round-by-round bisection maps
- Slider to navigate between rounds
- Table showing round progression

**How to use**:
- **Drag slider**: Move through rounds sequentially
- **Click round number**: Jump to specific round
- **Watch progression**: See how 1 state becomes N districts

**What to look for**:
- Balanced splits at each round
- How urban areas are subdivided
- How rural areas are subdivided

**Use case**: Understand the algorithm's decision-making process. Educational demonstrations, explaining methodology.

#### USA Tab

**Content**:
- National map (all 50 states)
- National statistics table
- State-by-state summary

**What to look for**:
- Total districts: 435
- National average compactness
- State-by-state district counts
- Cross-state comparisons

**Use case**: National overview, compare states, aggregate statistics.

#### Political Tab

**Available**: 2020 only (no data for 2010/2000)

**Content**:
- District-level presidential vote results (estimated)
- Maps colored by political lean (blue = Democratic, red = Republican)
- Competitive district highlights

**How to read the map**:

**Color scale**:
- Deep blue: Strong Democratic (margin > +20%)
- Light blue: Leans Democratic (+10% to +20%)
- Purple: Competitive (±10%)
- Light red: Leans Republican (-10% to -20%)
- Deep red: Strong Republican (margin < -20%)

**What to look for**:
- Number of competitive districts (purple)
- Partisan balance (total D vs. R seats)
- Efficiency gap (wasted votes analysis)
- Proportional representation (vote share vs. seat share)

**Important**: These are estimated results based on tract-level modeling, not actual election results for these new districts.

**Use case**: Assess partisan fairness, identify competitive districts, compare to current district maps.

#### Demographics Tab

**Content**:
- District demographics (race/ethnicity, sex)
- Maps colored by demographic composition
- Majority-minority district identification

**How to read the map**:

**Color options** (toggle in dashboard):
- White (Non-Hispanic) percentage
- Black (Non-Hispanic) percentage
- Asian (Non-Hispanic) percentage
- Hispanic/Latino percentage
- Diversity index (Simpson's Diversity Index)

**Color scale**: 0% (light) to 100% (dark) for each demographic group.

**What to look for**:
- Majority-minority districts (where a minority group is >50%)
- Demographic representation vs. statewide demographics
- Voting Rights Act compliance (Section 2)
- Community of interest preservation

**Use case**: Assess demographic fairness, identify minority representation, check VRA compliance.

#### Compactness Tab

**Content**:
- Compactness distribution histograms
- Comparison to baseline (existing districts)
- State ranking by average compactness
- Outlier identification

**Histogram interpretation**:

**X-axis**: Polsby-Popper score (0.0 to 1.0)
**Y-axis**: Number of districts

**Ideal distribution**: Right-skewed (most districts have high compactness)

**What to look for**:
- Average compactness (vertical line on histogram)
- Outliers (districts with very low scores)
- Comparison to baseline (algorithm vs. current maps)
- Improvement percentage

**Color coding**:
- Green: Above state average (more compact than typical)
- Yellow: Near state average
- Red: Below state average (less compact, investigate)

**Use case**: Quantify compactness, compare to existing districts, identify problematic districts.

#### Urban Areas Tab

**Content**:
- Maps showing urban vs. rural district classification
- Urban area boundaries overlaid
- Population density analysis

**Classification**:
- **Urban**: District contains major city or >80% urban population
- **Suburban**: Mixed urban/rural, adjacent to city
- **Rural**: <20% urban population, large geographic area

**What to look for**:
- Urban core representation (cities split across districts?)
- Suburban ring allocation
- Rural district sizes (very large geographic areas)

**Use case**: Understand urban/rural balance, identify city-splitting issues.

## Interpreting Compactness

Compactness is a key metric for assessing redistricting quality. Higher compactness suggests less gerrymandering.

### Polsby-Popper Score

**Formula**: `4π × Area / Perimeter²`

**Intuition**: How close is this district to a circle?

**Scale**: 0.0 (extremely irregular) to 1.0 (perfect circle)

**Typical values**:
- **0.8-1.0**: Very compact (rare in practice, only in simple geographic regions)
- **0.5-0.8**: Compact (good districts)
- **0.3-0.5**: Moderately compact (acceptable, may reflect geography)
- **0.15-0.3**: Irregular (concerning, investigate causes)
- **0.0-0.15**: Highly irregular (likely gerrymandered or extreme geography)

**Visual guide**:

```
Polsby-Popper Score:          Visual Shape:                    Interpretation:

    0.9-1.0                    ╔════════╗                       Very Compact
                              ║        ║                       (Ideal shape)
                              ║        ║
                              ╚════════╝

    0.5-0.8                  ╔══════════════╗                  Compact
                            ║              ║                  (Good district)
                            ║              ║
                            ╚══════════════╝

    0.3-0.5                ╔════════════════════╗              Moderately Compact
                          ║                    ║              (Acceptable)
                          ╚════════════════════╝

    0.15-0.3             ╔════════════════════════════╗        Irregular
                        ║                            ║        (Investigate)
                        ╚════════════════════════════╝

    0.0-0.15            ╔═══╗      ╔════╗                     Highly Irregular
                       ║   ╚══════╝    ║                     (Likely gerrymander)
                       ║ ╔════╗   ╔════╝
                       ╚═╝    ╚═══╝

Real-world examples:
• Wyoming at-large: 0.67 (fairly compact, roughly square)
• Maryland District 3: 0.14 (highly irregular, "tentacles")
• Hawaii Districts: 0.20-0.30 (low due to island geography, acceptable)
```

**Factors that lower scores**:
- Long, thin districts (high perimeter for area)
- Indented boundaries (fingers, tentacles)
- Archipelagos (island chains like Hawaii)
- Coastline following (natural feature, not gerrymandering)

**When low scores are acceptable**:
- Islands (Hawaii, coastal states)
- Mountain ranges (natural barriers)
- Rivers/lakes (following water boundaries)
- Legal requirements (preserving communities of interest)

### Reock Score

**Formula**: `District Area / Minimum Bounding Circle Area`

**Intuition**: How much of the smallest circle containing this district is actually filled?

**Scale**: 0.0 (district barely touches circle) to 1.0 (district fills circle)

**Typical values**:
- **0.7-1.0**: Very compact
- **0.5-0.7**: Compact
- **0.3-0.5**: Moderately compact
- **0.1-0.3**: Irregular
- **0.0-0.1**: Highly irregular

**Comparison to Polsby-Popper**:
- Reock scores are typically higher (less punitive)
- Reock is less sensitive to boundary complexity
- Polsby-Popper penalizes tentacles more heavily

**Use both metrics**: If both scores are low, district is likely problematic. If only Polsby-Popper is low, check for natural geographic features.

### Baseline Comparison

**Baseline**: Existing congressional districts (pre-algorithm)

**Comparison metrics**:
- **Improvement percentage**: How much more compact are algorithm-generated districts?
- **Side-by-side maps**: Visual comparison
- **Distribution shift**: Histogram of old vs. new scores

**Example**:
```
Baseline average Polsby-Popper: 0.28
Algorithm average Polsby-Popper: 0.42
Improvement: +50% (algorithm is 50% more compact)
```

**Interpretation**:
- Positive improvement: Algorithm creates more compact districts
- Large improvement (>30%): Existing districts likely gerrymandered
- Small improvement (<10%): Existing districts were already reasonably compact
- Negative improvement: Investigate - algorithm may need tuning or geography is constraining

## Political Analysis

**Available**: 2020 only

### Understanding the Maps

**Political lean map**: Districts colored by partisan margin.

**Color interpretation**:
- Blue shades: Democratic districts
- Red shades: Republican districts
- Purple: Highly competitive (margin < ±5%)
- Gray: No data (rare)

**Saturation**: Darker colors = stronger partisan lean.

### Key Metrics

#### Partisan Balance

**Question**: Do districts reflect statewide vote share?

**Example**:
- California statewide: 60% Democratic, 40% Republican
- Algorithm districts: 31 Democratic (60%), 21 Republican (40%)
- **Result**: Proportional representation achieved

**Red flag**: Large discrepancy between vote share and seat share suggests partisan gerrymandering.

#### Competitive Districts

**Question**: How many districts are competitive (could flip in close election)?

**Threshold**: Margin < ±5% is highly competitive.

**Example**:
- State has 20 districts
- 3 competitive (15%)
- 10 safe Democratic, 7 safe Republican
- **Result**: Most districts uncompetitive, few swing districts

**Interpretation**:
- High competition: More races in play, responsive to voter preference shifts
- Low competition: Most districts safely partisan, fewer competitive races

**Note**: Algorithm doesn't optimize for competitiveness - this is an emergent property.

#### Efficiency Gap

**Definition**: Difference in wasted votes between parties.

**Wasted votes**:
- Losing party: All votes (didn't win)
- Winning party: Votes beyond 50% + 1 (more than needed)

**Formula**: `(Party A wasted - Party B wasted) / Total votes`

**Interpretation**:
- Efficiency gap > 7-8%: Potential partisan gerrymandering
- Efficiency gap near 0%: Balanced wasted votes

**Example**:
- Democrats win 10 districts by 60% (waste 10% per district = 100% total)
- Republicans win 10 districts by 55% (waste 5% per district = 50% total)
- Democrats waste 50% more votes → efficiency gap favors Republicans

### Limitations

**Important**: These are modeled estimates, not actual election results.

**Data source**: County-level presidential results geocoded to census tracts, then aggregated to new districts.

**Assumptions**:
- Uniform voter distribution within counties (not always true)
- Presidential results proxy for congressional races (imperfect)
- No voter behavior changes from redistricting

**Use case**: Rough estimate of partisan lean, not precise prediction.

## Demographic Visualization

### Reading Demographic Maps

**Map types**:
- Choropleth maps: Districts colored by demographic percentage
- Pie charts: Show demographic composition per district
- Stacked bar charts: Compare demographics across districts

**Color scales**:
- **Single group** (e.g., Hispanic %): Light to dark (0% to 100%)
- **Multiple groups**: Qualitative colors (red, blue, green, yellow for different groups)

### Majority-Minority Districts

**Definition**: District where a racial/ethnic minority group is the majority (>50%).

**Why it matters**: Voting Rights Act (Section 2) requires preserving minority voting power.

**Visual identification**: Districts with deep color in one minority group map.

**Example**:
- District 34 (Los Angeles): 72% Hispanic
- This is a majority-minority district (Hispanic majority)

**Algorithm**: Doesn't explicitly create majority-minority districts (no racial/ethnic data used), but they may emerge from geographic clustering.

### Demographic Representation

**Question**: Do district demographics reflect statewide demographics?

**Example**:
- State: 40% White, 15% Black, 30% Hispanic, 15% Asian
- Average district: 40% White, 15% Black, 30% Hispanic, 15% Asian
- **Result**: Proportional representation

**Red flag**: Systematic under/over-representation of groups suggests packing or cracking.

**Packing**: Concentrating minority voters in few districts (reduces total minority-influenced seats).

**Cracking**: Splitting minority voters across many districts (dilutes voting power).

### Simpson's Diversity Index

**Definition**: Probability that two randomly selected people are from different demographic groups.

**Formula**: `1 - Σ(proportion_i²)` for all groups i

**Scale**: 0.0 (homogeneous) to 1.0 (perfectly diverse)

**Interpretation**:
- 0.0-0.2: Very homogeneous (one group dominates)
- 0.2-0.4: Low diversity
- 0.4-0.6: Moderate diversity
- 0.6-0.8: High diversity
- 0.8-1.0: Very diverse (no dominant group)

**Use case**: Identify most/least diverse districts, compare to statewide diversity.

## Round Progression

Understanding the recursive bisection process helps explain how districts are created.

### Round-by-Round Interpretation

**Round 0**: Starting point (entire state).

**Round 1**: First split (2 regions).
- **Look for**: North-south or east-west division? Urban-rural split? Equal population?

**Round 2**: Second split (4 regions).
- **Look for**: How are urban areas divided? Are rural regions kept together?

**Round 3-N**: Continued subdivision.
- **Look for**: When do individual cities emerge as districts? How are suburbs allocated?

**Final Round**: All N districts visible.
- **Same as full state map**: Each district is a leaf node in the bisection tree.

### Bisection Tree

**Visualization**: `rounds_hierarchy.csv` shows parent-child relationships.

**Example**:
```
Round 0: [1] (whole state)
Round 1: [2-27] (northern half), [28-52] (southern half)
Round 2: [2-14] (northeast), [15-27] (northwest), [28-39] (southeast), [40-52] (southwest)
...
```

**Interpretation**: Follow one district back to Round 0 to see its "ancestry" - which regions it was part of at each round.

**Use case**: Explain algorithm decisions, show how splits create final districts, educational demonstrations.

**Common questions**:

**Q: Why do some compact districts have low Polsby-Popper scores?**

A: Coastlines, rivers, and islands increase perimeter without adding area, lowering the score. Check the map - if the district follows natural boundaries, the low score is acceptable.

**Q: Why don't political lean maps match existing partisan boundaries?**

A: The algorithm doesn't use political data. It only considers population and geography. Any partisan outcomes are emergent from geographic clustering of voters.

**Q: Why are some urban districts split across multiple states' maps?**

A: Large cities (NYC, Chicago) may contain multiple districts. Each district is shown separately, but they're all within the same city boundary.

**Q: How do I export maps for publication?**

A: Use high-DPI renders (`--dpi 300`) for print quality. Maps are saved as PNG files in `outputs/.../maps/` directory. Open in image editor for cropping/annotation.

**Q: Can I customize map colors or styles?**

A: Yes, see [CONTRIBUTING.md](CONTRIBUTING.md) for developer guide. Visualization code is in `src/apportionment/visualization/`.
