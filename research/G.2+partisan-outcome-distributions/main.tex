\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The most frequently asked question about any redistricting algorithm is:
does it systematically favor one party?  This paper answers that question
for \ar{} by situating the algorithm's plans within the partisan outcome
distributions of \recom{} ensembles for six states.  The main finding is
negative: \ar{} is not systematically biased.  It produces partisan outcomes
within one seat of the ensemble median in five of six studied states
(NC, WI, PA, TX, CA), and its single notable deviation---Georgia at the
38th percentile of Democratic seats---reflects the state's documented
geographic partisan sorting, not algorithm design.  We characterise the
``proportionality corridor'' (the fraction of ensemble plans within one seat
of proportional) and show that \ar{} falls inside the corridor for three of
four states where this can be assessed.  We also explain why algorithm choice
determines which tail you land in, connecting the partisan outcome to B.0's
bakeoff of competing redistricting algorithms.
\end{abstract}
