# Review: G.2 — Partisan Outcome Distributions
**Reviewer**: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election law, redistricting doctrine)
**Round**: 2
**Score**: 3/4

## Summary

The revision addresses the two blocking issues and makes important progress on the framing. The Georgia corridor analysis has been added (answering my specific Round 1 question about whether compact 6D plans exist). The "geographic consistency vs. proportional fairness" distinction is now prominent. The paper is significantly more legally defensible than in Round 1. Several moderate-priority items remain unaddressed.

## Changes Evaluated

**Rodden-effect framing (B2):** The distinction is now in the abstract and prominently placed in the body. The paper no longer claims AR produces "fair" partisan outcomes — it correctly claims AR produces geographically consistent outcomes. This is the legally correct framing.

**Georgia corridor analysis:** The revised Section 3 addresses my Round 1 question directly: "Within the DeFord ensemble, approximately 28% of valid plans achieve 6D or more Democratic seats for Georgia. These plans typically have a less compact boundary that captures more Atlanta suburbs in Democratic-leaning districts — at the cost of a higher edge cut." This is the honest answer to the "your algorithm costs Democrats a seat" challenge. The paper now says: yes, compact plans with 6D exist; the AR algorithm's edge-cut objective trades that seat for compactness; this is a geometric property of the objective, not a partisan design choice. This will be effective in litigation.

**Overdispersion → underdispersion:** Correct. The legal significance: the underdispersion explanation (contiguity and population balance constraints narrow the plan space) actually strengthens the legal argument that the AR result is not an outlier — the ensemble is narrower than a purely random vote distribution would predict.

## Unresolved Issues

**Efficiency gap analysis (M2 from REVISION-PLAN): Not added.** I explicitly asked for a subsection computing the efficiency gap for the AR plans in each state. Without this, the paper remains disconnected from the standard metrics used in redistricting litigation. An expert witness for the opposing party will immediately produce efficiency gap numbers for the AR plan; the paper should preempt this by computing and contextualizing them itself.

**Comparison with enacted plans (my Round 1 concern):** The paper still does not benchmark the corridor fractions against enacted congressional plans. Without this comparison, the 28% corridor fraction for Georgia has no context — is this higher or lower than the actually enacted plans?

**"Not systematically biased" claim:** The abstract still leads with this. Given that all deviations when they occur are in the Republican direction (Georgia is the clearest case), the word "systematic" in this context deserves more careful treatment. The paper should acknowledge that the direction of deviation is geographically predictable — not random — and that this is what the Rodden effect predicts.

## Recommendation

Accept with moderate revisions. The blocking and some high-priority items are resolved. Add the efficiency gap analysis and the comparison with enacted plans for a litigation-ready paper.
