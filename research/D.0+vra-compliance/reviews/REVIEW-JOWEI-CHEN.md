# Review: Voting Rights Act Compliance Through Edge-Weighted Graph Partitioning

**Reviewer**: Jowei Chen (University of Michigan)
**Expertise**: Automated redistricting, compactness, neutrality
**Date**: February 7, 2026

---

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a systematic evaluation of optimization approaches for VRA compliance, with a genuinely interesting edge-weighting innovation that doubles success rates. The methodological rigor is strong—testing 4 approaches across 5 states with 140 edge-weighting configurations demonstrates thoroughness. However, the paper has three significant weaknesses: (1) no comparison to actual enacted districting plans, (2) insufficient validation that the algorithmic plans are actually "neutral" beyond compactness, and (3) missing discussion of how these methods compare to other automated redistricting approaches (MCMC ensembles, integer programming, etc.).

The central claim—that edge-weighting "resolves the VRA-compactness tension"—is supported for 4 of 5 states, but the paper doesn't establish that these plans are superior to human-drawn plans in terms of neutrality, compactness, or representation quality. Without this comparison, it's unclear whether this methodological advance matters in practice.

**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with minor revisions)

---

## Major Issues

### M1: No Comparison to Enacted Plans

The paper's most glaring omission: **zero comparison to actual 2020 districting plans** enacted in these states. This is critical for establishing whether algorithmic approaches provide any advantage over current practice.

**Questions requiring empirical answers**:

| State | Enacted Plan VRA Compliance | Enacted Plan Compactness | Algorithmic Advantage? |
|-------|---------------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Alabama | ? MM districts, ? avg minority % | ? edge cut / PPR | Edge-weighted: 2 MM at 50.8% |
| Georgia | ? MM districts, ? avg minority % | ? edge cut / PPR | Both methods: 5 MM at 70-77% |
| Louisiana | ? MM districts | ? | Edge-weighted: 2 MM at 55% |
| Mississippi | ? MM districts | ? | Both methods: 2 MM at 53% |
| South Carolina | ? MM districts | ? | Edge-weighted: 0 MM (fails) |

Without this comparison, I cannot assess whether:
- Edge-weighting produces **better VRA compliance** than enacted plans
- Edge-weighting achieves VRA compliance with **better compactness** than enacted plans
- The 49.6% → 50.8% Alabama "breakthrough" is meaningful (does enacted Alabama plan achieve 2 MM districts at lower compactness cost?)

**Recommendation**: Add Section 4.X "Comparison to Enacted Plans" with:
- VRA compliance (MM district count, minority percentages) for 2020 enacted plans
- Compactness metrics (edge cut if graphs available, otherwise Polsby-Popper, Reock)
- Discussion of where algorithmic plans improve upon enacted plans
- Analysis of cases where enacted plans outperform algorithms

This is standard practice in automated redistricting papers (see my Texas work, Duchin's MGGG reports).

### M2: "Neutrality" Beyond Compactness Not Established

The paper equates compactness (edge-cut minimization) with "principled" and "neutral" redistricting, but these concepts are not synonymous. A redistricting plan can be compact yet still exhibit:
- Partisan bias (favoring one party over proportion to votes)
- Incumbent protection (placing incumbents in safe districts)
- Community of interest violations (splitting cities, counties unnecessarily)

**What's missing**:
1. **Partisan symmetry**: Do these plans exhibit partisan bias? Compute efficiency gap, mean-median difference, or partisan symmetry tests using historical election data.

2. **Community preservation**: How many counties/cities are split? METIS optimizes edge cuts but has no knowledge of administrative boundaries or communities of interest.

3. **Comparison to neutral ensembles**: How do these single algorithmic plans compare to the distribution of plans generated by MCMC/random sampling? Are the VRA compliance levels typical or outliers?

**Example analysis needed**: Take Alabama edge-weighted plan (2 MM at 50.8%). Overlay with:
- 2020 presidential election results → compute Democratic seat share, efficiency gap
- County boundaries → count county splits
- MGGG ensemble data (if available) → percentile of this plan's VRA compliance and compactness

Without this analysis, calling the plans "principled" or "neutral" is an assumption, not a finding.

**Recommendation**: Add partisan analysis and community preservation metrics. If the plan is compact but gerrymanders Democrats/Republicans or unnecessarily splits communities, it's not truly neutral.

### M3: Comparison to Other Automated Methods is Superficial

The paper mentions MCMC approaches (Duchin et al.) and integer programming (Shirabe) but provides no quantitative comparison. How does edge-weighted METIS compare to:

**MCMC ensembles** (Duchin, DeFord, MGGG):
- What percentile of MCMC ensemble achieves 2 MM in Alabama?
- Do MCMC plans achieve better/worse VRA compliance than edge-weighted METIS?
- Computational cost comparison (METIS: minutes, MCMC: hours/days)

**Integer programming** (Shirabe, Oehrlein & Haunert):
- Can IP achieve VRA compliance in Alabama with provably optimal compactness?
- Scalability comparison (IP: 100s of units, METIS: 1000s)

**Shortest splitline** (Stephanopoulos):
- How does splitline perform on VRA compliance?
- Is edge-weighting compatible with splitline approach?

The paper positions edge-weighting as a breakthrough, but without comparing to alternative automated methods, I can't assess whether it's the best approach. Maybe MCMC already achieves 80% VRA success rates? Maybe IP achieves better compactness?

**Recommendation**: Either:
- Run MCMC/IP on same datasets and compare quantitatively (preferred)
- OR explicitly scope paper as "METIS-based approaches only" and acknowledge other automated methods may perform differently

## Minor Issues

### m1: Parameter Tuning Undermines "Neutrality" Claim

Edge-weighting requires choosing:
- Weight factor α ∈ {1, 5, 10, 50, 100, 500, 1000}
- Minority threshold τ ∈ {0.40, 0.45, 0.50, 0.55}

The paper tests 140 configurations and selects "optimal" configurations per state:
- Alabama: 5x @ 40% (achieves 2 MM)
- Louisiana: 5x @ 40% (achieves 2 MM)
- Mississippi: 1x @ 40% (achieves 2 MM)

**Problem**: This is **post-hoc parameter fitting to maximize VRA compliance**, which undermines neutrality claims. A truly neutral algorithm should use the same parameters for all states, chosen without knowledge of outcomes.

Compare to my approach: I use identical compactness weights and county preservation weights across all states. The paper's state-specific "optimal" parameters are essentially **tailored to achieve VRA targets**, which is what human mapmakers do.

**Questions**:
- What happens if you use the same α and τ for all 5 states? Do success rates drop?
- How sensitive are results to parameter choices? (Section 4.2.6-4.2.7 addresses this partially)
- Is there a principled way to select parameters without looking at VRA outcomes?

**Recommendation**: Either:
- Use uniform parameters across all states (e.g., 10x @ 45% for everyone)
- OR frame this as "parameter tuning can achieve VRA compliance" rather than "neutral algorithmic approach"

If parameter tuning is necessary, that's fine—but acknowledge it involves subjective choices (like human redistricting) rather than pure algorithmic neutrality.

### m2: "Compactness Cost" Analysis is Incomplete

The paper reports edge cut increases of +4% average (Table on p.12), with Alabama at -1.4% (improvement) and Louisiana at +17%. But:

1. **Edge cut is not a standard compactness metric**. Political scientists and courts use Polsby-Popper or Reock scores. How do these plans score on those metrics?

2. **District-level variation matters**. Average edge cut may mask large variation: some districts could be very non-compact even if average is acceptable. Need per-district compactness distribution.

3. **What is "acceptable" compactness cost?** The +17% for Louisiana—is this a reasonable tradeoff? How does it compare to actual Louisiana enacted plan compactness?

**Recommendation**:
- Compute Polsby-Popper and Reock scores for all districts
- Show distribution (boxplot) of district compactness, not just averages
- Compare to enacted plan compactness and to "typical" compact district benchmarks

### m3: Alabama Analysis Conflates Multiple Methods

Section 4.2.2 presents Alabama as "breakthrough" (Table on p.8), but the table mixes results from:
- Multi-constraint methods (tpwgts, ubvec) → 0 MM, maximum 49.6%
- Edge-weighted single-objective (5x @ 40%) → 2 MM, 50.8%

This is confusing. Are these comparable? The edge-weighted approach uses **completely different optimization objective** (minimize weighted edge cut) than multi-constraint (balance population + minority VAP).

**Better presentation**: Separate tables for:
- Table 4a: Multi-constraint approach variations (Methods 1-3)
- Table 4b: Edge-weighted approach variations (Method 4)
- Table 4c: Direct comparison of best-performing config from each approach

### m4: Threshold Analysis Lacks Statistical Rigor

The 42% and 36% thresholds are based on eyeballing 5 data points. This is not a robust statistical estimate. I'd expect:
- Logistic regression: P(VRA success) ~ state minority % + geographic clustering measure
- Confidence intervals on threshold estimates
- Cross-validation or out-of-sample testing (test on additional states)

With N=5 states, any "threshold" is highly uncertain. South Carolina at 35.1% fails while Alabama at 36.9% succeeds—a 1.8 percentage point difference determines the threshold? That seems fragile.

**Recommendation**: Either test more states to increase N, or reframe as "exploratory analysis suggesting threshold around 36-42%" rather than definitive finding.

### m5: Reproducibility Concerns

The paper provides insufficient detail for reproduction:
- METIS version used? (different versions produce different results)
- Random seed for METIS? (METIS has stochastic components)
- Exact graph construction (adjacency defined how? rook vs queen contiguity?)
- Census tract geometries source? (TIGER shapefiles, which year/vintage?)

I should be able to reproduce Table 1 results exactly. Currently, I could not.

**Recommendation**: Add "Data and Methods" appendix or link to public GitHub repository with:
- Data files (tract geometries, demographics)
- Complete METIS parameters (niter, ncuts, seed, ubfactor, etc.)
- Code for graph construction, METIS invocation, edge weighting

This is standard for computational papers in political science.

## Minor Corrections

- p.3, line 23: "Critical threshold discovery" is not a contribution—prior work (Lublin, Epstein, Handley) has explored demographic thresholds. Your contribution is **quantifying** the threshold for METIS-based methods specifically.

- p.7, line 64: Louisiana District 0 at 49.6% labeled as "0.4 points short"—but 50.0% - 49.6% = 0.4 percentage points, not 0.4 points. Clarify units.

- p.8, Table 2: Alabama showing 6 methods but text only describes 4 approaches. Clarify that rows 1-2 are recursive bisection variants, 3-6 are n-way/adaptive variants.

- p.12, line 316: "Alabama actually improves compactness (-1.4%)"—this seems suspicious. How does adding VRA constraint *improve* compactness? Likely this is noise/random variation, not systematic improvement. Don't oversell.

## Recommendations Summary

**Critical for publication**:
1. Add comparison to enacted 2020 districting plans
2. Analyze partisan symmetry (efficiency gap, mean-median) to establish neutrality beyond compactness
3. Compare to other automated methods (MCMC, IP) or explicitly scope to METIS-only

**Strongly recommended**:
4. Compute standard compactness metrics (Polsby-Popper, Reock) not just edge cut
5. Address parameter tuning implications for neutrality claims
6. Provide reproducibility materials (code, data, METIS parameters)

**Minor improvements**:
7. Statistical rigor for threshold analysis (logistic regression, confidence intervals)
8. Per-district compactness distributions, not just averages
9. County/municipality split analysis (community preservation)

---

## Significance

This paper makes a real contribution to automated redistricting methodology. The edge-weighting approach is clever and shows measurable improvement over multi-constraint optimization (40% → 80% success). If the additional analyses (comparison to enacted plans, partisan neutrality, other automated methods) support the conclusions, this would be a strong paper for *Political Analysis* or *APSR*.

However, without those comparisons, the paper establishes only that "edge-weighted METIS outperforms multi-constraint METIS for VRA compliance." That's a technical advance within one algorithmic framework, but doesn't demonstrate advantage over current practice (human redistricting) or alternative automated approaches (MCMC, IP). The broader significance remains unclear.

---

**Bottom Line**: Methodologically sound with interesting edge-weighting innovation, but missing critical comparisons to enacted plans and other automated methods. The "breakthrough" framing needs validation that these plans are actually better than alternatives in VRA compliance, compactness, AND neutrality. After revisions addressing these gaps, this could be an important contribution to redistricting science.
