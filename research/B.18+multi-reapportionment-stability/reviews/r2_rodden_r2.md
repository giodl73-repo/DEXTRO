# Review 2 — Reviewer: Jonathan Rodden (Political Geography / Geographic Sorting)
**Paper:** B.18 — Redistricting Stability Across Reapportionment Cycles
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

The authors have directly addressed my Round 1 requirement for geographic distribution analysis of boundary disruptions. Section 4.3 now reports that of the tracts changing district assignment in Texas (38→41), 43% are in urban cores (Houston, Dallas, Austin), 31% suburban, and 26% rural. This is the analysis I requested, and it partially addresses my concern. I am upgrading from 2/4 to 3/4. The upgrade is conditional on accepting that the urban concentration finding, while present, does not fully resolve the competitive-corridor question I raised.

## Why This Matters and What the Revision Provides

My Round 1 concern was that a 23% tract-level disruption concentrated in the I-35 corridor (the most electorally contested region of Texas) would be politically more significant than a uniformly distributed 23% disruption. The revision now shows the geographic breakdown by density type (urban/suburban/rural), finding that 43% of disrupted tracts are in urban cores.

This is useful but not quite what I asked for. I asked whether disrupted tracts are disproportionately in historically competitive vs. safe partisan areas — a different cut than urban/suburban/rural. Urban cores in Texas are overwhelmingly safe Democratic. The electorally competitive region is the suburban ring (the 31% I-35 suburban tracts). So the geographic breakdown is consistent with my concern: the 31% suburban share represents the electorally contested zone. The paper should make this explicit rather than presenting the geographic breakdown as neutral reassurance.

That said, the geographic breakdown is a genuine contribution: it shows that the disruption follows the pattern of population growth (where Texas is gaining population is where the tree changes), which is politically neutral rather than adversarial. The urban core's dominance (43%) reflects that Houston and Dallas are where the new seats are coming from, not gerrymandering logic.

## Addressed Issues

The seed variance analysis (Section 4.4) resolves my concern about single-seed Hamming distance estimates. The ±0.8pp stability across seeds confirms that the Texas d_Ham=0.23 is a reliable central estimate, not a seed artifact.

The geographic breakdown (urban/suburban/rural percentages) is now present for Texas, addressing the most important part of my C3 request. The analysis of competitive vs. safe is implicit in the urban/suburban/rural breakdown, but not explicit — I accept this as a partial response.

The clarification about the "previous map as useful starting point" note (Section 5.2) now explicitly states that this is an observation about the information content of the previous map, not a recommendation that the DIA use it as a warm start. This resolves my minor concern about contradiction with the fresh-recomputation design.

## Remaining Concerns

The Hamming distance comparison to political redistricting (35-55% disruption in political redistricting, cited as a comparative benchmark) still lacks citations. The paper now acknowledges this in a footnote ("based on the authors' analysis of redistricting changes across 2000→2010 and 2010→2020 cycles in Texas, Ohio, and North Carolina"), which is an improvement over the uncited assertion in Round 1, but the methodology for this estimate should be described more fully.

The "incumbent protection analysis" remains too brief. For legal purposes, the upper bound (at most 23% of incumbents potentially moved to a different district) should be accompanied by a lower bound and a geographic characterization of which incumbents are most at risk.

## Minor Issues

- Section 5.4 on the 2030 lobbying note is valuable. The suggestion that states may lobby for k=40=2³×5 to avoid the prime factorization disruption is the paper's most politically sophisticated insight. It should be more prominently placed.

## Recommendation

Accept. The geographic breakdown of disruption is present and the seed variance is now quantified. My core concern — that the political geography implications of boundary disruption were understated — is partially addressed. The paper is now at an acceptable level for publication.
