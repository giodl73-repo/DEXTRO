## Summary

C.7 is an important paper that fills a genuine gap: the redistricting literature has produced many point estimates but almost no uncertainty quantification. The paper's three-source UQ framework (seed variance, census sampling, resolution measurement error) is well-structured, and the synthesis into a joint CI for the +22% compactness improvement (Section 7) is the paper's most significant contribution. However, the paper has measurement theory issues that need to be addressed before it can serve as the reliable legal resource it aims to be. The Polsby-Popper section (Section 4) has a subtle conceptual error in how it treats measurement error, and the VRA section (Section 6) treats the delta method approximation as more precise than it actually is for minority VAP fractions.

## Strengths

- **The three-source taxonomy is a conceptual contribution in itself.** Distinguishing seed variance (algorithmic choice), census sampling uncertainty (input data quality), and shapefile resolution error (measurement instrument precision) separates three fundamentally different types of uncertainty that have distinct legal and practical implications. An expert witness who can answer "where does the uncertainty come from?" with this taxonomy is in a much stronger position than one who reports only a total CI.
- **The direction-of-bias analysis for PP is important and underappreciated.** Section 4.3's finding that tract-level PP systematically underestimates block-level PP by approximately +0.003 (8 of 10 states show higher PP at block resolution) means that published compactness claims are conservative: the algorithm is at least as compact as reported. This conservative bias is exactly what expert witnesses want --- uncertainty in the conservative direction.
- **The robustness table (Table 7.4) showing the +22% claim under six uncertainty scenarios is the right format for litigation.** Presenting the lower bound under the worst-case scenario (+15% absolute improvement, 95% CI lower bound with all three sources) answers the most aggressive adversarial challenge and shows that the claim survives even conservative assumptions. This format should be replicated across all major claims in the portfolio.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The PP measurement error model conflates two distinct sources: shapefile simplification and building-block resolution.** Section 4.1 lists two sources of PP measurement error: shapefile resolution error (from Douglas-Peucker simplification) and building-block resolution error (from using tract boundaries rather than block boundaries as district edges). But these two sources have different properties. Shapefile simplification affects the perimeter measurement of both algorithmic and enacted plans symmetrically (both use TIGER shapefiles). Building-block resolution error affects algorithmic plans differently depending on the resolution used for redistricting. The paper mixes these two sources throughout Section 4, treating the empirical $\Delta PP = 0.003$ from C.1 (building-block resolution) as if it captured both. The shapefile simplification error should be estimated separately --- possibly from comparing TIGER shapefiles at different tolerance levels for a sample of states --- or explicitly acknowledged as unmeasured and separately bounded.
- **The VRA delta method approximation ignores the dependence between minority VAP and total VAP.** Section 6's delta method approximation for the minority VAP fraction assumes that the coverage error rates $\epsilon_M$ (minority) and $\epsilon_T$ (total) are independent. But they are not: both are correlated with urbanicity, and minority populations in cities drive both terms. The approximation $f^* - \hat{f} \approx \hat{f}(\epsilon_M - \epsilon_T)$ is first-order correct, but the variance calculation requires knowing the covariance between $\epsilon_M$ and $\epsilon_T$ at the district level, not just their marginal distributions. For a majority-minority district in a highly urban state, the covariance is likely positive (both are undercounted in cities), which would reduce the uncertainty in the VAP fraction estimate. The paper should either compute the covariance from PES data or acknowledge that the CI for minority VAP fractions may be conservative due to positive covariance.
- **The CI formula in Section 4 contains an algebraic error.** Section 4.4 states the 95% CI for true PP as: "CI: [0.441 + 0.003 ± 1.96 × 0.003] = [0.438, 0.450]." Computing: $0.441 + 0.003 = 0.444$; $0.444 - 1.96 \times 0.003 = 0.438$; $0.444 + 1.96 \times 0.003 = 0.450$. This is arithmetically correct. However, the paper then states "the lower bound (0.438) is above the tract-level point estimate (0.441)," which is false: 0.438 < 0.441. The lower bound of the CI is below the point estimate, which is expected. The paper appears to have confused "above the enacted plan PP" (0.295) with "above the tract-level point estimate" (0.441). This error should be corrected.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **The population balance CI (Section 3.4: $|\delta_i| \pm 0.029$) deserves more careful legal framing.** The finding that a district satisfying the $\pm 0.5\%$ constitutional standard in official data may have true deviation up to $\pm 3.4\%$ due to census coverage uncertainty is legally significant but potentially alarming. The paper notes that "no redistricting method can achieve true population equality when the census itself has coverage errors" --- this is correct and should be the lead statement, not buried in Section 3.5.
- **Add a brief discussion of how UQ changes under the differential privacy constraints applied to the 2020 Census.** The 2020 PL 94-171 data were released with TopDown Algorithm noise injection, which introduces an additional source of measurement error beyond the demographic undercount discussed in Section 3. For small census units (blocks, small tracts), this noise can be substantial. The paper should acknowledge whether the calibrated $\sigma_\epsilon \approx 0.015$ captures this additional source or whether TopDown noise is a fourth source of uncertainty not addressed in C.7.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The paper's three-source UQ framework is conceptually sound and practically important. The three P1 items address real measurement theory issues: the PP measurement error source conflation, the VAP fraction covariance omission, and the factual error in Section 4.4. The first two require methodological refinement; the third is a straightforward correction.
