## Summary

C.7 applies three distinct statistical methods (bootstrap, delta method, sensitivity analysis) to quantify uncertainty from three distinct sources (seed variance, census sampling, resolution measurement error). As a statistical methods paper, it is competently executed and the three-source taxonomy is a useful conceptual contribution. The bootstrap CI framework reusing B.7's 10,000-seed realizations is efficient, and the delta method propagation for census undercount is methodologically appropriate. However, the paper has several statistical precision issues that affect the reliability of the headline CI. The variance decomposition for the joint +22% CI contains an arithmetic discrepancy between the abstract and the body, the delta method application for census uncertainty omits the within-district covariance structure, and the calibration of $\sigma_\epsilon$ from PES experimental microdata needs better documentation.

## Strengths

- **The delta method application for population deviation (Section 3.3) is methodologically sound and well-documented.** The derivation from the coverage error model through the delta method to the 95% CI ($\delta_i \pm 0.029$) is mathematically correct and clearly presented. Showing the intermediate steps (the first-order Taylor expansion, the variance formula, the empirical calibration from PES data) is exactly the right level of transparency for a paper that will be cited as a technical foundation for legal testimony.
- **The three-way separation of uncertainty sources (seed / census / resolution) maps cleanly onto actionable recommendations.** Each source has a different character: seed uncertainty is reducible (by fixing the seed, as the DIA does), census uncertainty is irreducible (fixed by official data), and resolution uncertainty is a methodological choice (using tracts vs. blocks). This three-way distinction suggests different policy responses and is a conceptually important contribution beyond the CI calculations.
- **The VRA correction direction (minority VAP systematically underestimated) is an important finding.** The finding that the 137 majority-minority district count is a conservative lower bound (corrected count: ~142) --- because census undercount disproportionately affects minority populations --- is both statistically precise and legally significant. Establishing that official Census data undercounts minority populations, and that the algorithmic VRA compliance claims are therefore conservative, preempts a class of legal challenges about whether majority-minority district counts are overstated.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The abstract and body report different CIs for the +22% claim.** The abstract states the 95% CI is "[+18%, +26%]" while the body's Table 7.4 shows "[+15%, +29%]" for "all three sources" and "[+18%, +26%]" for "seed + resolution" only. The abstract CI corresponds to the seed-and-resolution-only scenario, not the all-three-sources scenario. Since the abstract claims to account for "all three uncertainty sources," this is a factual error in the abstract. The abstract should either cite the [+15%, +29%] figure (all three sources) or clarify which sources are included in [+18%, +26%].
- **The calibration of $\sigma_\epsilon \approx 0.015$ from "PES experimental microdata" needs specific documentation.** Section 3.4 states that the SD of tract-level coverage error rates is calibrated from "the Census Bureau's tract-level undercount estimates from the 2020 PES experimental microdata." However, the Census Bureau's official PES produces state-level and demographic-group estimates, not tract-level estimates. Tract-level coverage error data would need to be derived from the experimental design using small-area estimation techniques that have significant additional uncertainty. The paper should specify: (a) exactly which PES product was used, (b) whether it provides tract-level estimates or whether extrapolation was used, and (c) what the uncertainty in the $\sigma_\epsilon = 0.015$ estimate itself is (i.e., what is the standard error of $\sigma_\epsilon$?). The calibration uncertainty propagates through to the delta method CI and may widen it substantially.
- **The joint CI derivation omits the variance in $\sigma_\text{seed}$ across states.** Section 7.2 uses $\sigma_\text{seed} = 0.010$ as a fixed national mean for the seed component. But the bootstrap CIs in Table 2.3 show substantial state-level variation in seed standard errors: from 0.000 (single-district states) to 0.012 (California, Texas). Using the national mean ignores this heterogeneity. For the joint CI of the national +22% claim, the relevant quantity is the variance of the population-weighted mean of state-level PP estimates, which requires combining state-level seed variances with their population weights. Using the national mean underestimates the joint uncertainty because states with large congressional delegations (which contribute most to the national mean PP) have systematically different seed variances than the national mean. A weighted combination of state-level seed variances should be used.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **Add a standard error for the variance ratio in the referenced C.0 results.** C.7 Section 7 uses the +22% improvement as its headline, which is computed from the algorithmic vs. enacted plan comparison. But the uncertainty in the enacted plan PP (used as the denominator) is not quantified beyond the seed + resolution CI described above. If enacted plan PP has its own measurement uncertainty (from shapefile resolution, for example), this should be propagated or explicitly noted as assumed negligible.
- **The 2020 Census TopDown Algorithm noise injection is not acknowledged as an additional uncertainty source.** The PL 94-171 data released in 2021 included differential privacy noise injected by the TopDown Algorithm. For census blocks and small tracts, this noise is non-trivial and exceeds the demographic undercount in magnitude. The paper should acknowledge whether this source of uncertainty is subsumed in the PES calibration, treated as a separate fourth source, or excluded with justification.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The delta method derivation and the three-source taxonomy are genuinely valuable. The three P1 items identify real statistical precision issues: the abstract/body CI inconsistency, the inadequately documented PES calibration, and the state-heterogeneity ignored in the joint CI. The abstract fix is trivial; the other two require additional methodological work that the paper's analysis should already support.
