## Summary

C.7 is the uncertainty quantification companion to C.0's validation portfolio. As a paper designed explicitly for use in expert witness reports and legal testimony, it gets most of the practical elements right: the motivating example (opposing counsel asking "what is the margin of error?"), the practitioner recommendations in Section 8.2, and the synthesis table showing the +22% claim under six uncertainty scenarios. From a legal and political science perspective, the paper succeeds in demonstrating that the headline claims of the portfolio are statistically robust. However, it has three significant gaps for its intended legal audience: it does not address how courts should treat the different uncertainty sources (are all three equally relevant to the legal claim?), it does not compare the CI widths to the effect sizes in relevant case law, and it conflates the legal concept of "margin of error" with the statistical concept of "confidence interval" in ways that could create problems under cross-examination.

## Strengths

- **The motivation from litigation context is exactly right.** The opening of Section 1.2 --- imagining opposing counsel asking "what is the margin of error on that difference?" --- correctly identifies the practical problem that UQ is meant to solve. This framing is rare in academic statistics papers and makes the paper immediately accessible to practitioners. The follow-up formulation of what a CI-equipped expert witness can say versus a point-estimate-only witness is the paper's strongest advocacy for why UQ matters.
- **The complete 50-state EG table (Table 5.5) is the most practically valuable table in the paper.** Providing state-level algorithmic EG estimates with bootstrap CIs and enacted EG point estimates for all 19 multi-district states gives practitioners everything they need to assess the algorithmic-enacted gap in their specific state without consulting additional sources. The reform/non-reform tagging is a useful contextualisation.
- **The distinction between seed CI (controllable) and electoral CI (uncontrollable) in Section 8.2 has legal significance that the paper identifies correctly.** Seed variance reflects an algorithmic implementation choice: a different seed would have produced different results, and the DIA's SHA-256 seeding protocol is the choice made. Electoral variance reflects external political conditions over which neither party has control. Courts may weight these differently when evaluating whether an expert's uncertainty is "reasonable." The paper correctly notes this distinction; it should develop it further.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The paper does not distinguish between CIs relevant to the legal claim and CIs relevant to algorithmic characterisation.** The three uncertainty sources have different legal relevance. Seed variance is relevant to the question "would a different algorithmic implementation have produced materially different results?" (relevant to claims about algorithmic determinism). Census uncertainty is relevant to the question "are the population equality requirements actually met?" (relevant to constitutional challenges). Resolution uncertainty is relevant to the question "is the compactness measurement accurate?" (relevant to compactness criteria claims). Courts evaluating algorithmic redistricting plans for different legal claims will emphasise different sources. The paper should include a brief legal-mapping section (analogous to C.0's Section 6.3 DIA compliance mapping) showing which uncertainty source is relevant to which category of legal challenge.
- **The "95% confidence interval" language will be misunderstood by lay audiences and potentially by judges.** A confidence interval means that 95% of intervals constructed by this method will contain the true parameter --- it does not mean that the true parameter is in this specific interval with 95% probability. This technical distinction matters in litigation: opposing counsel may challenge a CI-based assertion by arguing (correctly) that the specific interval may not contain the true value. The paper should either (a) explain the frequentist interpretation clearly and note its litigation implications, or (b) use a different framing (e.g., "the data are consistent with values in the range [X, Y] at 95% confidence levels") that is both statistically accurate and legally more robust.
- **The paper does not address how the joint CI should be cited when only one uncertainty source is relevant to a specific claim.** The +22% headline uses the joint CI from all three sources. But in a legal proceeding where only one source is challenged (e.g., opposing counsel challenges only the resolution measurement, not the seed or census uncertainty), the relevant CI to report is the single-source CI from Table 7.4, not the joint CI. The paper should provide explicit guidance on which CI to cite under which legal challenge scenario, to prevent expert witnesses from inadvertently understating or overstating uncertainty.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **Compare the CI widths to effect sizes in relevant redistricting case law.** The paper demonstrates that CIs are narrow relative to the algorithmic-enacted gaps. But courts have been more impressed by comparisons to prior testimony. If prior expert witnesses in *Whitford v. Gill*, *LWV v. Pennsylvania*, or *Harper v. Hall* testified to EG differences with no CIs, showing that the algorithmic advantage exceeds those differences even at the lower CI bound would be a powerful contextualisation.
- **Add a section on how CIs should be disclosed in the expert report itself.** The paper recommends what to report; it should also specify where and how CIs should appear in the expert report document --- in the executive summary, in each table, in the text body, or in a separate methodology appendix. Courts and opposing counsel have different norms for how statistical uncertainty should be presented in expert testimony.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The paper's practical orientation and its complete 50-state EG table are genuine contributions to the practitioner literature. The three P1 items address gaps that are specifically relevant to the paper's legal-use purpose: the missing legal-relevance mapping, the CI interpretation issue, and the single-source vs. joint-source citation guidance. These are contextual additions rather than new analyses.
