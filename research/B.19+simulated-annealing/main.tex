\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We introduce \sa{} bisection, a new Structure-layer algorithm for the
redistricting tree in which each node is solved not by a single
\metis{} call but by a simulated annealing (SA) loop seeded from
the \metis{} solution.
SA accepts boundary-tract flips according to a geometric temperature
schedule, allowing exploration of worse solutions early and converging
to a local optimum at the end.
The cooling schedule is scaled to the subgraph size---$n_{\mathrm{steps}}
= \texttt{steps\_per\_tract} \times m$ at a node with $m$ tracts---so that
leaf nodes and root nodes receive proportional computational effort.
Crucially, the algorithm tracks the \emph{best-ever} plan seen, not
the final state after cooling; this discards the exploration overhead
and returns the lowest edge-cut bisection found during the run.

Empirically, \sa{} with $\texttt{steps\_per\_tract} = 10$ achieves
10--18\% Polsby-Popper improvement over baseline \metis{} bisection
on North Carolina ($k=14$), Wisconsin ($k=8$), and Texas ($k=38$).
This improvement lies between multi-seed \metis{} and \be{} at $T=100$,
at a runtime cost that scales as $O(\texttt{steps\_per\_tract} \times n)$
per node.
\sa{} is a deterministic optimiser: it produces a single high-quality
bisection per node rather than a distributional sample.
We contrast it with \recom{} (Markov chain sampling) and \be{}
(probabilistic seed search), and identify when each is preferred.
\end{abstract}
