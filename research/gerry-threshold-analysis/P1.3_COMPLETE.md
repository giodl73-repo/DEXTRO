## What Was Done

### 1. New Section 2.2: "Gingles Framework and Study Scope" (~3 pages)

Added comprehensive subsection to sections/02_background.tex with five parts:

#### 2.2.1: The Three Gingles Preconditions

Detailed explanation of each precondition with specific requirements:

**Prong 1: Sufficiently Large and Geographically Compact**
- Numerosity: Enough minority population to form district majority (>50%)
- Geographic compactness: Concentrated enough for reasonably compact districts
- Assessment methods: Quantitative metrics (Polsby-Popper, Reock, edge-cut) + qualitative factors

**Prong 2: Politically Cohesive**
- Requirement: Minority voters must vote as bloc for preferred candidates
- Evidence needed: Statistical analysis across multiple elections
- Methods: Ecological regression, homogeneous precinct analysis, ecological inference

**Prong 3: Majority Bloc Voting**
- Requirement: White voters must bloc-vote to usually defeat minority-preferred candidates
- Evidence needed: Demonstration of racially polarized voting
- Persistence: Pattern must be consistent across multiple elections

#### 2.2.2: What This Study Addresses: Prong 1 Only

Explicitly clarifies our scope:
- Numerosity question: Can optimization create ≥50% minority districts?
- Proportionality question: Can optimization create MM districts proportional to state minority %?
- Geographic feasibility: Do constraints prevent proportional representation?

Operationalizes "geographically compact" as:
1. Contiguous districts (all parts connected)
2. Minimized edge-cut (fewer tract boundaries crossed)
3. Population equality (±0.5% of target)

#### 2.2.3: What This Study Does Not Address: Prongs 2-3

Explicitly states what we cannot assess:
- **Election data**: Requires precinct-level voting results (not available)
- **Statistical inference**: Requires ecological regression methods (not applicable to demographic data)
- **Case-specific context**: Political cohesion and polarized voting vary by jurisdiction
- **Behavioral analysis**: Concerns voter behavior, not geographic configurations

#### 2.2.4: Implications for VRA Analysis

Four key implications:
1. **Necessary but not sufficient**: Geographic feasibility required but not sufficient for Section 2 liability
2. **Burden allocation**: Threshold helps allocate proof burdens between plaintiffs and defendants
3. **Partial answer**: Answers feasibility question, not full legal compliance question
4. **Complementary evidence**: Supplements traditional VRA litigation evidence

#### 2.2.5: Why Algorithmic Analysis Is Valuable Despite Scope Limits

Five reasons our analysis matters:
1. **Eliminates geographic uncertainty**: Assess feasibility before expensive prongs 2-3 litigation
2. **Quantifies "sufficiently large"**: Operationalizes vague legal standard
3. **Reduces expert dispute**: Provides objective baseline across 645 configurations
4. **Enables ex ante planning**: Legislatures can assess feasibility before drawing maps
5. **Establishes feasibility precedents**: Creates empirical foundation for 43 diverse states

### 3. New Discussion Subsection: "Algorithmic Compactness vs Legal Compactness"

Added to sections/05_discussion.tex addressing relationship between our methods and court standards:

**Legal compactness is multidimensional**:
- Geographic compactness (shapes, geometric measures)
- Political subdivision integrity (county/municipal boundaries)
- Communities of interest (neighborhoods, cultural communities)
- Natural boundaries (rivers, mountains)

**Edge-cut minimization as proxy**:
- Respects political subdivisions (census tracts align with municipal boundaries)
- Maintains communities (contiguous minority tracts form cohesive clusters)
- Produces regular shapes (minimizing cuts discourages tentacles)
- Correlates with traditional metrics (Polsby-Popper scores)

**Limitations of algorithmic approach**:
- Doesn't explicitly consider named communities
- Ignores transportation networks
- Doesn't account for socioeconomic coherence
- Omits political context (incumbency, partisan balance)

**Why this matters for Section 2**:
- Demonstrates feasibility under one reasonable compactness operationalization
- Provides principled baseline for court evaluation
- Conservative estimate (stricter standards would make proportional MM harder)
- Courts apply jurisdiction-specific compactness standards
