## What Was Done

### 1. New Section Added: "Scope and Modeling Assumptions"

Added Section 2.4 (~3 pages) to sections/02_background.tex with five subsections:

#### 2.4.1: Research Question vs Legal Standard
- Clarifies distinction between empirical feasibility patterns (what we measure) and legal requirements (what courts mandate)
- **Research Question**: Can optimization create proportional MM districts?
- **Legal Question**: Must states create proportional MM districts for VRA compliance?
- Emphasizes findings inform but do not determine legal conclusions

#### 2.4.2: Proportional Representation as Modeling Assumption
- Explicitly states proportional representation is NOT a VRA legal requirement
- Three purposes: (1) baseline for comparison, (2) upper bound test, (3) empirical regularity detection
- Cites case law showing courts approve both more and fewer MM districts than proportional targets
- Clarifies proportionality likely provides upper bound on VRA obligations

#### 2.4.3: State-Level Threshold vs District-Level Feasibility
- Distinguishes state-level empirical patterns from district-level legal requirements
- State-level threshold provides contextual guidance, not bright-line legal test
- VRA operates at district level: specific communities in specific configurations
- Explains burden-shifting implications for different threshold ranges

#### 2.4.4: Algorithmic Feasibility vs Legal Compliance
- Addresses traditional redistricting principles courts must balance
- Notes Gingles prongs 2-3 (political cohesion, racially polarized voting) not addressed algorithmically
- Acknowledges partisan considerations and alternative configurations
- Clarifies optimization finds one feasible plan among many possible

#### 2.4.5: Contribution and Scope
- Establishes paper's contribution as empirical and descriptive, not normative or prescriptive
- **What we establish**: 42% threshold reflects demographic-geographic constraints
- **What we do not claim**: States must create proportional MM districts, 42% is legal bright-line, algorithmic feasibility alone determines compliance
- **Practical value**: Contextual guidance for feasibility assessment

### 3. Expanded Legal Implications Section

Added new subsection "From Empirical Findings to Legal Doctrine" with four paragraphs:

**How courts should use the threshold**:
- Empirical regularity, not self-executing legal standard
- One factor among many, not bright-line rule
- Provides contextual guidance with burden-shifting implications
- N=43 validation supports using threshold in feasibility assessment

**What the threshold does not determine**:
- Does NOT establish states must create proportional MM districts
- Does NOT mean states >42% automatically violate Section 2
- Does NOT exempt states <42% from Section 2 obligations
- Liability depends on all three Gingles preconditions plus totality-of-circumstances

**District-level vs state-level analysis**:
- Section 2 operates at district level: specific communities, specific districts
- State-level threshold informs but does not determine district-level feasibility
- Examples: 44% state may face constraints in specific regions; 40% state may have concentrated urban populations enabling MM districts

**Courts, legislatures, plaintiffs guidance**:
- Courts: Quantitative tool for Gingles prong 1, presumptions based on threshold
- Legislatures: Guidance on where proportional MM representation achievable
- Plaintiffs: Focus litigation on states >42% with strong empirical support
