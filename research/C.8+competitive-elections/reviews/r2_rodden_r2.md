# Review 2 — Reviewer: Jonathan Rodden (Political Geography / Geographic Sorting)
**Paper:** C.8 — Do Algorithmic Districts Produce Competitive Elections? Evidence from All 50 States
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

The revision addresses my two principal concerns: the asymmetric safe-seat conversion is now developed as a dedicated section (6.4), and the stratified enacted-map comparison separates partisan-legislature maps from commission maps. I maintain my score at 3/4. The paper is a solid contribution to the political geography of redistricting and is now ready for publication.

## Addressed Issues

The asymmetric safe-seat analysis (Section 6.4) is the most important addition from my perspective. The paper now identifies which states account for the 19-seat reduction in safe Democratic seats (primarily California, Texas, Illinois, New York — large, Democratic-leaning states with urban concentrations), and shows how the asymmetry plays out under ±4pt electoral shifts. The finding that the newly competitive Democratic seats are more vulnerable in Republican wave environments (the competitive district count falls from 85 to 71 under −4pt shift vs. staying at 73 under +4pt shift) is correctly characterized as a consequence of geographic sorting rather than algorithmic bias. This is consistent with my work on the geographic efficiency gap.

The stratified enacted-map comparison is a clean addition: algorithmic maps vs. partisan-legislature maps (85 vs. 58, +27 competitive districts) vs. commission maps (85 vs. 76, +9 competitive districts) is a much more precise comparison than the undifferentiated enacted-map baseline. The finding that algorithmic maps are close to commission maps in competitive district count validates the paper's claim that compactness-optimization achieves similar results to neutral human processes.

The geographic sorting analysis is now quantified (what fraction of the 65→85 increase is recoverable vs. structural), addressing my question. The paper estimates that approximately 14 of the 20 additional competitive districts are in "latent geographic competition" zones (where geographic distribution supports competition if the map does not pack or crack), while 6 reflect more creative boundary drawing.

## Remaining Concerns

The population-density correlation analysis to explain the urban-rural safe-seat asymmetry is present in Section 7.4 but not fully developed. The paper notes the correlation without quantifying it: the claim that "geographic sorting is the single largest factor limiting competitive districts" is qualitative. A scatter plot of competitive district count vs. urban-rural index across states would make this quantitative.

The longitudinal projection (Section 6) still uses uniform partisan shifts. I noted in Round 1 that real electoral environments involve heterogeneous shifts. The paper acknowledges this in a footnote but does not provide a sensitivity analysis.

## Minor Issues

- The Rodden (2019) citation is now operationalized as the geographic efficiency gap reference in Section 7.4. This is appropriate.
- The "31% increase" vs. "4.6 percentage point increase" framing issue I raised in Round 1 is now addressed with a footnote clarifying both framings and noting that the paper consistently uses the absolute count.

## Recommendation

Accept. The asymmetric safe-seat analysis and the stratified comparison are meaningful improvements. The paper now engages seriously with the political geography implications of its finding.
