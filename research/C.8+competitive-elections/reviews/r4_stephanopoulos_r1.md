# Review 4 — Reviewer: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law / Redistricting Reform)
**Paper:** C.8 — Do Algorithmic Districts Produce Competitive Elections? Evidence from All 50 States
**Round:** 1
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

This is the paper in the C-series that is most directly relevant to the democratic-legitimacy argument for algorithmic redistricting. The competitive elections finding is important for both legal and political audiences. The paper is well-organized and the methodology is carefully presented. My concerns are primarily about the partisan symmetry framing and the legal implications of the "byproduct not design target" argument.

## Strengths

The democratic-responsiveness framing in the introduction is accurate and well-calibrated. The claim that competitive districts produce higher turnout, stronger constituency service, and greater policy responsiveness is supported by the Jacobson (2015) citation. The engagement with the negative-partisanship / voter disengagement literature (Abramowitz) is appropriate.

The near-symmetric 43D/42R split of competitive algorithmic districts is the paper's most important finding from a legal standpoint. It directly addresses the concern — frequently raised in redistricting litigation — that algorithmic redistricting inadvertently advantages one party. The 1.024 D/R ratio versus 0.912 under enacted maps is a clean, defensible comparison.

The "byproduct not design target" framing (Sections 4.3 and the conclusion) is legally precise. An algorithm that targeted competitiveness would access partisan data, and in many states, consideration of competitiveness as a redistricting criterion is legally constrained. The paper correctly distinguishes between competitiveness as a target (problematic) and competitiveness as a byproduct (legally neutral).

## Weaknesses and Concerns

The legal treatment of competitiveness as a redistricting criterion needs to be more careful. Several states — Arizona, California, and Colorado, among others — include partisan fairness or competitiveness as statutory redistricting criteria. In these states, algorithmic maps that produce more competitive districts than enacted maps are not merely generating a neutral byproduct — they are also arguably satisfying a statutory criterion. This creates an interesting legal argument: algorithmic redistricting satisfies competitiveness criteria *by construction of its indifference to partisan data*, which is stronger than any ex-ante targeting of competitiveness could achieve.

Conversely, in states where competitiveness is not a criterion, the paper's finding that algorithmic maps produce more competitive districts than enacted maps could be used in litigation to argue that the enacted maps are suboptimal relative to a neutral baseline. After *Rucho*, federal courts cannot apply this argument, but state courts in states without an explicit competitiveness criterion might decline to adopt it as a grounds for constitutional challenge. The paper should briefly discuss the legal consequences of the competitiveness finding across the different state criterion frameworks.

The comparison in Section 5.3 (50.6% Democratic share of competitive seats vs. 51.3% national Biden share) is presented as evidence that competitive algorithmic districts "do not exhibit systematic partisan bias." But the appropriate test for partisan bias in redistricting is not alignment with the national presidential vote — it is symmetry in the relationship between vote shares and seat shares. The paper should compute the efficiency gap or mean-median difference for the algorithmic maps and compare to enacted maps. If both metrics are near zero for algorithmic maps, that is stronger evidence of partisan neutrality than the competitive-seat share test.

The conclusion's framing — "the 20 additional competitive seats represent 20 additional districts where incumbents must maintain genuine constituency contact" — is appropriate for a policy audience but should be accompanied by a caveat: these are competitive districts as measured by the 2020 presidential vote baseline, not as measured by actual congressional election outcomes. Incumbency advantage and candidate-quality effects mean that some nominally competitive districts will not be genuinely contested in any given cycle.

## Minor Issues

- The paper does not cite *Davis v. Bandemer* (1986), which established the framework for partisan gerrymandering claims before *Rucho* and in which Justice White's plurality opinion specifically mentioned the suppression of competitive districts as a harm. This is a relevant legal precedent for the competitiveness argument.
- The state-level gains table (Table 6) does not show losses. Are there any states where algorithmic maps produce *fewer* competitive districts than enacted maps? If so, which ones and why? This would strengthen the analysis.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. Add efficiency gap / mean-median analysis for the partisan symmetry claim, and add a legal analysis of how the competitiveness finding interacts with different state redistricting criterion frameworks.
