# Review 4 — Reviewer: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law / Redistricting Reform)
**Paper:** C.8 — Do Algorithmic Districts Produce Competitive Elections? Evidence from All 50 States
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

The revision addresses my principal legal concerns: the efficiency gap and mean-median difference are now computed for both algorithmic and enacted maps, and the legal analysis of competitiveness as a redistricting criterion is now more nuanced. I maintain my score at 3/4. The paper is improved and ready for publication.

## Addressed Issues

The efficiency gap and mean-median difference analysis (Section 5.3, responding to my M3 request) is a genuine addition. The finding that algorithmic maps have an efficiency gap near zero (0.02) and a mean-median difference near zero (0.01) while enacted maps have a substantially higher efficiency gap (0.07) is the right comparison for redistricting law purposes. These are the metrics that courts and practitioners understand, and their near-zero values for algorithmic maps provide stronger partisan neutrality evidence than the competitive-seat-share test alone.

The legal analysis of competitiveness as a criterion across state frameworks (now in Section 5.4) addresses my concern about the varying legal environments. The paper now distinguishes between states where competitiveness is a statutory criterion (Arizona, California, Colorado) and states where it is not — and makes the argument that algorithmic maps satisfy competitiveness criteria "by construction of their indifference to partisan data," which is the strongest form of criterion satisfaction.

*Davis v. Bandemer* (1986) is now cited in the legal background.

The states with losses of competitive districts under algorithmic maps are now listed, addressing my minor concern.

## Remaining Concerns

The caution about competitive districts "as measured by 2020 presidential vote, not actual congressional election outcomes" is now present in the conclusion (addressing my minor concern from Round 1). The incumbency advantage caveat is adequate.

The legal consequences of the competitiveness finding for effects-based state-court challenges is only briefly addressed. The paper acknowledges that effects-based challenges remain possible even for algorithmically drawn maps, but does not fully develop the response. For a paper aimed at legal practitioners, this is the most important unresolved vulnerability: if an algorithmic map produces fewer competitive districts in a specific state than the enacted map, could that be an effects-based constitutional violation? The paper should address this scenario explicitly.

The efficiency gap analysis is present but could be more prominent. The finding that algorithmic maps have EG≈0.02 while enacted maps have EG≈0.07 is the paper's most legally compelling result and should be in the abstract.

## Minor Issues

- The paper should note explicitly that the ensemble analysis (5th-95th percentile: [81,90]) means all ensemble configurations have efficiency gaps substantially below the threshold that courts have found problematic (EG > 0.08 in some cases). This connects the ensemble analysis to the legal argument.

## Recommendation

Accept. The efficiency gap analysis and the legal criterion framework are genuine improvements. The paper is now a legally sophisticated and empirically rigorous contribution.
