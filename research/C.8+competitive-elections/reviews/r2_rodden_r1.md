# Review 2 — Reviewer: Jonathan Rodden (Political Geography / Geographic Sorting)
**Paper:** C.8 — Do Algorithmic Districts Produce Competitive Elections? Evidence from All 50 States
**Round:** 1
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

This paper is directly relevant to my work on geographic sorting and electoral competition. The main finding — that algorithmic maps produce ~85 competitive districts vs. ~65 under enacted maps — is consistent with the theoretical prediction that partisan gerrymandering suppresses competition by exploiting the geographic sorting of Democrats into urban cores. The paper correctly identifies geographic sorting as the binding constraint on algorithmic competitiveness and engages seriously with the political geography literature. My concerns are about the treatment of the geographic sorting asymmetry and the durability analysis.

## Strengths

The geographic sorting analysis (Section 7.4) is the paper's strongest section from a political geography perspective. The identification of the urban-suburban boundary as the key constraint on competitive district production is correct and well-framed. The claim that "the algorithm produces competitive districts when it must cross that boundary to achieve population balance" accurately describes the mechanism.

The state heterogeneity table (Table 6) is useful: North Carolina, Texas, Ohio, and Florida as the top-gain states are consistent with their status as heavy gerrymandering targets. The characterization of the characteristics of high-gain states (geographic diversity, enacted map quality, partisan balance) is appropriate.

The engagement with the Brunell (2008) argument in Section 5.2 is sophisticated. The observation that the Brunell argument assumes symmetric safe-seat allocation — which is violated by geographic efficiency gaps — is correct and connects to the efficiency gap literature. The near-unity D/R ratio (1.024) among competitive algorithmic districts is the right way to address the partisan symmetry concern.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

The main finding that competitive districts increase from 65 to 85 primarily through a reduction in safe Democratic seats (from 208 to 189) deserves more analysis. Safe Republican seats barely change (162 to 161). This asymmetry is expected from the geographic sorting literature — Democrats are concentrated in urban cores, so any redistricting system that creates more geographically diverse districts must draw down safe Democratic seats — but it has important political implications.

Specifically: the 20 new competitive seats under algorithmic maps come primarily at the expense of safe Democratic seats. This means that in a favorable Democratic election environment (Biden+4 national shift), many of these formerly safe Democratic seats become competitive Democratic seats rather than safe ones. Conversely, in a Republican wave environment (Biden-4), these newly competitive seats swing Republican. The longitudinal analysis (Table 5) shows this: the number of competitive districts falls from 85 to 71 under a -4pt Republican wave and remains at 73 under a +4pt Democratic wave. The asymmetry in the durability table — the competitive district count falls more under a Republican wave than under an equivalent Democratic wave — reflects the underlying asymmetry in which type of safe seat is converted.

The paper notes this asymmetry in passing ("The increase in competitive districts comes primarily from a reduction in safe Democratic seats, which fall from 208 to 189. Safe Republican seats are nearly unchanged") but does not develop the implication. The implication is: algorithmic redistricting primarily helps Democrats compete in formerly safe Democratic seats that have become competitive, not primarily converts Republican safe seats. Whether this is a Democratic advantage or disadvantage depends on the election environment. In neutral or favorable Democratic environments, the new competitive seats increase Democratic seat count; in Republican wave environments, they are vulnerable.

The Rodden (2019) geographic efficiency argument is cited but not operationalized. The paper claims that "geographic sorting is the single largest factor limiting the number of competitive algorithmic districts," which is consistent with my argument that the efficiency gap is a geographic phenomenon, not a political one. But the paper should quantify this: how much of the 65-vs-85 difference is attributable to the algorithm's inability to overcome geographic sorting, and how much is attributable to enacted maps actively suppressing latent geographic competition?

## Minor Issues

- The "31% increase" claim (85 vs. 65 competitive districts) is correctly computed but the denominator matters. 20 additional competitive districts out of 435 is a 4.6 percentage point increase in the competitive share. The 31% figure is the increase in competitive district *count* relative to the enacted baseline. Both are valid framings, but the paper should be consistent in using one.
- The longitudinal projection (Section 6) uses uniform partisan shifts. Real electoral environments involve heterogeneous shifts — some states shift more than others. The uniform-shift projection is a simplification that the paper should acknowledge.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. Develop the asymmetric safe-seat conversion analysis, and quantify what fraction of the 65→85 increase is recoverable (latent geographic competition) vs. unrecoverable (structural geographic sorting).
