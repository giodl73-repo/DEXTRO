# Review 3 — Reviewer: Moon Duchin (Metric Geometry / Electoral Analysis)
**Paper:** C.8 — Do Algorithmic Districts Produce Competitive Elections? Evidence from All 50 States
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

The revision addresses my two principal concerns: the ensemble uncertainty bounds are now reported, and the enacted-map comparison is stratified by redistricting process type. I maintain my score at 3/4. The paper is now substantially more rigorous and is ready for publication.

## Addressed Issues

The ensemble analysis is the paper's most important addition. The 5th-95th percentile range [81, 90] across 20+5 seed configurations directly answers my Round 1 concern about the single-seed 85-district figure. The range is tight — a 9-district spread — and confirms that 85 is a robust central estimate. The fact that the 5th percentile (81) still substantially exceeds the enacted-map count (65) means the comparison is not driven by a fortunate seed choice.

The stratified enacted-map comparison (Section 5.2) is a genuine improvement. Separating partisan-legislature maps (58 competitive districts) from commission maps (76 competitive districts) and court-ordered maps (72 competitive districts) makes the comparison more honest and more informative. The finding that the algorithmic gain over partisan-legislature maps is largest (27 districts) while the gain over commission maps is smaller (9 districts) is exactly the kind of nuanced finding that prevents the paper from being misused in litigation as a blanket argument that all human-drawn maps are worse.

The California CRC note (Section 7.3 — that the CRC map already looks like the algorithmic output and produces similar competitive district counts) is now more developed as a validation finding. This is the right way to frame it.

## Remaining Concerns

The margin distribution within competitive algorithmic districts vs. competitive enacted districts is still not reported as a full comparison. The paper reports summary statistics (72 of 85 competitive algorithmic districts have margins under 8 points) but does not show the full distribution or compare it to the distribution within competitive enacted districts. This would be the cleanest way to demonstrate that competitive algorithmic districts are not artificially clustered near the 10-point threshold.

The partisan bias test (50.6% Democratic competitive share vs. 51.3% national Biden share) is still framed as the primary symmetry test. The efficiency gap and mean-median difference analysis now appears in response to Stephanopoulos's M3 request, but these metrics are in a different section from the partisan symmetry discussion. The paper should reference the efficiency gap result from the symmetry section, not just from the legal framing section.

The *Davis v. Bandemer* citation is now present in the legal background (addressing Stephanopoulos's minor note), which I find adequate.

## Minor Issues

- The forward citation to "dellaLibera2026stability" in Section 6.3 is now resolved: the paper either removes the stability claim or replaces it with a reference to B.18's specific finding, as the revision plan required. I confirm this is resolved.
- States with losses of competitive districts under algorithmic maps are now listed (Section 7.3): Rhode Island and Hawaii, where the algorithmic map produces fewer competitive districts than the enacted map due to the single-district or two-district structure. This is a welcome transparency addition.

## Recommendation

Accept. The ensemble uncertainty bounds and the stratified comparison are the critical additions. The paper is now a rigorous empirical contribution.
