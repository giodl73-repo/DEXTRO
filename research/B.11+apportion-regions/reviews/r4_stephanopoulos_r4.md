# Review: B.11 ApportionRegions — Round 4
## Reviewer: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Harvard Law School)
**Expertise**: Constitutional law, redistricting law, voting rights

---

### Summary

The constitutional argument has improved substantially across four rounds. The Round 4 revision of Step 3 — from "literally zero degrees of freedom" to "no discretionary choices remain once the census data and seat count are fixed" — is the change I most wanted. This is a legally accurate statement that AR can support with empirical evidence. I am prepared to recommend acceptance.

### Strengths

1. **"No discretionary choices" is the legally correct formulation.** This framing tracks judicial reasoning about the distinction between ministerial and discretionary acts. A redistricting plan with no discretionary choices would be treated differently by courts reviewing its constitutionality than one with thousands of human choices. The Round 4 language captures this correctly.

2. **The consistency of the formulation across the paper.** The abstract now reads "no discretionary choices once the census data and seat count are fixed," Step 3 of the constitutional argument uses the same language, and the ensemble comparison section echoes it. Terminological consistency is important for a paper making constitutional claims.

3. **Moore v. Harper engagement.** I raised this as a P2 item in Round 3. I note that Round 4 does not add the Moore engagement I requested. However, given the other improvements, I will not block acceptance on this — it can be added at the journal revision stage.

### Weaknesses

1. **The Article I §2 vs. §4 argument is still underdeveloped.** The paper argues that AR is an exercise of the apportionment power (§2) rather than the Manner clause (§4). This is a creative and potentially important constitutional claim. But it needs one more step: what does it mean for Congress to exercise the apportionment power through an algorithm? The paper would benefit from a paragraph engaging *Wesberry v. Sanders* (1964) directly on the distinction between seat allocation and geographic allocation.

2. **Moore v. Harper still not engaged.** *Moore v. Harper*, 600 U.S. 1 (2023), held that state courts may review redistricting under state constitutional law. This is directly relevant to AR's constitutional argument: if AR is mandated by a federal statute, state courts could still review it under state constitutions. The paper should note this interaction in one sentence.

3. **Constitutional argument is confined to §5.** The abstract and introduction should flag that the constitutional argument appears in §5, not defer it entirely to the discussion. Readers interested in the legal implications should be able to find the argument without reading the whole paper.

### Questions for Authors

1. If AR is an exercise of the §2 apportionment power, what happens when a state legislature draws different districts? Is there a preemption argument, or does AR require federal legislation?

### Suggestions

- **P2**: Add one sentence engaging *Moore v. Harper* in §5.1 (constitutional argument).
- **P2**: Add one paragraph engaging *Wesberry* on the §2 vs. §4 distinction.
- **P3**: Forward-reference the constitutional argument in the introduction.

### Verdict

[X] Accept with Minor Revisions

**Rationale**: The constitutional language revision is the key change I required. "No discretionary choices remain once the census data and seat count are fixed" is defensible and legally precise. The Moore and Wesberry items are P2/P3 and can be addressed at the journal stage.

**Score: 3.6 / 4.0** (up from 3.2)
