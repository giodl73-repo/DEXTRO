# Review: G.4 — Ensemble Diagnostics Paper
**Reviewer**: Jonathan Rodden (Political geography, partisan sorting)
**Round**: 1
**Score**: 3/4

## Summary

G.4 addresses the technical question that political scientists and lawyers should care about but rarely examine: are published redistricting ensembles actually converged? The paper's central finding — that 10,000-step chains are adequate for states with $k \leq 20$ but marginal or inadequate for Texas and California — has direct implications for how ensemble evidence is treated in litigation. The statutory minimum recommendations are well-motivated.

## Strengths

The paper's practical orientation is its greatest strength. Instead of just presenting theoretical convergence results, it provides a state-specific table of recommended minimum chain lengths that practitioners can use directly. The finding that Texas requires 25,000 steps and California 50,000 steps is a concrete recommendation that would prevent the most common type of expert-witness challenge (under-mixed ensembles).

The identification that ESS is the binding constraint for large states (TX, CA) while Rhat is the binding constraint for small states (NC, WI) is a useful practical distinction. For courts evaluating expert evidence, this means the right question to ask about a Texas ensemble is "what is the ESS?" rather than "what is the Rhat?"

The ConvergenceSweep comparison in Section 6 (mixing time vs. CS runtime) is a compelling empirical argument for why CS is better for plan generation while certified ReCom is better for plan evaluation. The finding that CS completes in under 60 seconds for any state while certified ReCom requires 4 minutes to 5 hours makes the complementary-tools argument concrete.

## Weaknesses

**The paper's characterisation of the Herschlag 2020 and DeFord 2021 ensembles as "adequate" is implicit but not stated.** If the paper is arguing that 10,000 steps is the statutory minimum for NC ($k = 14$) and NC was converged at 2,000 steps, then the Herschlag ensemble of 24,518 plans is well within the converged regime. But the paper never explicitly states "the ensembles used in G.1 are certified as converged under these standards." This explicit certification is what makes G.4 useful as a companion to G.1.

**The paper does not address multi-chain vs. single-chain runs in the context of published ensembles.** The Herschlag 2020 ensemble was a single chain of 24,518 plans. G.4 computes Rhat from 10 parallel chains. For a single-chain run, Rhat is undefined — only ESS and Hamming autocorrelation can be computed. The paper should address whether the G.1 ensemble sources met ESS criteria even if Rhat certification is unavailable.

**The calibration of the $\hat{\rho}_1 < 0.95$ threshold (Condition 3 in the statutory standards) appears arbitrary.** Any ReCom chain will have $\hat{\rho}_1 \approx 0.85$–$0.95$ by construction (from Section 3.1). A threshold of 0.95 would reject chains only if they are nearly frozen — i.e., every step is rejected. This is more a sanity check on the proposal than a convergence criterion. The paper should discuss what $\hat{\rho}_1 < 0.95$ is actually screening for.

## Minor Issues

- The Rhat threshold inconsistency with G.0 (1.05 vs. 1.1) needs resolution — this paper consistently uses 1.1 but G.0's body uses 1.05.
- The application section refers to "randomised initial plans generated by random spanning-tree bisection with no compactness objective." This is an important methodological choice: starting chains from random plans rather than from the AR plan or a human-drawn plan ensures the chains are exploring the full distribution rather than the high-compactness region. The paper should note that starting from the AR plan would give faster apparent convergence but would not certify stationarity.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. Add explicit convergence certification for the G.1 source ensembles, address single-chain vs. multi-chain Rhat, and justify the 0.95 Hamming threshold.
