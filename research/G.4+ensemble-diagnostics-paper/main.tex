\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Redistricting ensembles generated by Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
methods such as \recom{} are increasingly used to evaluate whether an
enacted map is an outlier in the space of valid plans.
The validity of any such comparison depends on whether the chain has
mixed---a question that is routinely elided in practice.
This paper formalises three convergence diagnostics for redistricting
ensembles: the Gelman-Rubin potential scale reduction factor ($\rhat$),
effective sample size (\ess{}), and Hamming autocorrelation ($\ham$).
We derive state-specific thresholds, apply them to ten parallel \recom{}
chains across six states (NC, WI, GA, PA, TX, CA), and show that typical
runs of 10{,}000 steps yield $\ess{} \approx 500$--$2{,}000$ for
Polsby-Popper compactness and $\rhat < 1.1$ after 2{,}000--20{,}000 steps.
We show that the \cs{} algorithm introduced in B.16~\citep{b16paper}
is a deterministic, $O(T \cdot n)$ alternative that provides a
certifiable stopping criterion without relying on asymptotic MCMC theory.
For statutory use under the \dia{}, certified convergence requires
approximately 10{,}000 steps of ensemble sampling---whereas \cs{}
achieves stronger guarantees at $T = 600$ sweeps.
The diagnostics are implemented in \texttt{redist analyze
--types ensemble-diagnostics}.
\end{abstract}
