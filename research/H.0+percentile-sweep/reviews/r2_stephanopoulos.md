## Review: PercentileSweep — Statutory Choice of Legal Posture in Algorithmic Redistricting (Round 2)

### Response to Round 1 Concerns

**S-P1-A (Karcher analogy qualification)**: Not resolved. The §5.1 statute box and surrounding prose still state the Karcher framework without qualifying that this is an advocacy argument by structural parallel, not an established federal holding. The revised paper retains "Under Karcher's logic, any challenger must produce an alternative plan that is at least as compact" without the qualifier "under the Karcher framework by analogy." I specifically requested that the statute box language say "under the Karcher framework by analogy" rather than implying direct doctrinal support. This has not been added. The Karcher analogy is the paper's core legal architecture; presenting it as established precedent rather than a reasoned structural parallel understates the doctrinal gap and could mislead practitioners.

**S-P1-B (structural-bias challenge route)**: Not resolved. The paper still does not acknowledge the distinct challenge route in which a litigant argues that the algorithm family (not the percentile) bakes in partisan outcomes. The revised paper's §4.2 TX/CA note and §6.3 future-work section do not address this. The paper continues to present the insensitivity finding as a universal defense against partisan objections, when it is specifically a defense against the percentile-choice objection. A structurally biased algorithm family produces the same partisan outcome at every percentile precisely because the structure bakes it in. This distinction was my P1-B in Round 1 and it remains completely unaddressed. B.0 and B.17 are cross-referenced in the conclusion but not in §5 where the legal argument is made.

**S-P1-C (VRA posture)**: Not resolved. The three-posture taxonomy in §5 still omits the VRA interaction. For Georgia specifically, the 6D/8R outcome at any percentile is not assessed against VRA requirements for majority-minority districts. The §6.3 future-work mention of "VrASection" is insufficient; the legal posture section needs at minimum a paragraph noting that VRA compliance operates independently of or in interaction with percentile choice, and that a fourth posture — VRA-constrained compactness — may be legally required in covered jurisdictions.

### Assessment of Round 2 State

All three of my P1 items remain unresolved. The revision addressed the TX/CA data integrity issue (which was primarily Rodden and Liang's concern) and the Duchin statute box conflation, but it did not engage the legal framework concerns I raised. For a paper whose primary contribution is a legal framework, three unresolved legal P1 items is a significant shortfall.

However, I note that the paper's core legal contribution — making the compactness percentile a visible statutory parameter — remains sound. The Karcher analogy issue (S-P1-A) is a qualification, not a retraction. The structural-bias issue (S-P1-B) requires adding two paragraphs, not reorganizing the paper. The VRA issue (S-P1-C) requires acknowledging a limitation. None of these require new experiments.
