\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Flip proposals---moving a single boundary tract from one district to an
adjacent district---are the coarsest-grain redistricting perturbation.
They run in $O(1)$ time per step, accept at high rates ($\approx$40--70\%
in practice), but mix exponentially slowly: reaching a fundamentally
different plan requires $O(\text{district size})$ consecutive tract moves.
This makes \flip{} unsuitable for ensemble sampling of the full plan space,
but ideal for \emph{local sensitivity analysis}: given a submitted plan, how
stable is the plan's partisan outcomes under single-tract perturbations?
We implement \flip{} in \texttt{redist-cli} as \texttt{SeedCompositor::Flip},
define EC stability and partisan stability as sensitivity metrics, and
characterise their behaviour on North Carolina ($k=14$) and Wisconsin ($k=8$).
Plans with high partisan stability under \flip{} are more defensible in
litigation; plans with low stability should disclose a full $p$-sweep.
The \flip{} audit chain records \texttt{visited\_count} and
\texttt{selected\_plan\_rank} in the manifest, providing a reproducibility
guarantee stronger than seed-only recording.
\end{abstract}
