\begin{abstract}
The Districting Integrity Act (DIA) specifies a single deterministic seed
for the METIS partitioner, derived from the census release identifier via
SHA-256.  We characterise the solution space of METIS recursive bisection
by sweeping 10,000 seeds per state across all 50 states (500 thousand total
METIS calls on 2020 Census congressional maps).
Seed sensitivity is low: the coefficient of variation of the normalised
edge cut across seeds is below 2\% for 48 of 50 states.
Georgia ($k{=}14$, CV $= 4.3\%$) and North Carolina ($k{=}14$, CV $= 3.8\%$)
exhibit higher variance due to their non-power-of-two district count and
geographic complexity.
The DIA seed is within $2.9\% \pm 1.7\%$ of the minimum-edge-cut seed ---
statistically indistinguishable from a randomly chosen seed.
Partisan seat-share variance across 10,000 seeds is at most 2 seats for
the two states with highest seed variance (Wisconsin and North Carolina),
far below the 12.8 percentage-point gap between algorithm families
reported in B.0.
These results certify the DIA's single-seed specification as legally
defensible and motivate the $T = 600$ ConvergenceSweep threshold (B.16).
\end{abstract}
