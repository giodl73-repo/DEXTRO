## Summary Assessment

Policy briefs inevitably simplify. I accept that. My question is whether this brief simplifies in ways that would mislead a legislator or commissioner, and I believe it does in two respects. First, the North Carolina partisan finding is presented without the Georgia counterexample that, together with NC, is B.11's actual finding. Second, the three adoption paths have constitutional viability issues that are not disclosed.

## Partisan Framing: North Carolina Without Georgia

Section 3 presents the North Carolina 7D/7R finding as evidence that the algorithm produces proportional outcomes. As I noted in my review of A.3, Paper B.11 reports this alongside Georgia 5D/9R — the same algorithm, the same factorization ($k=14 = 7 \times 2$), and an almost identical partisan division (NC 49.3% D, GA 50.1% D), but dramatically different partisan outcomes. B.11 discusses this divergence as its central finding.

A policy brief that presents NC as evidence of proportional outcomes without disclosing the GA result is misleading. A legislator who reads this brief and then commissions an independent analysis of what the algorithm would produce for Georgia would find a 5D/9R outcome — 14 percentage points biased — and would reasonably conclude they had been deceived by the brief. This is not a matter of minor omission; it goes to the document's central claim of partisan fairness.

The brief should either: (a) present the national aggregate (223D/209R, 51.6% D national) rather than cherry-picking the most favorable state, or (b) disclose that outcomes vary by state and explain why NC produces a proportional result while Georgia does not.

## "Closely Proportional in Partisan Representation"

The pull-quote claims districts are "closely proportional in partisan representation." This is inconsistent with Paper C.5, which documents a persistent $-3.2\%$ Democratic efficiency gap in algorithmic plans — a structural disadvantage attributable to geographic voter concentration, not to the algorithm's design. "Closely proportional" is an overstatement; "reduced partisan bias compared to enacted maps" would be accurate. C.5's 62% bias reduction figure is mentioned in Section 3 but the baseline of persistent Democratic disadvantage is omitted.

## Three Adoption Paths: Constitutional Viability

**Path 1 — Federal statute.** The brief states that one sentence of federal statute "can make gerrymandering structurally impossible." This is accurate as a description of the statute's mechanism. However, it is incomplete as a legal claim. A federal mandate requiring states to use a specific redistricting algorithm would need to survive challenges under the Elections Clause (Art. I, §4), the Spending Clause if implemented through conditions on federal funds, and potentially Tenth Amendment anti-commandeering doctrine (*Murphy v. NCAA*, 2018). The brief presents the statutory path without any of these caveats, which is appropriate for a 5-page policy brief but risks creating unrealistic expectations.

**Path 2 — State adoption.** The claim that states "can adopt algorithmic redistricting by statute or ballot initiative" is accurate. No constitutional issue here.

**Path 3 — Court-ordered remedy.** The claim that courts can "order the parties to submit the Census data to `redist` and adopt the output" raises a due process concern that a law professor (Stephanopoulos, not me) is better positioned to evaluate. But from a political science perspective, the court remedy path depends on a prior finding of gerrymandering under state law, which varies dramatically across states. Post-*Rucho*, this path is state-by-state and not reliably available.

## Recommendation

Major revisions needed. The North Carolina result, as presented without the Georgia counterexample, is materially misleading in a document aimed at legislators. The "closely proportional" pull-quote overstatement must be corrected. The three adoption paths should note, at minimum, that each faces specific legal hurdles. These concerns require more than sentence-level edits for the partisan framing issue.
