\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The most important missing comparison in algorithmic redistricting research
is the direct placement of a deterministic algorithmic plan within a
published Markov-chain ensemble.  This paper provides that comparison for
six congressional delegation states: North Carolina ($k=14$), Wisconsin
($k=8$), Georgia ($k=14$), Pennsylvania ($k=17$), Texas ($k=38$), and
California ($k=52$).  Using published ensemble results from
\citet{herschlag2020quantifying} and \citet{deford2021recombination}, we
report the percentile position of the \ar{} plan on three axes: Polsby-Popper
compactness (PP percentile), partisan seat share, and minority-majority
district count.  The central finding: the \ar{} plan falls at the
$61$st--$72$nd percentile of compactness (more compact than average, not an
extreme outlier) and near the $50$th percentile of partisan outcomes for five
of six states.  Georgia is the notable exception, where the \ar{} plan falls
at the $38$th percentile of Democratic seats---a moderate Republican lean
driven by geography, not by algorithm design.  We argue that a plan at the
$68$th PP percentile with deterministic, verifiable provenance is legally
stronger than any ensemble plan, even one closer to the partisan median,
because verifiability eliminates one class of challenge entirely.
\end{abstract}
