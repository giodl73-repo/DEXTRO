\section{The Legal Argument}
\label{sec:legal}

\subsection{The Verifiability Advantage}

The central legal claim of the G-track is not that the \ar{} plan produces
better partisan outcomes than ensemble plans---it often produces very similar
outcomes.  The claim is that the \ar{} plan's \emph{provenance} is superior
for legal purposes.

An ensemble plan, even one at the 50th percentile of partisan outcomes,
was selected from a distribution.  Selection requires a human decision: which
plan from the 10,000 available is the ``right'' one?  This decision is
vulnerable to attack: a legislature or court that picks the 50th percentile
plan on Democratic seats could be accused of having strategically chosen a
specific Democratic seat count while hiding behind the median label.

The \ar{} plan has no such vulnerability.  Given the 2020 census data and
the seat count, there is exactly one \ar{} plan.  The selection is made by
the algorithm, not by a human.  Any challenge to the \ar{} plan must
challenge the algorithm itself, not the selection among plans.

\subsection{Combining Verifiability with Ensemble Corroboration}

The G-track evidence structure is:

\begin{keybox}
\textbf{Primary argument}: The \ar{} plan is constitutionally required by
Article~I~\S2, as the geographic completion of the Huntington-Hill
apportionment (B.11, B.02).

\textbf{Corroborating evidence (G.1)}: The \ar{} plan falls at the
$61$st--$72$nd percentile of compactness and $38$th--$61$st percentile of
partisan outcomes in published \recom{} ensembles.  It is not an outlier on
any dimension that courts have used to identify gerrymandering.
\end{keybox}

This combination is stronger than either element alone.  An ensemble finding
that a plan is at the 50th percentile of partisan outcomes does not establish
that the plan is constitutionally required.  A constitutional argument without
ensemble corroboration leaves open the question of whether the algorithm
produces extreme partisan outcomes.  Together, they close both gaps.

\subsection{The \texorpdfstring{\rucho{}}{Rucho} Framework Revisited}

\rucho{} held that no judicially manageable standard for identifying
excessive partisan gerrymandering exists at the federal level.  The majority
acknowledged that maps could be evaluated but held that courts could not
determine how much partisanship is too much.  Ensemble evidence was implicitly
acknowledged but deemed insufficient to provide a constitutional standard.

The \ar{} plan offers a different path: a plan that is not selected for
partisan reasons at all.  The court is not asked to determine whether 7D/7R
is the ``right'' outcome for North Carolina.  It is asked to certify that
the \ar{} plan was produced by a process that could not produce a different
outcome regardless of partisan preferences.

\subsection{The 70th Percentile Threshold}

We propose the following heuristic for legal purposes: an \ar{} plan at the
$p$th percentile of compactness with $p \in [60, 85]$ and at the $q$th
percentile of partisan outcomes with $q \in [30, 70]$ is legally defensible
as non-gerrymandered.  All six states in this study satisfy these conditions,
with Georgia at $q = 38$ (within the $[30, 70]$ range, though at the lower end).

This threshold is not a constitutional requirement---it is a practical
standard derived from the ensemble literature's experience with cases that
courts have found compelling.
