\subsection{North Carolina ($k = 14$)}

North Carolina is the canonical benchmark state for ensemble redistricting
research.  The state has 14 congressional districts and has been at the center
of multiple redistricting challenges.  The Herschlag ensemble uses the 2016
precinct map as the geographic unit and the 2016 presidential vote as the
partisan baseline.  Because the \ar{} plan uses census tracts rather than
precincts, we project the \ar{} district assignments onto the precinct map
for comparison.

\subsection{Wisconsin ($k = 8$)}

Wisconsin is a competitive state with strong urban-rural sorting.  The DeFord
ensemble uses the 2020 census blocks.  At $k = 8$, the plan space is smaller
than for larger states, and convergence is generally achieved faster.  The
partisan baseline is the 2020 presidential vote.

\subsection{Georgia ($k = 14$)}

Georgia has 14 congressional districts and strong geographic partisan sorting:
Democratic voters are concentrated in the Atlanta metropolitan area and the
Black Belt, while Republican voters dominate the rest of the state.  This
geographic structure constrains the partisan distribution of any valid plan.

\subsection{Pennsylvania ($k = 17$)}

Pennsylvania is notable because $k = 17$ is prime, which means the \ar{}
plan structure differs from composite-$k$ states: there is no factorization
tree, and the plan is a direct 17-way partition of the state graph.  This
makes direct comparison with prime-$k$ and composite-$k$ states informative.

\subsection{Texas ($k = 38$) and California ($k = 52$)}

For Texas and California, no published \recom{} ensemble results are available
at the census-tract level at the scale required.  For Texas, we use the
compactness bounds from \citet{autry2021multiscale} to derive approximate
percentile estimates.  For California, we provide author estimates based on
the binomial model with $p_{\rm geo}$ calibrated to the state's geographic
partisan structure; these are marked \lit{} throughout and carry wider
uncertainty than the direct comparisons for NC, WI, GA, and PA.
Using published ensemble statistics from \citet{herschlag2020quantifying}
(NC) and \citet{deford2021recombination} methodology (other states);
state-specific ensemble parameters are calibrated to published figures.
