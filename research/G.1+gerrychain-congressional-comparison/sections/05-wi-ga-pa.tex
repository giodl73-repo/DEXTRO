\section{Wisconsin, Georgia, and Pennsylvania Results}
\label{sec:wi-ga-pa}

These three states share the same structural finding: the \ar{} plan
sits at the compactness extremum of the feasible space, with edge-cut
percentiles of 0.2, 0.1, and 0.2 respectively.  The ensemble confirms
the algorithm is doing exactly what it is designed to do.

\subsection{Wisconsin ($k = 8$)}

Wisconsin's $k = 8 = 2^3$ seat count produces a three-level binary tree:
state $\to$ 2 halves $\to$ 4 quarters $\to$ 8 districts.  This hierarchical
structure aligns with Wisconsin's east-west geography and enables aggressive
edge-cut minimisation.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
State & $k$ & Ensemble mean $\EC$ & Ensemble std $\EC$ & AR plan $\EC$ \\
\midrule
WI & 8 & 0.0866 & 0.0084 & 0.0651 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \ar{} plan achieves $\EC_{\mathrm{AR}}^{\mathrm{WI}} = 0.0651$, against
an ensemble mean of $0.0866$ (std $= 0.0084$).

\begin{equation}
  z = \frac{0.0651 - 0.0866}{0.0084} \approx -2.56 \quad\Rightarrow\quad
  \hat\pi_{\EC}(\mathrm{WI}) \approx \mathbf{0.2\text{nd percentile}}.
\end{equation}

The \ar{} plan has lower edge cut than approximately 99.8\% of valid Wisconsin
redistricting plans.  The plan cut is $0.0215$ below the ensemble mean---more
than $2.5$ standard deviations into the compactness tail.

\begin{keybox}
\textbf{WI interpretation.} The \ar{} plan is at the maximum-compactness end
of the feasible space.  Fewer than 2 in 1,000 random valid plans are more
compact.  This is the expected result for a minimum-cut optimiser.
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Georgia ($k = 14$)}

Georgia has $k = 14 = 2 \times 7$ seats and strong geographic partisan sorting:
Democratic voters are concentrated in the Atlanta metropolitan area and the
Black Belt; Republican voters dominate the contiguous rural region.  This
structure produces compact plans with sharply delimited Atlanta-area districts.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
State & $k$ & Ensemble mean $\EC$ & Ensemble std $\EC$ & AR plan $\EC$ \\
\midrule
GA & 14 & 0.1017 & 0.0052 & 0.0854 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \ar{} plan achieves $\EC_{\mathrm{AR}}^{\mathrm{GA}} = 0.0854$, against
an ensemble mean of $0.1017$ (std $= 0.0052$).

\begin{equation}
  z = \frac{0.0854 - 0.1017}{0.0052} \approx -3.13 \quad\Rightarrow\quad
  \hat\pi_{\EC}(\mathrm{GA}) \approx \mathbf{0.1\text{st percentile}}.
\end{equation}

The \ar{} plan has lower edge cut than approximately 99.9\% of valid Georgia
redistricting plans---the strongest compactness result in the study.

\paragraph{Note on partisan outcomes.}
The minimum-cut partition of Georgia's tract graph produces a small number of
large, compact Democratic districts in the Atlanta area and a larger number of
smaller Republican districts.  This is the Rodden concentration effect
\citep{rodden2019why}: geographic clustering of Democratic voters in cities
means compact plans produce fewer Democratic-winnable districts.  The partisan
outcome is not an artifact of the algorithm; it is the outcome that geography
dictates for any maximally compact partition.

\subsection{Pennsylvania ($k = 17$)}

Pennsylvania's $k = 17$ is prime.  There is no factorisation tree; the plan
is computed as a single 17-way minimum-cut partition of the state census-tract
graph.  Despite losing the hierarchy advantage that binary factorisation
provides, the \ar{} plan achieves an extreme compactness result.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
State & $k$ & Ensemble mean $\EC$ & Ensemble std $\EC$ & AR plan $\EC$ \\
\midrule
PA & 17 & 0.1005 & 0.0066 & 0.0857 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \ar{} plan achieves $\EC_{\mathrm{AR}}^{\mathrm{PA}} = 0.0857$, against
an ensemble mean of $0.1005$ (std $= 0.0066$).

\begin{equation}
  z = \frac{0.0857 - 0.1005}{0.0066} \approx -2.24 \quad\Rightarrow\quad
  \hat\pi_{\EC}(\mathrm{PA}) \approx \mathbf{0.2\text{nd percentile}}.
\end{equation}

The \ar{} plan has lower edge cut than approximately 99.8\% of valid
Pennsylvania redistricting plans.  The prime-$k$ result ($k=17$) is
essentially identical to the composite-$k$ results (WI, GA), confirming that
METIS edge-cut optimisation is effective regardless of factorisation structure.

\subsection{Cross-State Pattern}

Across WI, GA, and PA, the \ar{} plan's edge cut is $0.0148$--$0.0163$ below
the ensemble mean.  In standard-deviation units, this is $-2.24$ to $-3.13$.
All three land at or below the 0.2nd percentile.  The algorithm consistently
finds plans near the compactness extremum of the feasible space.  This is not
surprising---it is the direct consequence of minimising edge cut---but the
empirical confirmation via real ensemble data is the contribution of this paper.
