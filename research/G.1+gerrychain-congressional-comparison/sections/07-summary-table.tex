\subsection{Key Observations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Compactness is consistently above median.} The \ar{} plan
    is more compact than the average valid plan in every state studied.
    This is expected: the METIS edge-cut objective directly minimises shared
    boundary (which is closely related to compactness).

  \item \textbf{Partisan outcomes are near median in 5/6 states.}
    For NC, WI, PA, TX, and CA, the \ar{} plan produces a partisan outcome
    within one seat of the ensemble median.  Only Georgia shows a notable
    deviation, at the 38th percentile of Democratic seats.

  \item \textbf{Georgia deviation is geographic, not algorithmic.}
    The 38th percentile for Georgia's Democratic seat share reflects the
    Rodden concentration effect: Democratic voters clustered in Atlanta
    cannot efficiently win more than 5 compact districts regardless of
    algorithm choice.  This is confirmed by the fact that most ensemble
    plans also produce 5 or 6 Democratic seats.

  \item \textbf{Minority representation is near median.}
    The \ar{} plan preserves minority-majority districts at rates
    consistent with the ensemble median, reflecting the geographic
    concentration of minority populations in specific areas.
\end{enumerate}
