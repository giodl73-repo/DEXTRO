\section{Texas and California Results}
\label{sec:tx-ca}

\recom{} ensembles for Texas ($k = 38$) and California ($k = 52$) are
currently running.  This section provides the structural context and a
principled placeholder.

\subsection{Expected Result and Rationale}

Based on the WI/GA/PA pattern---and the theoretical guarantee that the \ar{}
algorithm finds the minimum-edge-cut plan---both states are expected to fall
below the 5th percentile of their ensemble distributions.  The specific
prediction:

\begin{itemize}
  \item TX ($k = 38 = 2 \times 19$): the two-level factorisation (2-way bisection
    then 19-way partition of each half) should achieve aggressive edge-cut
    minimisation.  Expected: $< 5$th percentile \lit.
  \item CA ($k = 52 = 2^2 \times 13$): the three-level tree (2-way $\to$ 2-way
    $\to$ 13-way) provides multiple opportunities for hierarchical compaction.
    Expected: $< 5$th percentile \lit.
\end{itemize}

The 5th-percentile bound is conservative.  WI, GA, and PA all landed at or below
the 0.2nd percentile.  TX and CA are expected to be similar.

\subsection{Texas ($k = 38$)}

Texas is geographically large and politically sorted: Democratic voters are
concentrated in the Houston, Dallas-Fort Worth, San Antonio, and Austin
metropolitan areas; Republican voters dominate the rest of the state.  The
minimum-cut partition should produce compact metropolitan districts and large
rural districts---a structure with very low edge cut relative to the ensemble.

\subsection{California ($k = 52$)}

California's complex geography (coast, Central Valley, mountains, Southern
California basin) combined with high diversity makes it the most complex case
in the study.  The $k = 52 = 2^2 \times 13$ factorisation produces a three-level
tree.  B.11 documented that the $k = 52$ structure differs completely from a
$k = 51 = 3 \times 17$ plan; we use $k = 52$ throughout.
