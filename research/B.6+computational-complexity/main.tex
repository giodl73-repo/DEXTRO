\begin{abstract}
We analyse the computational complexity of recursive bisection for
congressional redistricting on planar census-tract graphs.
We prove that the balanced $k$-cut redistricting problem is NP-hard even
on planar graphs with unit vertex weights ($k = 2$), by reduction from
Connected Planar Graph Bisection~\citep{dyer1985partitioning}.
The hardness result concerns optimisation (finding the minimum-cut
partition); feasibility (finding any valid partition) is trivially
solvable for connected graphs.
No polynomial-time algorithm is known to achieve better than $O(\log n)$
approximation for balanced $k$-partition in general; METIS's heuristic
quality is empirically characterised across 50 states.
The algorithm runs in $O(\texttt{niter} \cdot n \log k)$ time with
$O(n \log k)$ space ($\texttt{niter} = 100$ in experiments).
Empirically, on $k > 1$ states across three census years (2000, 2010,
2020), runtime scales as $O(n^{1.07 \pm 0.03})$ --- nearly linear.
Single-district states ($k = 1$) are excluded from the scaling analysis
as they require no bisection.
For California ($n = 8{,}057$ tracts, $k = 52$ districts), peak memory
is under 3\,MB at tract resolution.
These results provide the complexity-theoretic foundation for the Districting
Integrity Act's use of METIS as the statutory partitioner: exact optimisation
is intractable, making heuristic approximation not merely acceptable but
necessary.
\end{abstract}
