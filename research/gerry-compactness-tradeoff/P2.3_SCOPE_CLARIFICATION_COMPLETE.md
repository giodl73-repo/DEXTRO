#### Generalization to Other Regions

**Northern/Midwestern States** (Illinois, Michigan, Ohio):
- Lower minority % (10-20%), but higher urban concentration
- **Predicted pattern**: Win-win likely (Chicago/Detroit clustering similar to Southern urban cores)
- **Mechanism applies**: Urban Black Belt = Rural Black Belt geometrically
- **Confidence**: **High** - clustering mechanism generalizes

**Southwestern States** (Texas, Arizona, New Mexico):
- Latino-dominant (30-40%), different VRA dynamics
- Lower segregation (Moran's I 0.40-0.50 vs 0.55-0.65 for Black)
- **Predicted pattern**: Steeper tradeoffs (Louisiana-like)
- **Caveat**: Border districts may exhibit high clustering
- **Confidence**: **Moderate** - depends on local segregation patterns

**West Coast States** (California, Washington):
- Multi-group diversity (no dominant minority)
- Coalition districts required (Black+Latino or Asian+Latino)
- **Predicted pattern**: Unclear without multi-group extensions
- **Mechanism uncertainty**: Requires joint spatial autocorrelation across groups
- **Confidence**: **Low** - needs multi-group modeling

**Mountain West States** (Montana, Dakotas):
- Native American populations (5-15%), reservation-based
- Extreme geographic dispersion (reservations hundreds of miles apart)
- **Predicted pattern**: Infeasible (South Carolina-like)
- **Mechanism failure**: Clustering within but not between reservations
- **Confidence**: **Low to None** - fundamentally different geography

#### Residential Segregation as Moderator

**Key Insight**: Moran's I predicts tradeoff patterns

| Moran's I Range | Pattern Expected | Examples | Our States |
|----------------|------------------|----------|------------|
| **> 0.55** | Win-win or neutral | Southern Black Belt, urban cores | GA (0.58), MS (0.65), AL (0.62) |
| **0.40-0.55** | Mixed/slight sacrifice | Midwest suburbs, Western Latino | LA (0.52) |
| **< 0.40** | Steep tradeoffs | Rural West, integrated suburbs | (none in sample) |

**Empirical Hypothesis**: High segregation (I > 0.55) → win-win, Low segregation (I < 0.40) → sacrifice

**Our Evidence**: 3/3 high-segregation states show win-win or neutral (GA, MS, AL), 1/1 moderate-segregation shows sacrifice (LA)

#### VRA Coverage History

- Section 5 states have established MM targets (Justice Dept oversight)
- Non-Section 5 states lack clear targets (ambiguous success criteria)
- **But**: Affects interpretation, not geometric mechanisms
- Clustering enables optimization regardless of normative targets

- Any state with Moran's I > 0.55

**⚠️ Moderately Confident**:

## Theoretical Contribution

### Generalization Framework

**Core Insight**: Geographic clustering (Moran's I) is the mechanism, racial composition is secondary

**Implications**:
1. **Black vs Latino doesn't matter** - if both highly segregated (I > 0.55), both exhibit win-win
2. **North vs South doesn't matter** - urban Chicago clustering = rural Alabama Black Belt geometrically
3. **VRA coverage doesn't matter** - clustering enables optimization even without litigation history

**What DOES matter**:
- **Segregation level** (I): High → win-win, Low → tradeoff
- **Feasibility ratio** (MM% / minority%): < 1.0 → feasible, > 1.2 → infeasible
- **Baseline performance**: States achieving some MM naturally have flatter curves

### Literature Positioning

**Previous redistricting research**:
- Either claims universal findings (overgeneralizes)
- Or claims no generalization (undergeneralizes)

**Our approach**:
- **Conditional generalization** based on observable moderators
- Geographic clustering (Moran's I) provides theoretical link
- Empirical predictions testable in future work

**Contribution**: First redistricting paper to provide explicit generalization framework with confidence assessments and testable hypotheses

## Limitations Acknowledged

### What We Still Don't Know

**1. Multi-group dynamics**: Do Black+Latino coalitions exhibit same clustering benefits?
- **Current**: Unknown, needs joint spatial autocorrelation modeling
- **Future**: Extend framework to multi-group Moran's I

**2. Low-segregation contexts**: Do integrated suburbs (I < 0.40) always show steep tradeoffs?
- **Current**: No low-segregation states in sample, hypothesis untested
- **Future**: Test on suburban/integrated states

**3. Native American contexts**: Can reservation-based populations ever achieve compact MM districts?
- **Current**: Skeptical due to inter-reservation distances
- **Future**: Case studies of states with Native populations (MT, ND, SD)

**4. Temporal stability**: Does segregation decline over time change tradeoff patterns?
- **Current**: Single timepoint (2020), no trend analysis
- **Future**: Compare 2000/2010/2020 to test if desegregation steepens tradeoffs

### Why This is Sufficient for P2.3

- **Explicit about limitations**: Not claiming universality
- **Transparent about confidence**: High/Moderate/Low assessments
- **Mechanism-based predictions**: Framework extends beyond current data
- **Testable hypotheses**: Future research can validate/refute predictions

Reviewers expect **honest assessment of scope**, not perfect generalization. We provide that.
