## Summary

I review this paper for the first time. AreaSection introduces a geographic balance constraint to redistricting bisection: each half of the state must receive approximately 50% of the land area (±10% swing). The paper's legal argument — that land-area neutrality provides an independently defensible redistricting principle that is politically neutral and publicly auditable — is among the strongest legal arguments I have seen in the algorithmic redistricting literature.

The paper makes four interconnected legal claims: (1) land area is a constitutionally neutral redistricting criterion; (2) the area constraint is verifiable from public data; (3) the area constraint's partisan consequences are empirically nil at the 34-state scale; and (4) the area constraint eliminates knife-edge districts that arise from urban peeling. All four claims are supported by the paper's analysis. The empirical demonstration that the area constraint does not systematically advantage either party is particularly important for the legal argument's credibility.

## Strengths

**S1. The "content-neutral" argument is legally precise.**
The paper correctly frames the area balance criterion as treating all land area equally regardless of who lives there — a content-neutral criterion under the First Amendment analogy that courts apply to redistricting constraints. This is exactly the kind of procedural neutrality that can survive scrutiny under Rucho's "manageable standards" framework, which implicitly required criteria that courts could verify without adjudicating partisan intent.

**S2. The -1D net finding is the right legal proof.**
The finding that AreaSection produces −1D seat net across 34 states (statistically indistinguishable from zero, no systematic direction) is the critical empirical validation of the content-neutral legal claim. If the area constraint systematically favoured either party, it would be vulnerable to challenge as a partisan mechanism dressed in geographic clothing. The null result closes this vulnerability.

**S3. The NC finding is the most legally significant result.**
The North Carolina comparison (AreaSection 6D vs. GeoSection 5D) is legally important for a specific reason: it demonstrates that the area constraint, when applied, can produce more Democratic seats than the standard algorithm — while simultaneously demonstrating that the constraint is not a partisan mechanism (since it also reduces Democratic seats in 5 other states). Courts evaluating a redistricting algorithm that uses area balance can point to North Carolina as proof that the constraint does not systematically favour any party.

**S4. The legal articulation is correctly scoped.**
The Discussion correctly frames the area balance constraint as a procedural claim: "we drew district boundaries to minimise total boundary length while giving each half of the state comparable geographic footprint." This is a claim with a clear, politically neutral rationale that does not require courts to adjudicate complex social science questions. It is the kind of criterion Rucho implicitly suggested was missing from the federal partisan gerrymandering standard.

## Minor Concerns

**C1. State-court enforceability.**
The paper argues that the area constraint is judicially manageable. But it does not specify which state constitutional provisions or statutes would provide the legal hook for enforcing an area-balance requirement. Redistricting reform has proceeded through state constitutional amendments (Ohio Issue 1, Colorado Amendments Y and Z) and through state-specific commission mandates (Arizona, California). For a redistricting practitioner reading this paper, a one-paragraph appendix identifying which state constitutions already contain geographic balance language would be a useful addition.

**C2. Competitiveness and voter preference.**
The paper correctly observes that AreaSection eliminates knife-edge districts and notes normative ambiguity. But the VRA question is also relevant: eliminating competitive districts in states with significant minority populations could reduce minority voting power if the knife-edge districts happen to be swing districts that minorities vote in. A sentence acknowledging the VRA interaction would be legally complete.
