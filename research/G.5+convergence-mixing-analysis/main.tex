\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Redistricting Markov chains such as \recom{} operate on exponentially
large combinatorial state spaces whose mixing time---the number of
steps required to approach stationarity---governs the validity of any
ensemble-based analysis.
We derive theoretical mixing time bounds for redistricting chains on
planar graphs, establishing that the spectral gap $\sgap$ satisfies
$\sgap = \Omega(1/n^2)$ in the worst case, implying $\tmix = O(n^2
\log n)$ under reversible dynamics.
For the census-tract graphs encountered in practice ($n \approx 200$--$8{,}000$),
this bound predicts $\tmix$ on the order of $10^5$--$10^8$ steps---far
beyond what is run in reported studies.
Empirically, \recom{} chains achieve $\rhat < 1.1$ in 2{,}000--20{,}000
steps for Polsby-Popper, but the gap between theory and practice reflects
the concentration of the stationary distribution near high-compactness
regions of state space.
We contrast MCMC mixing with the \cs{} algorithm (B.16), which provides
a deterministic $O(T \cdot n)$ stopping certificate.
We identify the irreplaceable role of ensemble methods---auditing enacted
maps and characterising the feasible plan space---while arguing that plan
\emph{generation} for the \dia{} statute should use \cs{} rather than
an uncertified MCMC run.
\end{abstract}
