## Summary

This advocacy paper proposes the Districting Integrity Act as a one-sentence federal statute mandating the ApportionRegions algorithm for all congressional redistricting. The legal framework is sophisticated and the constitutional derivation argument is novel and largely sound. The paper is best read as a law review article rather than an academic research paper, and on those terms it is at or near the best in its genre — detailed, practically grounded, and constitutionally specific in ways that redistricting reform proposals typically are not.

## Strengths

- **The Elections Clause foundation for federal redistricting authority is correctly stated and adequately documented.** Article I, §4's "Times, Places and Manner" clause gives Congress plenary authority over congressional redistricting, confirmed in Smiley v. Holm (1932) and Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission (2015). This is the right constitutional hook, and the paper correctly notes that a Republican federalism objection would have to run against 85 years of settled law. This is a stronger foundation than most redistricting reform proposals, which struggle with the state sovereignty question.
- **The census-stability argument as a litigation tool (Section 5.3, second paragraph) is the paper's most original legal contribution.** The argument that a map produced by the same county-anchored algorithm in 2000, 2010, and 2020 "cannot plausibly be the product of partisan intent" because the algorithm didn't know which party would benefit in any particular decade is a powerful evidentiary argument for partisan-neutrality claims. County boundaries are fixed; the algorithm is published; the convergence of three independent runs is strong circumstantial evidence of geographic rather than partisan determination. This has not been articulated previously in this form and will be influential.
- **The Gingles overlay implementation is legally precise.** The three-prong Gingles test is correctly operationalised: Prong 1 (geographic compactness) is algorithmic, Prong 2 (minority cohesion) and Prong 3 (majority bloc voting) require precinct-level election data and ecological inference, evaluated by the Election Assistance Commission. Assigning Prongs 2–3 to the EAC rather than to the algorithm is correct — these are factual determinations, not algorithmic outputs. The VRASection weight w_vra=0.40 is calibrated below the Shaw-Miller racial-predominance threshold, which is the right design choice.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The Elections Clause does not give Congress authority to mandate a specific redistricting algorithm for state-level map-drawing in the ways the paper implies without engaging with Rucho's federalism dicta.** Rucho at p. 24 explicitly notes that redistricting is "a function that has been given to state legislatures" under state constitutions. While the Elections Clause gives Congress authority over the manner of congressional elections, it has never been interpreted to give Congress authority to dictate the specific geometric algorithm used for district-drawing as opposed to substantive standards (e.g., population equality via Wesberry). The paper's constitutional argument would be strengthened by engaging with this distinction: (a) Congress can mandate substantive standards (one person one vote), (b) Congress can establish procedural requirements (map submission, verification), and (c) whether Congress can mandate a specific computational algorithm as a "manner" regulation under Article I §4 is an open question that the paper papers over. The paper must either engage with this distinction or acknowledge it as a litigation risk.
- **The proposed statute's VRA overlay creates a constitutional problem that the paper does not address.** The statute mandates VRASection (a modified bisection algorithm) for states where the EAC determines Gingles conditions are met, and simultaneously prohibits the partisan-proportionality adjustment in those states. This creates a categorical rule: states with VRA exposure run VRASection only; states without it run AR only; no state can run both. But Callais's disentanglement requirement was stated in the context of a state that used race-conscious criteria to justify what was actually partisan manipulation. It does not necessarily prohibit a state from running both modes independently and using both results in a court-supervised comparison. The paper's Callais mutex may be over-broad: it goes further than Callais requires by prohibiting even sequential comparison of VRASection and ProportionalSection results. The statutory text should be reviewed against the actual Callais holding rather than the paper's interpretation of it.
- **The GCD incompatibility fallback (Section 5.6 / statutory subsection g) has a material error.** The paper identifies Nebraska (k=3) and New Mexico (k=3) as the "most significant" prime-seat-count fallback states. But it also lists them as examples of "states where the current seat count is prime" alongside k=1 (trivial) and k=2 (simple bisection) states. Nebraska's k=3 is prime, and the fallback is a direct 3-way METIS partition. However, the paper then states the fallback "applies to at most 6 states in the current apportionment (those with k prime and k≥3)." Six states should be explicitly listed so practitioners know which states receive the fallback treatment — the paper provides only Nebraska and New Mexico. A complete list is required in the statute.

## P2 Items (Suggested Improvements)

- **The "one sentence law" branding overstates the proposition.** The operative provision is 67 words, but the full proposed statute runs from subsection (c) through (h), covering convergence criterion, county preservation, VRA overlay, variance petitions, prime fallback, and plan verification — six substantial provisions. The "one sentence law" framing is rhetorically effective but legally misleading, and will be immediately challenged by any practitioner who reads the implementation section. Better framing: "The operative principle is 67 words; the implementation requires six provisions."
- **The paper should address mid-decade court-ordered redistricting more specifically.** Section 4.2 ("Census stability does not prevent constitutional correction") states that a court finding a constitutional violation "may order a new AR run using updated data." But it doesn't address what happens to the decade-stable map in states where the violation arises not from the initial map (which was AR-compliant) but from demographic changes during the decade (e.g., a Sun Belt state whose Latino population grew enough to trigger a new Gingles obligation mid-decade). Statutory language addressing this scenario is needed.

## Score: 3 / 4 — Minor Revision

As a law review advocacy piece, this paper is among the most practically grounded redistricting reform proposals I have reviewed. The Elections Clause jurisdictional question (P1.1) is a known weakness that any serious practitioner will press, and the paper needs to engage with it rather than assuming the answer. The VRA mutex over-breadth (P1.2) is a potential constitutional problem introduced by the paper itself. The prime fallback completeness (P1.3) is a drafting error. All three are addressable in revision. The census-stability legal argument is an original contribution worth preserving and developing.
