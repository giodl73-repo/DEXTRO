## Summary

This advocacy paper proposes a federal redistricting statute mandating the ApportionRegions algorithm on the grounds that it uniquely extends the existing Huntington-Hill constitutional apportionment framework to the intrastate level. The paper's rhetorical architecture is sophisticated and the bipartisan framing is thoughtful. However, the key mathematical claims — uniqueness of the HH bisection tree, the "Constitutionally Derived Algorithm" definition, and the contiguity-irrelevance argument — each contain errors that undermine the formal propositions.

## Strengths

- **The Practitioner's Algorithm vs. Constitutionally Derived Algorithm definitions (Section 3.4) are useful conceptual tools.** Even if the formal proofs of these propositions are flawed (see P1), the conceptual framework — "an algorithm is a practitioner's choice if two algorithms produce materially different seat-count outcomes in at least one state, and neither outcome is constitutionally derived" — is a productive way to frame the algorithm-selection-as-political-choice argument. The Wisconsin bakeoff provides the empirical proof of concept for this framing.
- **The separation of seed formula concerns into three distinct problems — fixed integer (cherry-picking), arbitrary random (unpredictable), and SHA-256 content-derived (neither) — is analytically clean.** The paper correctly identifies that the cherry-picking attack is the legally salient concern (not just unpredictability), and that a content-derived formula is the appropriate solution. This is the strongest section of the paper.
- **The prime factorisation uniqueness argument ("given k, there is exactly one AR bisection tree") correctly invokes the fundamental theorem of arithmetic.** For a power-of-prime seat count, this is unambiguously correct. The caveat — "modulo the ordering of prime factors, which is resolved by taking factors in decreasing order" — correctly identifies and resolves the ambiguity for composite seat counts with multiple distinct prime factors.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The "uniqueness" claim is false for the case of repeated prime factors.** For k = 8 = 2³, the prime factorisation is {2, 2, 2} — three copies of the same prime. The "ordering in decreasing order" convention produces the same ordering regardless of how you arrange three 2s, so the tree is indeed unique for k = 8. But for k = 12 = 2² × 3, the decreasing-order convention would produce the split sequence [3, 2, 2] (first 3-way, then two 2-way). But a split sequence of [2, 2, 3] (two 2-way first, then 3-way) produces a geometrically different tree — one where the state is first bisected, then quartered, and then each quarter is split into three, versus one where the state is first trisected and then each third is bisected. These produce different geographic structures, different district boundaries, and potentially different seat outcomes. The paper must either (a) prove that the two orderings always produce the same geographic partition for connected planar graphs with geographic edge weights (which I doubt is true), or (b) specify a uniqueness-preserving convention beyond "decreasing order" that handles ties among distinct prime factors differently, or (c) acknowledge that the tree is unique only for prime-power seat counts and for seat counts with all distinct prime factors.
- **The Proposition proof ("AR is uniquely constitutionally derived") is circular.** The proof says: "AR's selection criterion is the Huntington-Hill priority rule. The HH priority rule is uniquely mandated by 2 U.S.C. §2a(a). The intrastate problem is structurally isomorphic to the interstate problem. Therefore, AR is uniquely constitutionally derived." But the isomorphism claim (Premise 4) has the gap identified in Rodden's review: the adjacency constraint changes the problem from an unconstrained set partition to a connected subgraph partition, and HH's priority rule was not designed for the constrained version. More specifically, the proof never defines what the "priority value" of a census tract is in the intrastate HH application. Without this definition, "the HH priority rule applied to census tracts" is an undefined computational procedure — the Proposition is proved about an algorithm whose definition is incomplete.
- **The "no political choices remain" claim overstates AR's determinism.** The paper states: "The prime factorization of the seat count determines the bisection tree. The TIGER adjacency graph determines the boundaries. No political choices remain." But several political choices are encoded in the statutory text itself: (a) the choice of census tracts as the redistricting unit (vs. census blocks, voting precincts, or counties), (b) the county-preservation weight α=2.0, (c) the convergence threshold T=600, and (d) the w_vra=0.40 VRA overlay weight. Each of these choices affects the resulting map and was made by the paper's author. The paper should acknowledge that "no practitioner choices remain after the statute is enacted" (which is true, conditioned on the statutory parameters), rather than claiming "no political choices remain" (which is false, since the statutory parameters are themselves political choices).

## P2 Items (Suggested Improvements)

- **The paper should engage with the "method of equal proportions" vs. "method of smallest divisors" alternative.** Huntington-Hill uses the geometric mean divisor sqrt(n(n+1)). The alternative "method of greatest divisors" (Webster/Sainte-Laguë) uses the arithmetic mean n+0.5. Both minimise a notion of fairness across parties; they produce different results for small-population states. The constitutional argument (HH was enacted and upheld) answers the question of which is correct at the interstate level, but does not address whether the same method is the right choice for the intrastate problem where the objective is different (geographic compactness, not proportional representation across states).
- **The conclusion's "The argument from first principles is, in the author's view, airtight. The reader is invited to find the flaw" is an invitation the author should take seriously.** The flaws found in this review are in the formal propositions, not in the overall rhetorical argument. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged the formal limitations of the isomorphism argument and relied more on the doctrinal extension argument (applying an established rule to a new factual context) and less on claiming mathematical necessity.

## Score: 2 / 4 — Major Revision

The paper's central formal claims — uniqueness of the HH bisection tree and the "Constitutionally Derived Algorithm" proposition — each have errors that require substantial revision. The uniqueness error for k with repeated prime factors in different combinations (P1.1) may affect a significant fraction of states. The circular Proposition proof (P1.2) cannot stand without defining the intrastate HH priority value, which requires B.11 to be complete. Until B.11 exists, the paper should rely on the doctrinal rather than the formal argument.
