## R3 Change: VRA Mutex Scope

**P1.2 — VRA overlay mutex over-breadth: RESOLVED.**

The new paragraph in Component 5 (Section 5) reads:

> "Callais p. 36 holds that race and party cannot be co-optimized in the same METIS objective: a redistricting process that combines VRASection (minority geographic alignment score) and partisan-weighted bisection in a single run is constitutionally suspect because a reviewing court cannot isolate the racial motivation from the partisan motivation. Consistent with this holding, the DIA prohibits combining VRASection and ProportionalSection in a single computational run. It does not prohibit the sequential, independent comparison of a VRASection map against a ProportionalSection map — such comparison is the Callais strong-inference test itself, and is expressly contemplated by the majority opinion as the correct analytical framework for identifying when political goals could have been achieved with better minority outcomes."

This is exactly the clarification I required. My R2 concern was that the paper's statutory language ("A State to which this subsection applies shall not simultaneously apply any partisan-proportionality adjustment") was potentially broader than Callais requires. The new paragraph correctly identifies that Callais's prohibition is against simultaneous use in a single run, not against sequential comparison. The statutory language's "simultaneously" (in subsection (e)) now has an explicit interpretation in the paper that limits it to a single-run prohibition.

This is constitutionally significant. If the DIA's mutex were interpreted to prohibit sequential comparison, it would constrain the very strong-inference analysis that Callais endorses as the remedy mechanism. The clarification prevents this over-reading.

**Structural isomorphism paragraph (Section 2, Premise 4).**
The new paragraph improves the isomorphism argument substantially. The framing — "the isomorphism is in the decision procedure (priority sequence → factorization → tree), not in the feasible set (states vs. contiguous connected subgraphs)" — is legally useful. When the DIA is challenged on the grounds that the interstate and intrastate problems are different, the paper now has a precise answer: the constitutional isomorphism is in the decision procedure (how many districts each region gets), not in the implementation (how the contiguous boundaries are drawn). This is the correct level at which to make the constitutional extension argument.

## Remaining Journal Condition

The paper still states "applies to at most 6 states in the current apportionment" without naming all six. Nebraska (k=3) and New Mexico (k=3) are named but the four remaining states are not. For a piece of proposed legislation, this is a drafting gap. Legislative counsel reviewing the DIA would require the complete enumeration. Based on the 2020 apportionment, states with prime k ≥ 3 should include Nebraska (3), New Mexico (3), and any others — the paper should enumerate them explicitly in the statutory text or a footnote.
