## Summary

This advocacy paper argues for a federal redistricting statute mandating the ApportionRegions algorithm on a Huntington-Hill bisection tree, grounded in a four-step first-principles argument extending the existing interstate HH apportionment method to the intrastate level. The legal architecture is creative and the writing is persuasive. However, the paper relies on the geographic sorting thesis in ways that systematically overstate what AR achieves for Democratic representation, and the structural isomorphism between interstate and intrastate apportionment is less complete than the paper claims.

## Strengths

- **The compactness-proportionality paradox is the paper's strongest argument and it is correctly stated.** The Wisconsin illustration — unweighted bisection gives 4D/4R, GeoSection gives 3D/5R, purely from algorithmic choice — is a genuine empirical finding that demolishes the "compactness = fairness" argument that practitioners often make. Using this to argue that algorithm selection is inherently political, and therefore that mandate of any algorithm is a political act, is logically sound. This is the right foundational argument for the DIA.
- **The bipartisan case is structured correctly.** Addressing Republican and Democratic concerns separately, and arguing that AR satisfies each party's legitimate (not opportunistic) interest, is the right rhetorical approach for a federal mandate proposal. The Republican case (no racial targeting, compact districts, county preservation by default, no locked-in advantage) is particularly well-constructed.
- **The content-derived seed formula (SHA-256 of census_release_id) addresses a genuine manipulation vulnerability.** The cherry-picking attack — a political official who has early census access chooses a seed after observing its partisan consequences — is a real litigation risk that a fixed seed cannot foreclose. Tying the seed to the census release identifier, which is publicly disclosed simultaneously to all parties, is the correct design.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The structural isomorphism argument (Premise 4) has a fatal gap: the adjacency constraint changes the problem's mathematical character, not just its feasible set.** The paper asserts: "The adjacency constraint does not change the problem's structure; it changes the feasible set." This is incorrect as a matter of combinatorial optimization. In the interstate problem, any subset of states can be grouped together — the feasible set is all partitions of 50 states into k groups. In the intrastate problem, districts must be connected subgraphs of the census-tract adjacency graph — the feasible set is all balanced k-partitions of a planar graph where each part is connected. The Huntington-Hill priority rule, which assigns the next unit to the highest-priority district, was designed for a problem where "unit" means "one state" with no adjacency constraints. Applying HH to a planar graph where district contiguity is required produces a different kind of priority computation, and the claim that the same rule applies "at the next level down" requires justification that the paper does not provide. Specifically: HH is defined by a priority value P_s(n) = pop_s / sqrt(n(n+1)) where n is the current seat count. In the intrastate problem, what is the "priority value" of a census tract in a district? The paper does not answer this; it says AR "applies the HH priority rule to census tracts" without defining what that means computationally. B.11 presumably defines it, but B.11 is listed as "planned." The isomorphism argument cannot stand on a reference to a paper that doesn't yet exist.
- **The Wisconsin AR result (4D/4R) that the paper treats as AR's constitutional vindication is being used as evidence for both the problem and the solution.** The paper says GeoSection produces 3D/5R in Wisconsin (algorithmic bias = political choice) and then says AR produces 4D/4R (constitutional derivation = proportional outcome). But this is a single-state observation. The paper's own geographic sorting thesis predicts that compact-district algorithms will systematically under-represent Democrats in states with urban concentrations. If AR happens to produce 4D/4R in Wisconsin, this may be a consequence of the specific HH factorisation (8 = 2³ → quadrant split that happens to track WI partisan geography), not a general property of AR. The paper should either: (a) show that AR produces proportional outcomes across all competitive states from B-series data, or (b) explicitly disclaim proportionality as an AR property and argue only for first-principles derivation.
- **The Democratic case's response to geographic sorting is inadequate.** The paper concedes that "compact districts, drawn without political inputs, tend to produce Republican-leaning outcomes in states with significant urban Democratic concentrations" (the Rodden-Chen result) and then says this argues for proportional representation via different legislation, not against the DIA. This is too quick. It leaves the paper with no answer to the question: if AR systematically produces Republican-leaning outcomes in California, Pennsylvania, and other large Democratic-leaning states (as the geographic sorting literature predicts it will for compact algorithms), why should Democrats support a federal mandate requiring AR? The paper needs to either show AR's outcomes in those states or develop a more complete answer to this objection.

## P2 Items (Suggested Improvements)

- **The Rodden-Chen result should be cited with greater precision.** The paper cites "Rodden 2019, Chen and Rodden 2013" but the relevant empirical finding — that compact, politically neutral districts systematically under-represent Democrats in states with concentrated urban Democratic populations — was first demonstrated for state legislative races in Chen and Rodden 2013 and extended to congressional races in my 2019 book. The exact claim the paper is making ("compact districts produce Republican-leaning outcomes") should cite the specific empirical context: it holds for single-member plurality districts in states with a specific population geography. It does not hold uniformly (Massachusetts, Maryland, and other states with dispersed Democratic populations produce Democratic over-representation under compact algorithms).
- **The "airtight" claim in the introduction ("The argument from first principles is, in the author's view, airtight") should be removed or substantially qualified.** Advocacy papers in law review format are expected to acknowledge and respond to the strongest counterarguments, not dismiss them. The airtight claim will be read as overconfidence by reviewers who know the literature and will weaken the paper's persuasive credibility.

## Score: 2 / 4 — Major Revision

The paper's core legal argument is creative and its practical implementation is well-specified. However, the structural isomorphism at the heart of the constitutional claim is not established — the paper cannot rest the DIA's constitutional derivation on a reference to an unimplemented algorithm (B.11). The Wisconsin evidence is used to simultaneously establish the problem (algorithm selection is political) and the solution (AR is constitutional), which is circular when the AR result is a single-state observation. Major revision is required to resolve these structural problems.
