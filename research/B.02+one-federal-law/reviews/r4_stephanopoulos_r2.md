## Summary

The Round 2 revision adds a substantive Elections Clause paragraph directly addressing whether Congress can mandate a specific redistricting algorithm — my primary P1 concern. The VRA mutex over-breadth (P1.2) and the prime fallback complete enumeration (P1.3) are not fully resolved. I am upgrading my score from 3 to 3 (unchanged) because the Elections Clause addition is exactly what I required but the other two items carry forward.

## P1 Resolution

**P1.1 — Elections Clause jurisdictional question: RESOLVED.**
The new paragraph in Section 4.1 directly addresses whether Congress can mandate a specific computational algorithm (not just substantive standards) under Article I §4. The argument proceeds correctly: Article I §4 authorises "make or alter Regulations in the broadest sense" per Smiley v. Holm (1932); mandating a specific redistricting algorithm is a regulation of the "manner" of holding elections in the same category as mandating ballot formats or electronic voting systems; and the algorithm mandate is at the zenith of Elections Clause authority because it is "technically precise (eliminating discretion), politically neutral (no partisan inputs), and grounded in an existing statutory mandate (2 U.S.C. §2a(a)) that Courts have already upheld."

This is the strongest available legal argument for the algorithm-mandate and it is now in the paper. I note the acknowledgement that "the litigation risk on this point is low" — this is a defensible judgment, but I would characterise it as "medium" given that no court has specifically upheld an algorithm mandate (as opposed to substantive standards). The paper's framing ("the best available response") correctly presents the argument as responsive rather than certain.

**P1.2 — VRA overlay mutex over-breadth: NOT ADDRESSED.**
The paper still prohibits VRASection and ProportionalSection from being run in the same redistricting process ("A State to which this subsection applies shall not simultaneously apply any partisan-proportionality adjustment"). My concern was that Callais's disentanglement requirement was stated in the context of combining race-conscious and partisan signals in one run, not necessarily prohibiting sequential comparison of results from two separate runs. The paper does not engage with whether its mutex is broader than what Callais requires. This is a P1 carry-forward for the journal submission — the statutory language needs to be reviewed against the actual Callais holding language rather than the paper's interpretation.

**P1.3 — Prime fallback state enumeration: NOT ADDRESSED.**
The paper still states the fallback "applies to at most 6 states in the current apportionment" without explicitly listing all six states. Nebraska (k=3) and New Mexico (k=3) are named. The four unnamed prime-seat-count states with k≥3 should be explicitly listed in the statutory text or in a footnote. For a piece of proposed legislation, this is a drafting error. The complete list should appear in the statute. (Based on the 2020 apportionment: Nebraska k=3, New Mexico k=3, and any other states with prime k≥3 should be checked against the current apportionment table.)

## Positive Assessment

The Elections Clause addition is the most important new content in this revision and is correctly argued. It converts what was a gap ("the paper papers over this distinction") into a substantive response ("the litigation risk on this point is low, and here is the best available argument").

The canonical ordering fix for k=12 is important for New Jersey litigation — NJ's 12 congressional seats make it a state where the AR tree structure will be challenged by whichever party the [3,2,2] tree disadvantages. Having the canonical ordering specified in advance, with a principled justification (largest-first = maximal early-stage geographic compactness) rather than just a tie-breaking convention, significantly reduces the litigation risk.

The B.11 companion-paper footnote correctly handles the zero-variance citation without fabricating results. This is the right approach for a paper that is outrunning its companion papers.

The Elections Clause addition resolves my primary concern. VRA mutex scope and prime fallback enumeration are carry-forward P1 items. The paper is substantially improved and is a strong candidate for law review publication after the remaining items are addressed.
