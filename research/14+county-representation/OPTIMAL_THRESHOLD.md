## Why 2.0× (Not 1.5×, Not 3.0×)?

### Analysis Results (2020)

| Multiple | Threshold | Counties | % US | CV | Avg Districts | Issue |
|----------|-----------|----------|------|-----|---------------|-------|
| 1.0× | 762K | 87 | 40% | 0.151 | 1.9 | Too many counties; many not fragmented |
| 1.5× | 1.14M | 39 | 27% | 0.122 | 3.1 | Good but less principled than 2.0× |
| **2.0×** | **1.52M** | **25** | **21%** | **0.100** | **3.7** | **Perfect: Lowest CV, guaranteed fragmentation** |
| 2.5× | 1.90M | 18 | 18% | 0.107 | 4.3 | Fewer people protected for minimal CV gain |
| 3.0× | 2.29M | 11 | 13% | 0.099 | 5.2 | Too restrictive - only major metros |

### Three Reasons for 2.0×

**1. PRINCIPLED: Guaranteed Multi-District Fragmentation**
- At 2.0× ideal, county WILL span 2+ districts under any reasonable apportionment
- Below 2.0×: County could theoretically stay unified (1-2 districts)
- Above 2.0×: More restrictive without principled justification

**Mathematical certainty**:
- County at 2.0× ideal = 2× one district → will get ≥2 districts
- County at 1.5× ideal = 1.5× one district → might get 1-2 districts (ambiguous)
- County at 2.0× ideal = clear fragmentation threshold

**2. EMPIRICAL: Lowest Inequality (CV = 0.100)**
- Best coefficient of variation among all multiples tested
- Balances coverage (21% of U.S.) with equality
- Average 3.7 districts per county (substantial fragmentation)

**3. PRACTICAL: Clean Mathematical Formula**
- Simple to write in law: "2× ideal district size"
- Automatically adjusts with population growth
- No arbitrary round numbers (like "1.5 million")
- Works for all census years with consistent principle

## Comparison: Absolute vs Relative Threshold

### Absolute Threshold (e.g., "1.5 million")

**Problems**:
- ❌ Doesn't scale with population growth
- ❌ Different counties qualify in different census years
- ❌ Requires periodic updating by Congress
- ❌ Arbitrary number with no principled justification

**Example**:
- 2000: 1.5M = 2.3× ideal (too restrictive)
- 2010: 1.5M = 2.1× ideal (about right)
- 2020: 1.5M = 2.0× ideal (about right)
- 2040: 1.5M = 1.7× ideal (too permissive)

### Relative Threshold (2.0× ideal)

**Advantages**:
- ✅ Scales automatically with U.S. population
- ✅ Consistent principle across all census years
- ✅ Self-executing (no congressional action needed)
- ✅ Principled justification (guaranteed fragmentation)

**Example**:
- 2000: 2.0× = 1.29M
- 2010: 2.0× = 1.42M
- 2020: 2.0× = 1.52M
- 2040: 2.0× = ~1.64M

**Same principle, different values = correct approach**

## Academic Justification

### The Fragmentation Principle

> "Counties that would inevitably be divided across multiple congressional districts under any proportional apportionment scheme merit autonomous representation to preserve their natural political community."

**Operationalization**: County population ≥ 2× (U.S. population / 435)

**Why 2×?**
- 1× = Could fit in single district (no fragmentation problem)
- 2× = MUST span multiple districts (fragmentation inevitable)
- 3× = Overrestrictive (many fragmented counties excluded)

**Mathematical proof**:
- If county population = 2× ideal district size
- Then county "deserves" ≥2 districts by Huntington-Hill
- Therefore county WILL BE FRAGMENTED
- QED: County needs protection from fragmentation

### Comparative Precedent

**Similar scaling formulas in U.S. law**:
1. **Electoral College**: Each state gets seats = (House + Senate)
   - Scales with House apportionment automatically
   - No fixed numbers, formula-based

2. **House apportionment**: Huntington-Hill with 435 fixed seats
   - Proportion scales with population
   - No absolute thresholds, relative allocation

3. **Federal poverty line**: Adjusted annually for inflation
   - Scales with economic conditions
   - Maintains consistent real purchasing power

**County threshold should follow same principle**: Scale with population, not fixed numbers.

## Legal Language for Constitutional Amendment

### Proposed Text

> **Section 1. County Representation**
>
> Any county whose population, as determined by the decennial census, equals or exceeds twice the ideal congressional district size shall receive autonomous congressional representation via the Huntington-Hill method of apportionment.
>
> The ideal congressional district size shall be defined as the total apportionment population of the United States divided by four hundred thirty-five.
>
> **Section 2. Calculation**
>
> For each qualifying county:
> (a) The county shall be treated as a separate entity for purposes of apportionment
> (b) The Huntington-Hill method shall allocate seats directly to the county
> (c) The county shall not be subject to state redistricting authority
>
> **Section 3. Remaining Population**
>
> The population of each state, excluding all qualifying counties, shall constitute that state's remaining apportionment population. States shall receive seats via Huntington-Hill for their remaining population and shall conduct redistricting only for non-qualifying counties within their borders.

### Key Features

1. **Self-executing**: No congressional action needed after ratification
2. **Scales automatically**: Threshold adjusts with each census
3. **Clear formula**: 2× (population / 435) = unambiguous
4. **Preserves federalism**: States retain control over non-qualifying counties

## Alternative Formulations Considered

### Rejected: 1.5× ideal (1.14M in 2020)

**Pros**:
- More counties qualify (39 vs 25)
- Higher coverage (27% vs 21%)

**Cons**:
- Less principled (why 1.5×?)
- Includes counties that might not be fragmented (1-2 districts)
- Slightly higher inequality (CV = 0.122 vs 0.100)

**Verdict**: Good but not optimal. 2.0× is more principled.

### Rejected: 3.0× ideal (2.29M in 2020)

**Pros**:
- Very low inequality (CV = 0.099)
- Only major metros qualify

**Cons**:
- Too restrictive (only 11 counties)
- Only 13% of U.S. protected
- Excludes many fragmented counties

**Verdict**: Too conservative. Leaves too many fragmented counties unprotected.

### Rejected: Absolute "1.5 million"

**Pros**:
- Round number
- Easy to understand

**Cons**:
- Doesn't scale with population
- No principled justification for 1.5M specifically
- Different relative threshold each census
- Requires congressional updates

**Verdict**: Impractical and unprincipled.
