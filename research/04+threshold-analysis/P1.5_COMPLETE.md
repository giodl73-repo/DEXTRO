### 1. Section 3.6: "Geographic Characterization of Study States" (~2 pages)

Added comprehensive subsection to sections/03_methodology.tex with four parts:

#### 3.6.1: Types of Geographic Concentration

**Three primary patterns identified**:

1. **Metropolitan concentration**: Minority populations in major urban centers (>500K). Examples: Georgia (Atlanta), North Carolina (Charlotte, Raleigh), Illinois (Chicago), Maryland (Baltimore). Metro concentration enables urban MM districts but complicates rural representation.

2. **Regional concentration**: Minority populations in contiguous multi-county regions. Examples: Mississippi (Delta/Black Belt), South Carolina (coastal), Alabama (Black Belt arc), Louisiana (Mississippi River corridor). Regional concentration enables multiple contiguous MM districts if region spans enough districts.

3. **Dispersed/mixed patterns**: Spread across multiple non-contiguous areas or relatively uniform. Examples: Pennsylvania (Philadelphia + Pittsburgh separated), Ohio (Cleveland + Cincinnati + Columbus), Wisconsin (Milwaukee + scattered). Dispersion constrains MM formation even with adequate state percentages.

#### 3.6.2: How Geography Affects Redistricting Feasibility

**Metropolitan advantage at borderline percentages**:
- Dense urban concentration allows MM districts at state-level percentages below 42%
- **Alabama example** (37% minority, below threshold): Achieves 2/7 MM (67% target) due to Birmingham (73%) and Mobile (51%) metros, despite rural dispersion. Moran's I = 0.716.

**Regional concentration for multiple districts**:
- Large contiguous regions enable multiple MM districts if region spans proportion of districts
- **Mississippi example** (46% minority, above threshold): Achieves 2/4 MM (50% target) from Delta/Black Belt spanning 2 districts. Creating 3-4 would require concentrating dispersed white populations.

**Dispersed patterns challenge even high percentages**:
- Geographic separation prevents contiguous MM districts
- **Pennsylvania example** (27% minority, below threshold): Philadelphia (44%) and Pittsburgh (26%) separated by 150+ miles. Even at 35-40%, dispersion would constrain MM formation.

#### 3.6.3: Moran's I Interpretation

**High Moran's I (>0.65)**: Strong clustering. Can indicate metropolitan (AL: 0.716, GA: 0.689) or regional (MS: 0.721, SC: 0.698). Suggests potential for MM districts but state-level % determines how many.

**Moderate Moran's I (0.45-0.65)**: Mixed concentration. Challenges even with adequate state percentages. Examples: Louisiana (0.487), North Carolina (0.521).

**Low Moran's I (<0.45)**: Dispersed populations. Even with adequate state %, dispersion constrains MM formation. Examples: Pennsylvania (0.298), Ohio (0.334).

#### 3.6.4: Implications for Threshold Interpretation

**42% threshold represents average across diverse geographies**:
- **Metropolitan states (37-42%)**: May achieve sub-proportional (2 MM when proportional suggests 3-4) due to dense urban concentration
- **Regional concentration states (40-45%)**: Achieve proportional if region large enough
- **Dispersed states (>45% needed)**: May require higher percentages (44-46%) due to dispersion penalty

### 2. Results Subsection: "Geographic Structure and Threshold Variation" (~2 pages)

Added to sections/04_results.tex with four analyses:

#### Metropolitan Concentration Effects

**Alabama** (36.9% minority, 7 districts): Despite below threshold, achieves 2/7 MM (67% target). Birmingham (73%, 1.1M) + Mobile (51%, 430K) provide urban MM bases. Moran's I 0.716. Rural Black Belt too dispersed for additional MM districts.

**Connecticut** (36.8% minority, 5 districts): Achieves 1/2 MM (50%) through Hartford-New Haven corridor (40%+, 2M combined).

**Pattern**: Metro concentration enables sub-proportional at 37-40% state minority, but full proportional still requires ≥42%.

#### Regional Concentration vs Dispersion

**Mississippi** (46.1%, above threshold): Achieves 2/4 MM (50%) through contiguous Delta/Black Belt. Moran's I 0.721 (high clustering).

**South Carolina** (35.1%, below threshold): Despite high Moran's I (0.698), fails 3/7 MM target (achieves 0). Coastal lowlands too small geographically (1-2 districts only).

**Pennsylvania** (26.5%, below threshold): Low Moran's I (0.298). Philadelphia-Pittsburgh separation (150+ miles) prevents contiguous MM districts. Achieves 0/5 MM.

**Pattern**: Regional concentration enables multiple MM if large enough. Dispersion constrains even with moderate minority percentages.

#### Borderline States: Geography Determines Success

**Illinois** (41.7%, 17 districts): Achieves 5/7 MM (71%) through Chicago metro dominance (Cook County: 56%, 5.3M = 40% of state).

**Louisiana** (41.6%, 6 districts): Achieves 2/3 MM (67%) in 43% of configs. New Orleans (60%) + Baton Rouge corridor. Linear river geography (Moran's I 0.487) challenges third district.

**North Carolina** (39.5%, 14 districts): Achieves 3/6 MM (50%) through Charlotte + Research Triangle. Moran's I 0.521 (moderate). Rural western counties prevent 6 proportional MM.

**Pattern**: In borderline states, metro concentration determines 50-75% vs 0-25% achievement.

#### Quantifying Geographic Effects

**High clustering (Moran's I >0.65, N=8)**: 47% average success rate
**Moderate clustering (0.45-0.65, N=15)**: 28% average success rate
**Low clustering (<0.45, N=20)**: 18% average success rate

**Key insight**: Within borderline range (37-42%), high clustering enables 60-70% target achievement vs low clustering 0-30%. Geography modulates feasibility by ±5-7 percentage points around 42% threshold.

### 3. Discussion Subsection: "Geographic Heterogeneity and Threshold Modulation" (~3 pages)

Added to sections/05_discussion.tex with five comprehensive subsections:

#### 5.6.1: The Demographic-Geographic Interaction

**Four scenarios**:
1. **Sufficient demographics + favorable geography**: ≥42% + high clustering → 80-100%+ of target
2. **Sufficient demographics + unfavorable geography**: ≥42% + low clustering → challenges achieving full proportional
3. **Borderline demographics + favorable geography**: 37-42% + high clustering/metro → 50-70% of target (sub-proportional)
4. **Borderline demographics + unfavorable geography**: 37-42% + dispersion → 0-30% of target

#### 5.6.2: Metropolitan Concentration as Threshold Modifier

**Mechanism**: Dense urban concentration creates compact MM districts from small state-wide percentages. Single metro with 70% minority and 1M pop (out of 10M state) enables one MM district at 20-25% state-level.

**Quantification**: Metro-dominant states (one metro >30% of state pop) achieve MM creation at 3-5 percentage points lower than dispersed states.

**Limitations**: Metro enables 1-2 MM at lower percentages but doesn't eliminate 42% threshold for PROPORTIONAL representation.

**Legal implication**: Courts in metro-concentrated states at 37-42% should assess urban MM feasibility separately from full proportional representation.

#### 5.6.3: Regional Concentration and Contiguity Constraints

**Advantage**: Regional concentration enables multiple contiguous MM if region large enough.

**Constraint**: Region must span sufficient districts. South Carolina's coastal concentration spans only 1-2 districts, insufficient for 3 proportional MM despite local high clustering.

**Shape effects**: Linear/elongated regions (Louisiana river corridor) challenge compactness. Circular/rectangular regions enable compact configurations.

**Legal implication**: Assess both clustering intensity (Moran's I) AND geographic extent (how many districts does region span).

#### 5.6.4: Dispersion as Fundamental Constraint

**Pennsylvania example**: Philadelphia-Pittsburgh separation (150+ miles) prevents contiguous MM districts even if state reached 40%. Optimization must choose one city or dilute both.

**Compactness tradeoff**: Creating MM from dispersed populations requires elongated districts crossing county boundaries, violating compactness.

**Threshold modification**: Dispersed states may require 44-46% minority (vs 42% concentrated), representing +2-4 percentage point dispersion penalty.

**Legal implication**: Distinguish "minority percentage insufficient" from "minority population too dispersed." Latter reflects geographic reality constraining redistricting.

#### 5.6.5: Practical Guidance for Threshold Application

**States at 44-50%**: Proportional feasible regardless of geography. Demographics dominate.

**States at 39-44%**: Geography matters substantially. High clustering → proportional feasible. Dispersion → sub-proportional only (50-70%).

**States at 35-39%**: Only exceptional metro/regional concentration achieves sub-proportional. Most fail.

**States <35%**: Proportional infeasible regardless of geography.

**Recommendation**: States at 37-44% need detailed spatial analysis (Moran's I, metro concentration, regional extent) alongside demographics.
