# Review 4 — Nicholas Stephanopoulos
**Paper**: F.4: Satisfying 50 Different Rule Sets — State Constitutional Redistricting Criteria and Algorithmic Adaptation
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4

## Summary

F.4 is the paper in the F track with the most direct legal relevance. As someone who has written extensively on redistricting criteria and the efficiency gap, I find the five-type taxonomy useful and the legal analysis largely accurate. My review focuses on the accuracy of state-specific characterisations and the legal defensibility of the paper's claims.

## Strengths

The taxonomy is well-designed and legally well-grounded. The distinction between constitutionally mandated criteria (which must be satisfied before any other consideration) and hortatory criteria (which are aspirational) is the right legal framework. The California priority ordering — population equality and VRA first, then compactness, community of interest, county boundaries — is correctly described and is constitutionally mandated by Proposition 11.

The Florida Amendment 6 analysis is accurate and legally precise: the prohibition on districts drawn "with the intent to favor or disfavor a political party or an incumbent" is a facial intent test, and the paper correctly notes that structural partisan blindness satisfies an intent requirement more completely than procedural compliance.

## Concerns

**C1 — California's Citizens Redistricting Commission and nesting.** The paper's introduction to F.2 (referenced in F.4) states that California's constitution requires senate districts to consist of two complete, contiguous assembly districts. As noted in my F.2 review, this is not current California constitutional law. The current CRC criteria (post-Proposition 11) do not require nesting. F.4's Table 1 correctly shows California as a Type III commission state, but if F.4 cross-references F.2's incorrect characterisation of the California nesting requirement, that error propagates here. F.4 should independently verify its California characterisation.

**C2 — Vermont and Delaware classified as Type I.** Vermont (H=150, S=30, 1 at-large congressional district) and Delaware (H=41, S=21) are classified as Type I (permissive). For Vermont, the "1 at-large district" note in the classification table is misleading: Vermont's congressional delegation has only 1 seat, so it draws a single at-large congressional district with no redistricting problem. But Vermont's state legislative districts (150 house, 30 senate) are subject to state constitutional criteria. The table conflates congressional and state legislative criteria by noting "1 at-large district" as the basis for Type I classification. For F.4, which explicitly focuses on congressional redistricting criteria, this conflation is understandable but should be flagged more clearly.

**C3 — Ohio classification as Type IV.** Table 1 places Ohio in Type IV (hybrid), noting "Amendment 1 (2015); commission since 2021." However, Ohio's redistricting commission was found by the Ohio Supreme Court to have repeatedly violated the partisan fairness requirements of Amendment 1 in the 2020-cycle congressional redistricting (the maps were struck down multiple times). Ohio's commission-based process is arguably not functioning as a Type IV hybrid — it has operated more like a partisan legislature under commission form. The paper should acknowledge the Ohio case as an instance where commission form does not guarantee substantive neutrality.

**C4 — North Carolina's "Harper doctrine."** Table 1 lists North Carolina as Type IV with "Harper doctrine; court oversight." The Harper doctrine refers to the North Carolina Supreme Court's decision in Harper v. Hall (2022) striking down the congressional map as an unconstitutional partisan gerrymander under state constitutional law. However, the subsequent Harper II (2023) reversed this holding when the court's composition changed. The paper should reflect the current state of North Carolina redistricting law, which has reverted to legislative control following Harper II.

**C5 — 50-state classification accuracy.** The paper classifies all 50 states in a landscape table. I spot-checked several: Texas (Type I, legislative, minimal criteria) — correct; Iowa (Type II, LSB-administered, mandatory compactness) — correct; Michigan (Type III, MICRC, Proposal 2) — correct; Pennsylvania (Type IV, LWV criteria, court-imposed) — correct; Florida (Type V, Amendment 6) — correct. The spot-check is favorable but a full legal review of all 50 classifications would be needed for formal publication.

## Recommendation

Accept with revisions. C4 (North Carolina Harper II reversal) and C3 (Ohio commission dysfunction) are factual errors about current law that must be corrected before publication. C1 (California nesting) should be cross-checked with F.2.
