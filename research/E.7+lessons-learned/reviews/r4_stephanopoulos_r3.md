## Response to Revision

My Round 2 score was 4 (Accept with Minor Revision). My P1 items from previous rounds were addressed in Round 2. In Round 3, the revision adds documentation for the 20% irreducibility bound via a Herschlag et al. footnote.

**20% irreducibility bound — legal implications.** From a legal perspective, the documented bound has implications for the *Rucho* framework. The bound establishes that approximately 20% of the WI sorting gap — roughly 2.3 pp — is irreducible under any contiguous equal-population partition. This means that a map challenger who shows 2.3 pp seat-vote deviation in WI-like states has not thereby shown gerrymander; the deviation could be entirely structural. The Herschlag citation is the right legal-empirical bridge here: the ensemble methodology is the dominant approach in redistricting litigation for distinguishing structural from engineered seat bias.

**Section 7.2 normative inference (P1.2 from Round 2 synthesis).** The conclusion that proportionality should not be a drawing criterion still lacks explicit normative justification. The empirical argument — that drawing cannot solve a structural problem — is compelling but does not by itself answer the normative question of whether we should try anyway and accept inferior results. I maintain this as a P2 item; it does not prevent acceptance.

**Overall assessment.** The paper's legal and normative contributions are sound. The five lessons, taken together, provide the best available algorithmic foundation for the argument that redistricting reform must be structural to be effective. The Herschlag documentation closes the last evidentiary gap.
