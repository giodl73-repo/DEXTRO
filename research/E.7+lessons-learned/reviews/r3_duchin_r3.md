My Round 2 score was 3. Three P1 items remained open: (1) Polsby-Popper sensitivity to coastal/river boundaries, (2) fractional voting mathematics, (3) conflation of sorting-based and institutional-cost lessons.

**P1.1 — PP sensitivity to coastal/river boundaries (not addressed).** Still open. The paper uses PP throughout without acknowledging the coastal/river boundary confound. For an Annual Review synthesis paper, this is more concerning than in an experimental paper: the synthesis is generalizing across all 50 states, and the compactness-proportionality exchange rate presented in Lesson 5 (0.015 PP/pp) is computed from a national average that includes FL, LA, and AK — states where PP systematically understates compactness. I would still prefer a robustness note.

**P1.2 — Fractional voting mathematics (not addressed).** Lesson 3 still describes fractional voting without engaging with Banzhaf power indices or supermajority implications. Annual Review readers in political theory will notice this gap.

**P1.3 — Sorting-based vs. institutional-cost lessons (not addressed).** The synthesis section (Section 7.1) still conflates the two logical categories.

**20% irreducibility bound (not my item, but relevant to my geometric expertise).** The Herschlag et al. footnote is an improvement. The NC ensemble lower bound is the correct methodological reference. The WI/NC scaling is defensible as an approximation. My technical concern is that the NC ensemble was computed for a specific year and redistricting cycle; scaling it to WI by "sorting ratio" is not a standard methodology and the phrase "sorting ratio" is undefined in the text. The citation gives the bound a literature anchor, which is the most important improvement.
