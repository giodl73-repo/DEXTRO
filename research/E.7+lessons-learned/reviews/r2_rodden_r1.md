## Summary

E.7 is the capstone synthesis of the E-track, organised around five design lessons derived from algorithmic comparison of six alternative representation systems. For a geographer of political representation, this is the most satisfying paper in the E-track because it takes the Rodden sorting mechanism seriously as the central analytical object rather than treating it as a caveat. Lessons 1 and 2 --- that sorting is structural (not a drawing artifact) and that multi-member districts partially but not fully escape it --- are the correct conclusions from the E.4 and E.1 evidence respectively, and the E.4 null result (partisan-similarity clustering doesn't change aggregate seat shares) is the cleanest demonstration of the structural nature of sorting I am aware of in the literature. The paper is well-written and appropriately targeted at the Annual Review of Political Science audience. However, it overstates the irreversibility of the sorting constraint and understates the geographic heterogeneity in how the constraint operates across states.

## Strengths

- **The E.4 null result is the most important evidence in the paper.** Lesson 1's demonstration that partisan-similarity clustering produces 95% safe seats but an essentially unchanged aggregate seat--vote relationship ($+4.0$ pp vs. $+4.2$ pp Republican seat bonus) is the strongest available evidence that the sorting gap is a structural property of voter geography, not of drawing methodology. This result deserves more prominence --- it should be in the abstract.
- **The concavity finding for multi-member district size (Section 3.4) is a policy-relevant quantitative result.** The observation that going from 1 to 3 members yields 40% gap reduction, 3 to 5 yields an additional 24%, and 5 to 7 yields approximately 12% establishes a diminishing returns pattern that directly addresses the policy question of what district size to adopt. The finding that the Fair Representation Act's 3--5 member specification is "near the knee of the marginal-benefit curve" is exactly the kind of translation from algorithm to policy that political scientists need.
- **The synthesis section's distinction between three design tensions (compactness/proportionality, community preservation/compactness, structural reform/drawing optimisation) is a conceptual contribution.** The third tension --- that drawing-process reform addresses a different problem than structural reform --- is the most important finding of the entire E-track and is articulated clearly and precisely here. The observation that "the bulk of litigation, legislative effort, and research attention has focused on drawing-process reform" while "structural reform that would actually change the seat--vote relationship has received far less policy attention" is both empirically accurate and politically important.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The geographic heterogeneity in the sorting gap is underemphasised and the 4.2 pp national figure is misleadingly uniform.** The paper reports the sorting-driven Republican seat bonus as "$+4.2$ pp Republican (baseline)" as if this were a uniform national number. But the sorting gap is highly heterogeneous across states: states like Iowa, Wisconsin, and Arizona have modest sorting due to more evenly distributed partisanship, while states like Illinois, New York, Massachusetts, and Maryland have extreme sorting due to dense Democratic urban cores. The paper should decompose the national 4.2 pp figure by state or by geography type (high-sorting vs. low-sorting states), because the policy implications differ dramatically. In low-sorting states, multi-member districts provide little additional benefit; in high-sorting states, they provide more but at greater compactness cost. The headline figure implies a uniform constraint when the real constraint is highly variable.
- **Lesson 3 (county representation) understates the proportionality improvement in competitive states.** Section 4.3 reports that county representation produces seat--vote deviations of 1--2% versus 3--5% for algorithmic single-member redistricting in competitive states. This is a meaningful improvement that is described as "at least as proportional" and "often more so" --- but the improvement is not emphasised. Given that the paper's Lesson 5 argues the Pareto frontier is steep (0.015 PP per pp), a 2--3 pp improvement in proportionality in competitive states without any compactness cost (since county representation doesn't draw districts at all) is a significant exception to the frontier steepness claim. The paper should note this: county representation partially escapes the compactness--proportionality frontier in competitive states because it does not involve geometric optimisation at all.
- **The paper needs to acknowledge the causal identification limitations of the simulation evidence.** Sections 2.3 (multi-member districts reduce the sorting gap by 40/65%) and 6.3 (the +18% minority representation benefit) are based on simulations across all 50 states. But the simulations hold voter geography constant --- they don't account for the possibility that voter geography itself might change under a different electoral system (for example, residential sorting patterns might change if multi-member districts eliminated the incentive for partisan residential clustering). A paper targeting the Annual Review of Political Science audience should acknowledge this endogeneity limitation and cite the relevant empirical work (Rodden, Chen, or otherwise) on residential sorting as an equilibrium phenomenon.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **Add the E.4 null result to the abstract.** The current abstract mentions the five lessons but emphasises the quantitative results (40%, 65%, 12%). The E.4 null result --- that partisan-similarity clustering doesn't change aggregate seat shares --- is the paper's most compelling piece of evidence and should be in the abstract.
- **The "captive Democratic votes" calculation (20% of sorting as irreducible) should be extended to a policy implication.** If 20% of the sorting gap is irreducible by any feasible district-size mechanism, what does this mean for the Fair Representation Act's specific proportionality claims? Connecting the irreducibility bound to specific legislative claims would make the paper more policy-relevant.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The E.4 null result and the design tension synthesis are the paper's strongest contributions. The three P1 items address real analytical gaps: the misleadingly uniform sorting gap representation, the underemphasised county representation exception to the Pareto frontier, and the unacknowledged endogeneity limitation. The first requires disaggregating a key statistic; the second requires adding a paragraph acknowledging an important exception; the third requires a methodological caveat.
