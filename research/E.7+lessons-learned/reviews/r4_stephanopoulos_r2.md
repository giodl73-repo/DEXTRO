## Response to Revision

Round 1 raised three P1 items: (1) the incomplete constitutional analysis of county representation conflating two Wesberry requirements, (2) the normative inference from an empirical premise in the constraint hierarchy, and (3) the inconsistency between stated neutrality and normative conclusion on multi-member districts.

**P1.1 — County representation constitutional analysis (not addressed in this revision).** Lesson 3's constitutional discussion still conflates the two Wesberry requirements — equal-population districts within a state and each person's vote being worth as much as any other. The fractional voting mechanism addresses the second but not the first, and the distinction from at-large congressional elections is not drawn. Not addressed.

**P1.2 — Normative inference from empirical premise (not addressed in this revision).** Section 7.2's conclusion that adding proportionality "at any level of the hierarchy is inappropriate" still rests on the empirical observation that drawing instruments cannot fully solve the structural problem — without a normative justification for why partial reduction at compactness cost is worse than no reduction. The inference remains invalid as stated. Not addressed.

**P1.3 — Stated neutrality vs. normative conclusion (partially addressed).** The revised Section 6 now explicitly distinguishes within-system from cross-system exchange rates and justifies the 0.015 figure as the appropriate headline for this paper. More importantly, the exchange rate clarification contributes to the paper's overall intellectual coherence, which partially addresses the neutrality/normative-conclusion tension by grounding claims more precisely. The paper's conclusion still says "we do not advocate" while the synthesis section argues multi-member districts are "the only mechanism" for simultaneous proportionality and minority representation improvement — this tension persists. Partially addressed.

## Assessment of What Was Done

The targeted revisions address Karypis's and Rodden's concerns precisely and are well executed. The within-system vs. cross-system distinction for exchange rates is a conceptual improvement that also modestly addresses the normative precision concern — the paper now uses numbers more carefully, which reduces the risk of overclaiming. The paper's main analytical claims survive the unresolved issues.
