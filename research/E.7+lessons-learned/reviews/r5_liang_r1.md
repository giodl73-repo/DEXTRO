## Summary

E.7 is a capstone synthesis paper reporting five design lessons from the E-track algorithmic comparison of representation systems. From a statistical and machine learning perspective, the paper's methodological contribution --- using algorithmic counterfactual implementation to enable controlled causal inference about institutional design --- is genuinely important and analogous to ablation studies in ML. The paper is well-structured and the five lessons are empirically grounded. However, the paper has significant statistical precision issues that affect the reliability of several key claims. The exchange rate inconsistency (0.015 vs. 0.019) suggests internal data inconsistency, several key statistics are reported without uncertainty characterisation, and the concavity finding (diminishing returns from increasing district size) is based on too few data points to be reliably estimated.

## Strengths

- **The algorithmic counterfactual method is correctly analogised to controlled experiments.** The introduction's argument that holding the algorithm constant and varying design dimensions one at a time enables cleaner causal inference than comparative political science field research is methodologically correct and well-stated. This is the exact logic of ablation studies in ML --- removing one component at a time to isolate its contribution --- and applying it to institutional design is a genuine methodological innovation.
- **The E.4 null result (partisan-similarity clustering does not change aggregate seat shares) provides a controlled natural experiment.** The finding that varying the edge-weighting scheme from pure compactness to pure partisan homogeneity changes the distribution of margins (95% safe seats) but not the aggregate seat shares (+4.0 pp vs. +4.2 pp Republican seat bonus) is a clean null result that is precisely the kind of finding that algorithmic counterfactual analysis can produce and field research cannot. This result is presented clearly and interpreted correctly.
- **The five-lesson structure provides a clear empirical architecture.** Each lesson is anchored in specific E-track papers, specific quantitative findings, and a specific mechanism explanation. The structure allows readers to assess each lesson independently and to trace claims back to primary evidence. This is excellent scientific practice for a synthesis paper.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The exchange rate inconsistency between 0.019 (E.5-derived) and 0.015 (E.0 cross-system average) is a data consistency problem.** Section 6.1 derives an exchange rate of $0.073 / 3.9 = 0.019$ from the E.5 parametric results and then immediately switches to using 0.015 as "the E.0 synthesis estimate when averaged across multi-member and pure-proportionality systems." These two numbers are being used interchangeably throughout the paper as the Pareto frontier slope, but they are derived from different data and by different methods. The paper should (a) specify which estimate is being cited in each use, (b) explain why two estimates exist and how they are derived, and (c) test whether the 0.004 difference is statistically significant or within estimation uncertainty. If the two estimates are measuring different things (within-system vs. between-system trade-offs), the paper should maintain the distinction throughout rather than treating 0.015 as the canonical figure.
- **The concavity of district-size proportionality improvement is claimed from three data points (1, 3, 5 members, yielding 0%, 40%, 65% gap reduction).** Section 3.4 states that "the relationship between district size and proportionality improvement is concave" based on these three observations. Three data points are insufficient to distinguish concavity from linearity or convexity with any statistical confidence. The paper should either (a) fit a functional form (log, power, or polynomial) to the three points and report goodness of fit and the implied functional shape, acknowledging the very low degrees of freedom; or (b) qualify the claim as "the three available data points are consistent with a concave relationship" rather than stating the relationship "is concave" as a fact.
- **The 20% "irreducibility bound" for the sorting gap is an unvalidated calculation.** Section 3.3 states: "The theoretical maximum reduction in the sorting gap from multi-member districting... is approximately 80%... derived from the fraction of Democratic votes that are in census tracts with >70% Democratic composition --- the 'captive' Democratic votes." This calculation is presented without documentation: (a) what is the fraction of Democratic votes in >70% tracts? (b) how does this fraction translate to the 20% irreducibility bound? (c) is the 70% threshold motivated by theory or is it arbitrary? The calculation needs to be documented as a derivation or provided in the E.1 paper with a specific citation to the relevant section.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **Provide a summary table for the five lessons.** A table reporting each lesson's title, key statistic, evidence source (E.x), and mechanism would make the paper more efficiently citable. The current narrative structure requires readers to extract key statistics from five different sections.
- **The paper should report the spatial distribution of the sorting gap across states, not just the national average.** The national 4.2 pp sorting gap is used throughout as the baseline. But the sorting gap is highly heterogeneous across states --- E.7 mentions this briefly in Lesson 1's discussion of state-by-state variation in the cost of proportionality (Section 2.3), but does not report the distribution. A histogram or table of state-level sorting gaps would reveal whether the lessons apply uniformly or mainly to high-sorting states.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The algorithmic counterfactual method and the five-lesson structure are genuine contributions. The three P1 items address real statistical precision issues: the exchange rate inconsistency, the insufficient data points for the concavity claim, and the undocumented irreducibility calculation. The first requires reconciliation between the two estimates; the second requires a statistical qualification; the third requires a documented derivation.
