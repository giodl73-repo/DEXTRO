## Summary

E.7 synthesises five design lessons from the E-track experiments, targeting the Annual Review of Political Science audience. From a computational perspective, the paper's strongest sections are Lesson 5 (the Pareto frontier steepness) and the synthesis (the optimal constraint hierarchy for single-member systems). The exchange rate derivation (0.015--0.019 PP per pp of proportionality) from E.5's parametric optimisation is the most quantitatively precise result in the E-track, and the paper's identification of the optimal constraint hierarchy (population first, contiguity second, compactness third) as a logical rather than merely conventional ordering is a genuine insight. However, the paper has two technical weaknesses: the exchange rate derivation in Section 6.1 produces an inconsistency between the E.5-derived value (0.019) and the E.0 value (0.015), and the claim that the Pareto frontier is "not an artifact of the specific algorithm" needs stronger methodological support.

## Strengths

- **The exchange rate derivation (Section 6.1) is the clearest quantitative specification of the compactness--proportionality trade-off in the redistricting literature.** The parametric calculation from E.5 --- moving from PP = 0.367 at weight 0 to PP = 0.294 at weight 1, against a proportionality improvement of 3.9 pp, gives $\Delta PP / \Delta deviation = 0.073 / 3.9 = 0.019$ --- is a direct derivation from observable data. Publishing this calculation explicitly is more useful than the summary claim (0.015 per pp) because it allows readers to verify and criticise the derivation.
- **The "thought experiment" for the random redistricting baseline (Section 6.3) is a useful pedagogical device.** Showing that a perfectly random partition (subject only to population equality and contiguity) would produce mean PP near 0.15--0.20 and near-zero proportionality deviation clarifies that compactness and proportionality are not intrinsically in tension --- they are both improvements over the random baseline. The trade-off exists only because improving compactness beyond the random baseline (from 0.15 to 0.367) and improving proportionality beyond the random baseline (from ~0 to near-zero deviation) require different types of geographic alignment.
- **The optimal constraint hierarchy argument (Section 7.2) elevates the paper beyond empirical description.** The argument that population equality first, contiguity second, compactness third is not merely conventional but logically ordered --- each constraint is a prerequisite for the next one to be meaningful --- is a genuine conceptual contribution. The paper correctly notes that adding proportionality as a criterion "at any level of the hierarchy is inappropriate" because it confuses a drawing instrument with a structural problem.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The paper reports two different exchange rates (0.015 and 0.019) without resolving the discrepancy.** Section 6.1 derives 0.019 from the E.5 parametric optimisation. Section 6.1 then reports 0.015 as the "E.0 synthesis estimate when averaged across multi-member and pure-proportionality systems" and uses the "lower 0.015 figure as a conservative estimate." But the paper does not explain why averaging across multi-member systems (which have a different mechanism than the proportionality-weight-varying algorithm in E.5) produces a meaningful exchange rate, or why the average of E.5-derived (0.019) and multi-member system-derived exchange rates yields 0.015. The two estimates need to be reconciled: either the paper should report one exchange rate with a stated derivation, or it should explicitly characterise the two as measuring different things (E.5 measures within-system trade-offs; the cross-system average measures between-system trade-offs).
- **The claim that the Pareto frontier "is not an artifact of the specific algorithm" needs stronger support.** Section 6.3 argues that the steep Pareto frontier is "not an artifact of the specific algorithm or the specific data" but "a consequence of geographic sorting." This is plausible but not demonstrated. The argument requires showing that a different algorithm that also minimises edge cuts (e.g., spectral bisection, flow-based partitioning) would produce a similarly steep exchange rate. Without this comparison, the claim is based on the intuition that geographic sorting is the driving force, not on algorithmic comparison. The paper should either (a) cite evidence from other algorithms (even from the comparative literature) showing similar exchange rates, or (b) qualify the claim as "consistent with the sorting hypothesis" rather than "not an artifact of the algorithm."
- **The residual 20% of sorting that is "irreducible" (Section 3.3) is stated without a derivation of the 20% figure.** Section 3.3 states that "the theoretical maximum reduction in the sorting gap from multi-member districting, holding state boundaries constant and allowing unlimited district size, is approximately 80%." The remaining 20% is described as "irreducible" because it corresponds to "captive Democratic votes in census tracts with >70% Democratic composition." The 20% figure is derived from "the fraction of Democratic votes that are in census tracts with >70% Democratic composition." This is a specific empirical calculation that should be documented: how many Democratic votes nationally are in >70% Democratic tracts, and how does this translate to the 20% of the seat gap? The paper should provide the calculation or a reference to where it is computed in E.1.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **The paper would benefit from a quantitative summary table analogous to E.0's Pareto table.** The five lessons are presented narratively; a summary table showing each lesson's key statistic, the evidence source (E.x paper), and the key quantitative parameter (sorting gap reduction, exchange rate, etc.) would make the paper more efficiently citable and would confirm that the lessons are empirically grounded.
- **The concavity of the size--proportionality relationship (Section 3.4) should be tested more rigorously.** The paper asserts that the relationship between district size and proportionality improvement is concave (diminishing returns), based on three data points (1, 3, 5 members). With three data points, concavity cannot be distinguished from a wide range of functional forms. The paper should acknowledge this limitation and avoid claiming "the marginal gain diminishes" as if it were a statistical finding rather than a pattern in three observations.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The constraint hierarchy argument and the exchange rate derivation are the paper's strongest contributions. The three P1 items address real technical gaps: the unexplained exchange rate discrepancy, the unsupported algorithm-independence claim, and the undocumented 20% irreducibility bound. The first requires a methodological clarification; the second requires either comparative evidence or a qualification of the claim; the third requires a documented calculation.
