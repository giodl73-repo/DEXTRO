## Summary

E.7 synthesises five design lessons from the E-track experiments, targeting the Annual Review of Political Science. From a constitutional law and political science perspective, this paper engages the right questions: what structural alternatives to single-member districts are constitutionally available, what are their costs and benefits, and how should they be evaluated? The paper's strongest sections are the synthesis (Section 7) and Lesson 5 (the steep Pareto frontier), both of which advance beyond descriptive findings to genuine normative argument. However, the paper makes significant constitutional claims in Lesson 3 (about county representation's constitutional obstacles) and the synthesis (about the optimal constraint hierarchy) that require more careful legal analysis, and it draws normative conclusions from empirical findings in ways that need more explicit justification.

## Strengths

- **The design tension framework (Section 7.1) is a genuine conceptual contribution to the redistricting literature.** Identifying three tensions (compactness/proportionality, community preservation/compactness, structural reform/drawing optimisation) and distinguishing between the first two (geometric, operating within the Pareto frontier) and the third (between-level, where drawing optimisation cannot solve a structural problem) is a sophisticated analytical framework. The third tension --- that litigation over drawing process cannot fix the seat--vote gap caused by sorting --- is both empirically supported and normatively important for how we think about redistricting reform.
- **The paper's coda ("Value of Algorithmic Counterfactuals") identifies a methodological contribution to comparative institutional analysis.** Noting that algorithmic implementation of constitutionally unavailable systems (national redistricting, strict county representation) generates quantitative evidence about the costs of constitutional constraints --- evidence that manual redistricting cannot produce --- is an important methodological observation. This observation has implications beyond redistricting: it applies to any context where constitutional constraints prevent the implementation of a reform that can nonetheless be evaluated computationally.
- **The constraint hierarchy argument (Section 7.2) provides principled support for the current redistributing priority ordering.** The argument that population equality, contiguity, and compactness are not merely conventional priorities but logically ordered (each is a prerequisite for the next to be meaningful) is a genuine intellectual contribution. The argument that adding proportionality as a constraint "is inappropriate" because it substitutes a structural problem for a drawing problem is the correct normative conclusion from the E-track evidence, and it is stated clearly.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The constitutional analysis of county representation (Lesson 3) conflates two separate Wesberry requirements.** Section 4.4 states that Wesberry v. Sanders requires congressional districts to be "as nearly equal in population as practicable" and uses this to argue that county populations' 20,000-fold variation makes county representation constitutionally unavailable. This is correct but incomplete. Wesberry has two separate implications: (a) districts within a state must be nearly equal in population, and (b) each person's vote must be "worth as much" as any other person's vote. The fractional voting mechanism addresses (b) by giving small counties proportionally weighted votes, but it does not address (a) because there are no districts within which equality is required. The paper should also engage with the lower-court precedent on at-large congressional elections (which have been upheld in some small states) and distinguish county representation from at-large elections.
- **The normative claim that proportionality should not be a drawing criterion is stated as an empirical conclusion but requires normative justification.** Section 7.2 concludes that "adding proportionality as a criterion at any level of the hierarchy is inappropriate" because "it attempts to solve the structural problem through a drawing instrument." This is an empirical observation (drawing instruments cannot fully solve the structural problem) elevated to a normative conclusion (therefore drawing instruments should not try). But the inference is invalid: even if drawing instruments cannot solve the structural problem fully, they might partially reduce it --- and even a 1--2 pp reduction in the sorting gap through a proportionality-weighted drawing criterion might be worth the compactness cost in some states. The paper needs to argue why "partial reduction at compactness cost" is worse than "no reduction at compactness savings," which requires a normative premise about the relative value of compactness and proportionality.
- **The paper's conclusion about multi-member districts is stated as normatively neutral but implicitly normative.** The conclusion states: "We do not advocate for multi-member districts as a policy position. We observe that the E-track research program, taken as a whole, provides a stronger empirical foundation for the structural case for multi-member districts than previously existed." But the immediately preceding synthesis section argues that multi-member districts are "the only mechanism within a geographic representation system that can materially improve both proportionality and minority representation." This is not neutral description --- it is an argument for multi-member districts conditional on valuing proportionality and minority representation. The paper should either (a) acknowledge that the conclusion is conditional on normative premises about the value of proportionality (and defend those premises), or (b) present the conditional structure explicitly: "IF proportionality is a legitimate goal, THEN multi-member districts are the structural reform most supported by the evidence."

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **The paper should address the interaction between multi-member districts and VRA Section 2 requirements.** The VRA analysis in Lesson 2 (multi-member districts improve minority representation by 18%) is presented separately from the constitutional analysis. But the interaction is important: if multi-member districts produce minority representation through proportional allocation rather than geographic concentration (the Gingles framework), they may satisfy the substantive VRA goal while technically avoiding the Gingles precondition requirements. This distinction has significant legal implications that the paper should acknowledge.
- **Consider engaging with the partisan symmetry measure (seats-votes curves) alongside or instead of the seat--vote deviation measure.** The paper uses "seat--vote deviation" as the primary proportionality measure throughout, but the standard tool in the political science literature for measuring partisan asymmetry is the seats-votes curve (or the partisan bias at 50% vote share). Translating the 4.2 pp deviation into a partisan bias at 50% figure would make the paper more directly comparable to prior work.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The design tension framework and the constraint hierarchy argument are the paper's strongest contributions. The three P1 items address real analytical gaps: the incomplete constitutional analysis of county representation, the normative inference from an empirical premise, and the inconsistency between stated neutrality and normative conclusion. These require conceptual revisions rather than empirical additions.
