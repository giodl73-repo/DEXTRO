## Response to Revision

Round 1 raised three P1 items: (1) Polsby-Popper used without acknowledging its sensitivity to coastal and river boundaries, (2) Lesson 3's inadequate treatment of fractional voting's mathematical structure, and (3) the conflation of sorting-based lessons (1, 2) and institutional-cost lessons (3, 4) in the synthesis.

**P1.1 — PP sensitivity to coastal/river boundaries (not addressed).** The paper still uses Polsby-Popper as the universal compactness measure without any acknowledgment of its sensitivity to perimeter length in states with complex coastal and river boundaries. This matters for Lessons 1 and 5 because the compactness-proportionality exchange rate may differ between states with simple inland boundaries and states with complex coastal ones, and the sorting gap itself may be confounded by geographic complexity. Not addressed.

**P1.2 — Fractional voting mathematics (not addressed).** Lesson 3 still describes fractional voting as "institutionally unfamiliar" without engaging with the power-index implications for weighted voting bodies (Banzhaf, Shapley-Shubik), coalition formation, or supermajority thresholds. An Annual Review paper should engage with this political theory literature. Not addressed.

**P1.3 — Sorting-based vs. institutional-cost lessons (not addressed).** The synthesis section (Section 7.1) still presents all five lessons as symmetric inputs to the design tension analysis. Lessons 1 and 2 are about the sorting mechanism (causal claims about geographic concentration). Lessons 3 and 4 are about institutional costs (empirical costs of specific choices). The synthesis does not distinguish these two logical categories. Not addressed.
