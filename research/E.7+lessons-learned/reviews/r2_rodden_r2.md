## Response to Revision

Round 1 raised three P1 items: (1) the 4.2 pp national sorting gap presented as uniform when it is highly heterogeneous, (2) the underemphasised county representation exception to the Pareto frontier, and (3) the unacknowledged causal identification limitations of holding voter geography constant.

**P1.1 — Sorting gap heterogeneity (addressed, substantially improved).** The empirical results paragraph in Section 3 (Lesson 2) now introduces the baseline as "the median sorting gap of +4.2 pp Republican (range: 1.1 pp in ND to 11.3 pp in WI; baseline)" and adds: "The gap reduction is smallest in low-sorting states (IA, ND) and largest in high-sorting states (IL, NY, PA, MI)." This is the disaggregation I requested. The state range (1.1 pp to 11.3 pp) quantifies the heterogeneity clearly and the identification of high-sorting vs. low-sorting states by name makes the policy implications concrete. The synthesis section (Section 7) retains 0.015 PP/pp as the cross-system exchange rate while the empirical section now appropriately contextualises the state-level variation. Satisfied.

**P1.2 — County representation exception (not addressed in this revision).** Lesson 3 (Section 4) still does not explicitly note that county representation partially escapes the compactness-proportionality frontier because it involves no geometric optimisation. The paper continues to describe county representation's proportionality improvement in competitive states as a factual finding without connecting it to the Pareto frontier claim in Lesson 5. This remains a real analytical gap — a system that achieves proportionality improvement without compactness cost is an important exception to the steepness claim, and the paper should acknowledge it.

**P1.3 — Voter geography endogeneity (not addressed in this revision).** The paper does not acknowledge that all simulations hold voter geography constant and that residential sorting patterns may change under different electoral systems. This is a methodological caveat that the Annual Review of Political Science audience will expect. Not addressed.

## Assessment of What Was Done

The sorting gap heterogeneity revision directly addresses my most important concern. The range (1.1 pp to 11.3 pp) and the named high-sorting/low-sorting states are exactly the contextualisation I asked for. This is a significant improvement to the empirical presentation of Lesson 2.

## Score: 4 — Accept with Minor Revision

The sorting gap heterogeneity fix is the most important revision for my concerns, and it is executed well. The county representation exception and voter geography endogeneity remain open but are second-order issues that do not undermine the paper's main findings. I raise my score from 3 to 4 on the strength of the disaggregation improvement.
