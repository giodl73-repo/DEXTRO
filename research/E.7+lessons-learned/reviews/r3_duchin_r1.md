## Summary

E.7 is a design lessons paper that synthesises findings from six alternative representation system experiments. As a paper targeting the Annual Review of Political Science audience, it is appropriately synthetic: it extracts general principles rather than reporting primary results, and the five lessons are well-chosen to cover the major dimensions of the design space. From a geometry and measurement perspective, the paper is at its strongest in Lesson 5 (the Pareto frontier) and the synthesis section's constraint hierarchy argument. It is at its weakest in Lesson 3 (county representation), where the constitutional and mathematical properties of fractional voting receive less careful treatment than the other lessons. The paper also has a systematic omission: it treats compactness as measured by Polsby-Popper throughout without acknowledging that PP has well-known pathologies that may affect the lessons.

## Strengths

- **The design-space framing (borrowed from engineering design theory, Section 1.1) is appropriate and productive.** Introducing the "design space" and "Pareto frontier" concepts from Simon's engineering design theory locates the redistricting problem correctly: it is a design problem with multiple competing objectives, not an optimisation problem with a single objective. This framing is more intellectually honest than papers that treat compactness as the primary goal and treat everything else as a constraint.
- **The "thought experiment" for the random baseline (Section 6.3) clarifies the nature of the compactness--proportionality trade-off.** Pointing out that a random partition would produce mean PP near 0.15--0.20 and near-zero proportionality deviation establishes that the DIA is not trading off two independent objectives but is recovering two different types of structure from the randomness. This is a subtle and important point about the geometry of the problem.
- **The synthesis section's three design tensions (Section 7.1) advance the paper beyond a summary of findings.** The three tensions --- compactness/proportionality, community preservation/compactness, drawing optimisation/structural reform --- identify the fundamental design constraints at different levels of the system: geometric, institutional, and political. Quantifying the costs of each tension (0.015 PP per pp for the first; 12% PP for state boundaries, 18% PP for 85% county preservation for the second; and the E.4/E.5 evidence for the third) makes the paper rigorously empirical rather than merely descriptive.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **Polsby-Popper is used throughout without acknowledging its sensitivity to coastal and river boundaries.** The PP score is systematically lower for states with complex coastal and river boundaries (Florida, Louisiana, Alaska) than for inland states with simple boundaries, because PP is sensitive to perimeter length and coastal boundaries have high perimeter-to-area ratios. The paper uses PP as the universal compactness measure without noting this geographic confound. This matters for the lessons because: (a) the sorting-compactness connection in Lesson 1 may be confounded by the fact that high-sorting urban states often have complex coastal boundaries; (b) the exchange rate in Lesson 5 may differ between states with simple boundaries and states with complex ones. The paper should either supplement PP with a second metric (Reock, convex hull ratio) that is less sensitive to boundary complexity, or explicitly acknowledge this limitation.
- **Lesson 3 (county representation) does not adequately address the mathematical implications of fractional voting in Congressional procedure.** Section 4.4 mentions fractional voting briefly but does not address the mathematical structure of a legislative body with fractional members. Specifically: if county representatives have fractional votes, the body is no longer operating under majority rule in the standard sense (majority of members) but under majority of weighted votes. This has implications for the power index of each representative (Banzhaf or Shapley-Shubik), coalition formation, and procedural majority requirements (quorum, supermajority thresholds). A paper targeting political scientists at the Annual Review level should engage with the political theory of weighted voting bodies, not merely note that it is "institutionally unfamiliar."
- **The paper cites Lessons 3 and 4 as if they were symmetrical in structure to Lessons 1 and 2, but they have a different logical character.** Lessons 1 and 2 are about the sorting mechanism (how geographic sorting constrains proportionality) and are grounded in a causal claim. Lessons 3 and 4 are about the costs of specific institutional choices (county representation, state boundaries) and are grounded in empirical comparisons. The paper's synthesis (Section 7) treats all five lessons as equivalent inputs to the design hierarchy, but Lessons 3 and 4 do not bear on the sorting mechanism at all --- they quantify the costs of institutional constraints that are separate from sorting. The synthesis should distinguish between sorting-based lessons (1, 2) and institutional-cost lessons (3, 4) before combining them in the constraint hierarchy.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **The paper should engage more explicitly with the mixed-member proportional (MMP) system as a potential hybrid.** Section 8 ("Limitations and Future Research") mentions hybrid systems (multi-member in urban, single-member in rural) as future research, but MMP systems (Germany, New Zealand) are the most widely adopted hybrid. The international evidence from E.6 suggests that the algorithm transfers to Westminster systems; a brief discussion of whether it could be adapted for MMP systems would connect the E.7 lessons to the most institutionally familiar non-US alternative.
- **The coda ("Value of Algorithmic Counterfactuals") is well-written and should be promoted.** The observation that "algorithmic implementation of constitutionally unavailable systems... has significant analytical value even when the systems cannot be adopted" --- and that this is a uniquely algorithmic contribution to representation design theory --- is one of the most important methodological claims in the paper. It should appear in the introduction, not as a coda.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The design tension framework and the exchange rate derivation are the paper's strongest contributions. The three P1 items address real analytical gaps: the PP sensitivity limitation, the underdeveloped fractional voting mathematics, and the conflation of sorting-based and institutional-cost lessons. The PP limitation requires either an additional metric or a caveat; the fractional voting issue requires engagement with weighted voting theory; the lessons conflation requires a structural revision of the synthesis section.
