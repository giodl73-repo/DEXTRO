\begin{abstract}
Multi-constraint graph partitioning is the standard approach for balancing multiple objectives in graph partitioning problems. However, we demonstrate that for congressional redistricting under the Voting Rights Act (VRA), edge-weighted single-objective optimization dramatically outperforms multi-constraint methods. Across 160 experiments on five states, edge-weighted optimization achieves a 47.9\% configuration success rate compared to multi-constraint's 35.0\%, with 7 percentage points higher minority concentration and only 13\% compactness penalty. We provide both theoretical and empirical evidence that constraint conflict---where tight population balance constraints dominate loose minority concentration constraints---fundamentally limits multi-constraint effectiveness. Through systematic experiments varying constraint tolerance from $\pm30\%$ to $\pm400\%$, we show that even extreme loosening of minority constraints fails to achieve VRA compliance targets. Our findings generalize beyond redistricting: when partitioning problems have one tight constraint and secondary aspirational goals, encoding the secondary goal in edge weights is more effective than multi-constraint formulation. This work provides practical guidance for algorithm selection in graph partitioning and challenges the assumption that multi-objective problems always benefit from multi-constraint methods.
\end{abstract}
