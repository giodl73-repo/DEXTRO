\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We introduce Centroidal Voronoi Districts (\cvd{}), a new Structure-layer
algorithm for redistricting that assigns each census tract to its nearest
seed by graph hop-count distance and iteratively moves seeds to the
population-weighted graph-distance medoid of their assigned district.
\cvd{} is the discrete redistricting analogue of Lloyd's algorithm
for optimal quantisation~\citep{lloyd1982}, adapted to weighted planar
graphs via the \emph{graph-distance medoid} as the centroid surrogate.

Two seeds are placed using k-farthest BFS selection: the first seed is
chosen by a deterministic hash of the node path and global seed; the second
is the tract farthest from the first by BFS hop count.
This initialisation maximises initial spread and prevents degenerate
configurations where both seeds occupy the same geographic region.
Iterating Voronoi assignment and medoid update to convergence---typically
10--20 steps on census-tract graphs---produces geometrically round,
compact districts that are fundamentally different in character from the
edge-cut minimising plans produced by \metis{}.

Empirically, \cvd{} on North Carolina ($k=14$) achieves Polsby-Popper
compactness competitive with \metis{} despite not optimising edge cuts,
and typically converges in 8--14 iterations.
\cvd{} runs in $O(n \times k \times n_{\mathrm{iter}})$ time with no spanning
trees, no MCMC, and no parameter tuning beyond iteration count.
We compare \cvd{} with \metis{} and \sa{} on compactness, edge cut, and
runtime, and preview Phase~2 Geographic \cvd{} using Euclidean distance on
EPSG:5070 projected tract centroids.
\end{abstract}
