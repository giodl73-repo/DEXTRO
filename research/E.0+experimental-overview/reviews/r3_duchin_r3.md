## Response to Revision

Round 2 maintained a score of 3 because three P1 items were unaddressed: (1) VRA benchmark specification, (2) county preservation effective rate, (3) manipulation resistance criterion. This round addresses items 1 and 3. Item 2 remains open.

**P1.1 — VRA benchmark specification (addressed, satisfactory).** The footnote to Section 3.1 now provides the operational definition I requested: (a) a three-tier scoring specification tied to the 42% CVAP threshold from D.1, (b) the MMD definition as >50% single-minority CVAP, (c) the data source (Census P.L. 94-171). This makes the minority representation column of Table 1 independently verifiable. I had asked for exactly this. The citation to D.1 for the threshold derivation is appropriate — the threshold itself is established empirically in the companion paper, and the footnote correctly points readers there for the underlying methodology. Satisfied.

**P1.3 — Manipulation resistance criterion (addressed, satisfactory).** The paper now includes a fifth column in Table 1 and a below-table note explaining the scoring rationale. The scoring is internally consistent: systems that accept partisan data inputs (E.4, E.5) score 2 on the grounds that parameterizable data inputs create gaming vectors; systems that are fully data-agnostic and deterministic score 3. The note correctly distinguishes county representation (score 2, because county boundary-drawing is susceptible to legislative manipulation over time) from the DIA and E.1 (score 3, fully deterministic). From my perspective in redistricting mathematics, this distinction is exactly right. The manipulation resistance of deterministic algorithmic systems is not absolute — a sufficiently bad-faith legislature could adopt any redistricting method by statute and then repeal it — but the paper correctly identifies the proximate source of manipulation risk for each system. Satisfied.

**P1.2 — County preservation effective rate (not addressed).** The paper still reports "38% whole-county preservation" without distinguishing counties that are physically preservable (small enough relative to district quotas that they could be preserved whole) from counties that are not preservable regardless of algorithm (too large or too small relative to quotas). The effective rate — preservable counties preserved / preservable counties total — is the meaningful metric. This gap remains open. I note it for the response letter and final revision but accept that the paper can go forward without it.

## Recalibration

Two of my three P1 items are now addressed. The third (county preservation effective rate) is a methodological refinement that would strengthen the paper's claims about county preservation but is not a fatal gap. The manipulation resistance column closes the most important substantive gap in the paper's Pareto argument. The VRA benchmark footnote makes the minority representation column independently verifiable. Both items addressed were the ones I had most urgently flagged.

## Score: 4 — Accept with Minor Revision

The paper is at *Electoral Studies* / *Perspectives on Politics* quality. My remaining concern (county preservation effective rate) should be addressed in the final manuscript response letter and preferably in a footnote or supplementary note in the paper, but it does not require another review round.
