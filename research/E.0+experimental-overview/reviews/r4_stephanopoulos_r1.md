## Summary

E.0 is the design-space synthesis for the E-track, systematically comparing six alternative representation systems and providing a constitutional taxonomy. From a law and political science perspective, this paper is attempting to answer a question of direct policy relevance: given that *Rucho* has foreclosed federal judicial remedies for partisan gerrymandering, what structural alternatives exist and what are their costs and benefits? The constitutional taxonomy in Section 4 is the paper's strongest contribution to this literature --- it provides the clearest available summary of the legal obstacles to each reform proposal. However, the paper has significant gaps in its engagement with the post-*Moore v. Harper* legal landscape and in its treatment of the efficiency gap as a proportionality measure, and its conclusion that the DIA is the "Pareto-optimal feasible choice" is both overclaimed and potentially counterproductive for reform advocates.

## Strengths

- **The constitutional taxonomy (Section 4, Table 2) is the most practically useful contribution in the paper.** The four-category classification (Available/State statute; Available/Federal statute; Constrained/Amendment; Unavailable/Amendment) for all six systems and the DIA is more precise than anything in the reform literature. The distinction between E.1 (federal statute, repeal of 2 U.S.C. §2c) and E.2 strict version (constitutional amendment for fractional voting) is legally accurate and practically important for advocacy organisations deciding where to focus their efforts.
- **Correctly framing E.4 (partisan-similarity clustering) as a demonstration that algorithmic gerrymandering is technically feasible is an important normative contribution.** The redistricting reform debate sometimes implies that algorithmic redistricting prevents partisan manipulation. E.4 directly refutes this: if the algorithm is allowed to use partisan data, it can produce partisan outcomes just as effectively as legislative map-drawing. The value of the DIA is not that algorithms cannot gerrymander but that the DIA algorithm is specifically designed not to.
- **The Pareto table's presentation of trade-offs is better than a ranked list of systems.** Presenting systems as Pareto-competing rather than ranking them avoids the implicit value judgment that any one objective is more important than others. This is the right approach for a paper targeting both political scientists (who care about proportionality) and judges (who care about compactness and community preservation) simultaneously.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The paper does not address *Moore v. Harper* (2023) and its implications for state-court redistricting review.** The introduction correctly notes *Moore v. Harper*'s rejection of the independent-state-legislature theory and its confirmation that state courts may review redistricting under state constitutional law. But the paper does not draw out the implication: *Moore* substantially expanded the venue for redistricting challenges and reform, because state courts applying state constitutional standards are now unambiguously open as a forum. This has significant implications for the constitutional taxonomy: systems that are "available" via state statute (including the DIA) are not only legally available but also judicially reviewable --- meaning their compliance with state constitutional compactness, community preservation, and partisan fairness standards matters enormously. The paper should connect *Moore* to the constitutional taxonomy more explicitly.
- **The paper does not distinguish between proportionality as a descriptive finding and proportionality as a normative criterion.** Section 3 uses proportionality as one of four evaluation dimensions, but it is unclear throughout whether the paper is (a) empirically reporting how proportional each system is as a matter of description, or (b) normatively recommending proportionality as a criterion that redistricting should aim to satisfy. These are different claims with different implications. If the paper is making a normative argument for proportionality, it needs to engage with the *Rucho* holding that partisan symmetry is a "judicially unmanageable standard" and explain why proportionality is nonetheless a legitimate evaluation criterion for non-judicial contexts. If it is making only a descriptive claim, the paper should say so explicitly and avoid language that implies proportionality is an objective that systems should pursue.
- **The conclusion's advocacy for the DIA as "Pareto-optimal" is counterproductive in the post-*Rucho* reform context.** The conclusion states that the DIA "occupies the Pareto frontier for feasible systems given current constitutional constraints" and that it is "immediately available without federal legislation or constitutional amendment." This framing implies that the most important reform is DIA adoption, with multi-member districts as a secondary, longer-term option requiring federal legislation. But in states that have already adopted independent commissions (California, Colorado, Michigan, Arizona), the DIA is not a reform --- it is a modest improvement in commission methods. For these states, the relevant policy question is whether multi-member districts should be added on top of algorithmic single-member redistricting, not whether the DIA should replace legislative map-drawing. The conclusion should distinguish between states that have and have not adopted independent commissions, and discuss the reform implications differently for each context.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **Provide quantitative context for the VRA implications of multi-member districts.** Section 4.1 notes that multi-member districts might create minority representation opportunities rather than diluting them, and cites E.1's finding that the MMD algorithm produces a "minority-representation surplus relative to the VRA benchmark." Quantifying this surplus (how many additional majority-minority districts?) would make the VRA analysis more precise and more useful for practitioners.
- **The E.5 (partisan-fairness allocation) section should more explicitly discuss the Article I, §4 argument.** Section 4.4 states that a congressional proportionality mandate is "constitutionally available" under Article I, §4 and cites the Court's broad deference to congressional redistricting regulation. But this argument is contested: the Supreme Court's post-*Rucho* reasoning was partly premised on the idea that proportionality standards are inherently political and therefore not judicially manageable, which might (though need not) make a congressional mandate similarly problematic under separation of powers. The paper should acknowledge this argument and explain why it does not apply to a statutory (as opposed to judicial) proportionality mandate.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The constitutional taxonomy is a genuine contribution to both legal and political science literatures. The three P1 items address real gaps in the paper's legal and normative engagement: the absent *Moore v. Harper* implications, the proportionality descriptor/normative confusion, and the overclaimed Pareto-optimal conclusion. These are contextual revisions that would substantially improve the paper's usefulness for practitioners.
