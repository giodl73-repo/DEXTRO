## Response to Revision

Round 1 raised three P1 items: (1) the absent Moore v. Harper implications for state-court review, (2) the proportionality descriptor/normative confusion, and (3) the overclaimed Pareto-optimal conclusion. I also suggested distinguishing commission states from legislative states in the reform implications.

**P1.1 — Moore v. Harper (addressed, well placed).** The footnote added to Section 5 (constitutional availability section) correctly states: "Moore v. Harper (2023) confirmed that state courts may enforce state constitutional redistricting requirements, which creates new state-court review venues for all systems listed as immediately available." It also correctly notes the DIA's model-statute status and that federal courts remain closed post-Rucho. Placing this in Section 5 (constitutional taxonomy) rather than the introduction is the right choice — it is a legal specification, not a framing claim. The connection to state-by-state adoptability is precise. Satisfied.

**P1.2 — Proportionality as descriptor vs. normative criterion (partially addressed).** The revised Section 3.2 methodology paragraph notes proportionality's unsettled post-Rucho legal status. This acknowledges the contested normative status of proportionality as a redistricting goal, which was my main concern. What is still missing is an explicit statement in the main Section 3 body (not just the methodology footnote) that the paper uses proportionality as a descriptive evaluation dimension rather than a prescriptive redistricting requirement. The sentence "we use it as a descriptive dimension rather than a prescriptive requirement" from the REVISION-PLAN description would make this explicit. Partially satisfied.

**P1.3 — Pareto overclaim (addressed, substantially improved).** The conclusion's revised heading "The DIA as Best Available Single-Member Option" and the non-dominance language are exactly right. The explicit statement that "This is not a claim of Pareto optimality for all possible systems, but of non-dominance within the examined portfolio" combined with the acknowledgment that "Multi-member districts weakly dominate the DIA on proportionality and minority representation" presents an honest and defensible picture. The abstract revision is correspondingly accurate. Satisfied.

**P2 — Commission vs. legislative state distinction (not addressed).** The conclusion still does not distinguish between states with existing independent commissions (where DIA adoption is an incremental improvement) and states with legislative redistricting (where it is a major reform). This is the most practically relevant context distinction for advocacy organisations, and its absence makes the reform implications section less useful than it could be. I note it remains open.

## Score: 4 — Accept with Minor Revision

The Moore v. Harper footnote and the Pareto overclaim revision are the most important legal and normative fixes, and both are well executed. The proportionality descriptor/normative distinction would benefit from a sentence in the main body of Section 3, and the commission vs. legislative state distinction remains undeveloped. Neither prevents acceptance. The paper now makes defensible claims about the DIA's position relative to the examined alternatives.
