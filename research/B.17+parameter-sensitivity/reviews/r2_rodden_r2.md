# Review 2 — Reviewer: Jonathan Rodden (Political Geography / Geographic Sorting)
**Paper:** B.17 — How Sensitive Are Redistricting Outcomes to Algorithm Parameters? A 50-State Sweep
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

The authors have made a genuine effort to address my Round 1 concern about state-level partisan effects. The methodology section now explicitly records a state-level partisan gap metric ($D_s - 0.5k_s$) as a second outcome variable alongside the national aggregate. The joint `ufactor × acounty` sweep for Wisconsin and North Carolina — the two states at the top of my concern list — provides the state-level analysis I requested for the two most consequential swing states. The paper is now at a level I find acceptable for publication, though some of my substantive concerns about the geographic interpretation of population neutrality persist.

## Addressed Issues

The swing-state analysis is now substantially more developed. Wisconsin and North Carolina are the focus of the joint factorial sweep (Section 4.6), and the state-level effects are directly reported for both. The finding that ΔD in Wisconsin reaches at most 0.4 seats in the worst-case joint cell is a more honest characterization of the state-level risk than the national aggregate alone. This is exactly the kind of state-level framing I argued was necessary.

The caution note about 2020 election data (now added to the conclusions) acknowledges the electoral-environment limitation. The note that the findings might differ in a more nationalized environment (such as 2022) is appropriate.

## Remaining Concerns

The paper still has not addressed my core concern about the population neutrality claim in Section 5.3. The claim that "population is not correlated with partisan preference at the tract level in a way that would make compactness parameters partisan" is a testable empirical statement. The geographic sorting literature (including my own work) documents precisely that urban-density is correlated with partisan lean. The paper does not provide a correlation analysis, and the state-level joint sweep for Wisconsin and North Carolina, while useful, does not resolve this for states like Michigan, Pennsylvania, or Arizona — all of which have strong urban-suburban partisan gradients.

I recognize that the paper's scope is parameter sensitivity, not geographic sorting, and that a full correlation analysis would be a significant addition. I am not making this a blocking condition for acceptance, but the paper should either (a) include the correlation analysis as a figure, or (b) qualify the population neutrality claim with the hedge "In the 50-state average..." rather than stating it as a universal structural property.

The state-level analysis for the full top-10 competitiveness list (Pennsylvania, Michigan, Arizona, Georgia, Nevada, Ohio — states I specifically named in Round 1) is still absent. The paper focuses on Wisconsin and North Carolina. A table showing the maximum ΔD per state across the parameter range, for the top 10 competitive states, remains the cleanest way to answer the question: is the national insensitivity result driven by diffuseness or by the specific swing-state geography? The joint factorial sweep is a start, but it is only two states.

## Minor Issues

- The 2020 electoral environment caveat should mention the 2024 environment as well — the nationalization question is not just about 2022.
- The efficiency gap discussion (new to the revision, responding to Stephanopoulos's request) is brief but adequate. It would benefit from a footnote connecting it to my own work on geographic efficiency gaps.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The swing-state analysis is meaningfully improved and the joint factorial sweep is a genuine contribution. I would like to see the population neutrality claim qualified, and the top-10 state-level table added — but I do not consider either blocking for publication.
