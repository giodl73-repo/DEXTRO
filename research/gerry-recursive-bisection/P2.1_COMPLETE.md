## Conceptual Contributions

### 1. **Geography as Constraint vs. Geography as Tool**
**Definition**:
- **Constraint**: Residential patterns that redistricting must respect → legitimate
- **Tool**: Strategic use of geographic knowledge to manipulate outcomes → illegitimate

**Implication**: Algorithmic redistricting treats geography purely as constraint (no partisan data access), while traditional redistricting uses geography as tool (even "bipartisan" commissions know partisan patterns).

### 2. **Process Legitimacy Framework**
**Position**: Geography-induced bias is legitimate IF redistricting process was neutral

**Justification**:
- Legal realism (Rucho forecloses outcome standards)
- Rawlsian procedural justice (fair process legitimates outcome)
- Judicial manageability (can verify process, not outcomes)
- Normative acceptability (geographic constraints vs. strategic manipulation)

### 3. **Three Lines of Evidence Convergence**
**Method**: Multiple independent approaches all demonstrate same conclusion
1. Statistical (correlation between sorting and bias)
2. Predictive modeling (Chen-Rodden model)
3. Qualitative case studies (WI, PA, MD mechanisms)

**Implication**: Triangulation provides robust evidence that geography, not algorithm, drives outcomes.

### 4. **Asymmetry Insight**
**Finding**: Geographic sorting doesn't universally favor Republicans

**Conditions for Democratic advantage**:
- State is majority-Democratic (65%+) AND
- Highly urbanized (80%+)
- Example: Maryland (6D-2R despite high sorting)

**Implication**: Geographic effects depend on state context, not just sorting magnitude.
