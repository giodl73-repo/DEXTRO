### Phase 3: Normative Framework (2-3 days)

#### 3.1 Philosophical Discussion (Rodden's core concern)
**Question**: Is geography-induced bias ethically different from intentional gerrymandering?

**Framework**: Three positions on geographic bias

**Position 1: Geography-Induced Bias is Legitimate**
*Argument*:
- Geographic distribution is natural/organic (not manipulated)
- Reflects genuine residential patterns (freedom of movement)
- Democratic disadvantage is consequence of voluntary clustering
- Courts cannot/should not override geographic reality

*Proponents*: Competitive federalism, Tiebout sorting
*Implication*: Algorithmic approach is normatively superior (doesn't manipulate)

**Position 2: All Bias is Problematic (Outcome-Based)**
*Argument*:
- Proportional representation is normative ideal
- Source of bias (intentional vs. geographic) is irrelevant
- Democratic voters systematically disadvantaged regardless of cause
- Minority rule violates democratic principles

*Proponents*: Proportional representation advocates
*Implication*: Algorithmic approach insufficient (still produces bias)

**Position 3: Process Legitimacy (Hybrid)**
*Argument*:
- Geographic bias is legitimate IF redistricting process is neutral
- Intentional manipulation is impermissible
- Geographic outcomes acceptable as long as they're not *exploited*
- Process fairness matters more than outcome proportionality

*Proponents*: Rawlsian procedural fairness, *Rucho* dicta
*Implication*: Algorithmic approach is appropriate (process-based legitimacy)

**Our Position**: **Position 3** (Process Legitimacy)

**Justification**:
1. *Rucho* explicitly rejects outcome-based standards (Position 2)
2. Position 1 ignores that gerrymandering *exploits* geography
3. Position 3 threads the needle: Process neutrality + geographic realism

#### 3.2 Legal Alignment
**Connect to *Rucho* analysis** (Section 6):
- Roberts rejected outcome-based fairness standards (efficiency gap, proportional representation)
- But invited "neutral criteria such as compactness"
- Geographic determinism = structural neutrality
- Algorithm cannot *exploit* geography (no partisan data access)

**Key distinction**:
- **Intentional gerrymandering**: Uses geographic sorting as *tool* for manipulation
- **Algorithmic redistricting**: Geographic outcomes are *byproduct* of neutral optimization

#### 3.3 Empirical Defense
**Argument**: Algorithmic outcomes match *geographic expectations*

**Evidence**:
1. **Correlation analysis**: Algorithmic bias predicted by geographic sorting
2. **Comparison to enacted**: Algorithmic outcomes less extreme than gerrymanders
3. **Seats-votes curves**: Algorithmic outcomes fall within expected range
4. **Case studies**: Urban concentration explains outcomes, not manipulation

**Conclusion**: Geography explains outcomes, not algorithmic bias
