\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Multi-scale MCMC (\ms{}) improves redistricting chain mixing by alternating
between fine-level tract proposals and coarse-level county proposals, with a
fixed probability $\alpha$ of making a coarse move at each step.
The optimal $\alpha$ varies by state: Texas ($k=38$, 254 counties, 5{,}265
tracts) benefits from $\alpha \approx 0.30$, while Iowa ($k=4$, 99 counties,
825 tracts) benefits from $\alpha \approx 0.15$.
Manual tuning of $\alpha$ across 50 states is impractical.

We introduce \ams{}, which tunes $\alpha$ automatically using
\emph{Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation}~\citep{robbins1951}.
Every $\Delta = 50$ steps the algorithm measures the observed coarse acceptance
rate in the current window; if it exceeds the target (0.30) $\alpha$ is
increased; if below, $\alpha$ is decreased.
The Robbins-Monro step size $\gamma_t = \gamma_0 / \sqrt{t}$ satisfies the
classical convergence conditions ($\sum \gamma_t = \infty$, $\sum \gamma_t^2 < \infty$),
so the alpha trace converges to the state-specific optimal under a monotonicity
assumption empirically satisfied for typical redistricting county graphs.

Empirically, \ams{} converges: North Carolina ($k=14$) stabilises at
$\alpha^* \approx 0.28$, Texas ($k=38$) at $\approx 0.33$, and Iowa ($k=4$)
at $\approx 0.17$.
Autocorrelation of the edge-cut objective at lag 100 matches or improves on
fixed-$\alpha$ \ms{} in all three states, with no manual tuning.
\ams{} is a drop-in replacement for \texttt{--search multiscale}, exposed as
\texttt{--search multiscale-adaptive}.
\end{abstract}
