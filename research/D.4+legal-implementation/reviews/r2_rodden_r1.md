# Review 2 — Jonathan Rodden (Partisan Implications / Electoral Geography Lens)
**Paper**: D.4 — Adopting Algorithmic Congressional Redistricting: Legal Pathways, Constitutional Constraints, and Model Legislation
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with Minor Revisions)

## Summary

This paper makes a serious legal argument for the constitutional viability of algorithmic redistricting and provides model statute text that is well-drafted and legally coherent. From an electoral geography and political science perspective, the paper has significant strengths in its structural analysis and some weaknesses in its treatment of the partisan implications of algorithmic redistricting — particularly the claim that algorithmic maps are politically neutral. I am recommending acceptance with revisions that primarily concern the paper's characterization of the partisan consequences of compactness-based redistricting.

## Strengths

The paper correctly identifies the structural mechanism by which algorithmic redistricting eliminates manipulation opportunities: data restriction, not operator virtue. This is an important and accurate framing that distinguishes algorithmic redistricting from commission-based reforms, which still depend on human decision-makers' good faith.

The three-pathway analysis (federal statute, state legislation/commission, court-ordered remedy) is comprehensive and the state constitutional typology (Types I-V) is a genuine contribution to the redistricting reform literature. The categorization of 50 states into five types based on constitutional obstacles to algorithmic adoption provides practical guidance that has been absent from the reform literature.

The court-ordered remedy pathway (Section 4.3) is particularly timely. Pennsylvania (2018), North Carolina (2022), and New York (2021) demonstrate that algorithmic methods are immediately available as remedial tools, without waiting for legislative adoption. The paper's argument that algorithmic redistricting is superior to special-master processes on transparency and speed grounds is well-developed.

## Concerns

**The paper systematically understates the partisan implications of compactness-based redistricting.** The paper acknowledges in a footnote (Introduction, fn. 2) that geographic sorting produces a Democratic disadvantage in algorithmic maps mirroring the efficiency gap literature, attributing this to urban-rural sorting. But this acknowledgment is buried in a footnote and is not integrated into the legal analysis in ways that matter. The claim in Section 6.4 that compactness-optimizing redistricting "produces more majority-minority districts than enacted plans" and generally protects minority voting is not paired with the countervailing observation that compactness optimization that respects urban density tends to "pack" Democratic voters and produce fewer competitive districts — a pattern documented extensively in the geographic sorting literature (Chen and Rodden 2013, McGhee 2020). This systematic partisan tilt from geographic sorting is not a minor technical footnote; it is a central political objection to compactness-only redistricting that the paper should engage directly.

**The political feasibility of federal legislation is asserted without serious engagement with the political obstacles.** The paper establishes the constitutional authority for federal algorithmic redistricting legislation, which is a genuinely important contribution. But it treats Rucho's invitation to Congress to act as a near-sufficient condition for congressional action, noting only briefly that "the question is whether the political will exists." In reality, any federal redistricting statute would require 60 votes in the Senate and would face unified opposition from whichever party currently holds more House seats from gerrymanders. The paper should be explicit that the legal pathway it establishes faces formidable political obstacles, and that the state and court-ordered pathways may be more immediately achievable.

**The "69 more majority-minority districts" claim requires more careful political contextualization.** Section 6.3 states that the algorithm "produces 69 more majority-minority districts than the enacted plans for the same states." This is presented as evidence that algorithmic redistricting serves rather than undermines Section 2. However, this net figure aggregates across states with very different political contexts: in some states, enacted Republican gerrymanders have deliberately fragmented Black and Latino communities (the Allen v. Milligan pattern); in others, enacted Democratic plans have deliberately packed Black voters into majority-minority districts to reduce their statewide political influence. The net +69 figure may reflect a combination of (a) correctly restoring fragmented minority communities in Republican-controlled states and (b) correctly reducing the over-packing of minority communities in Democratic-controlled states. These are very different legal situations under Section 2, and a single aggregate figure does not adequately distinguish them.

**The state constitutional typology does not account for the political dynamics of amendment adoption.** The paper identifies eight states where constitutional amendments are required for algorithmic redistricting adoption, and suggests amendments with "targeted" language. However, the political dynamics of redistricting-related constitutional amendments are not addressed. Redistricting reform amendments have been adopted by citizen initiative in Michigan, Colorado, and elsewhere, but have been blocked or reversed in states where the legislature controls the amendment process. The eight states requiring amendment are likely to include states where the legislature that would need to refer the amendment or the electorate that would need to approve it has little interest in reform. The paper should acknowledge this and discuss strategies that do not depend on legislative referral.

**The adjustment process creates a political vulnerability the paper does not fully acknowledge.** Section 3.2 describes a 60-day adjustment period in which "any person may submit to the Bureau proposed adjustments." The paper specifies that adjustments must not introduce partisan data, but in practice, geographic and community-of-interest adjustments in redistricting have historically served as proxies for partisan manipulation. Any adjustment that shifts a district boundary moves some set of voters — and sophisticated political actors can select adjustments that predictably shift partisan balance without referencing partisan data explicitly. The Bureau's review process (requiring "written explanation" for each accepted or rejected adjustment) may not be sufficient to prevent this. The paper should acknowledge this risk and propose stronger procedural safeguards, such as requiring the Bureau to run a counterfactual partisan-impact analysis on each proposed adjustment.

## Minor Issues

The paper refers to "district shapes that are geographically stable across three census cycles" (80% of boundaries stable, citing della Libera 2026stability). This temporal stability claim is important for the community-of-interest argument, but it conflates two different questions: whether algorithmic district boundaries are stable over time (which the cited paper likely shows) and whether those boundaries correspond to communities of interest as communities define themselves. A boundary can be geographically stable and still fail to respect a meaningful community of interest.

The paper's treatment of Hawaii, New Jersey, Montana, and New York as "Type IV: Hybrid" conflates states with very different commission structures and legislative ratification requirements. New York's failed 2021 referendum and the subsequent court-ordered redistricting suggest that Type IV states are far more complex than the paper's brief treatment acknowledges.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The constitutional analysis is the paper's core contribution and it is well-executed. Required revisions: (1) move the partisan tilt of compactness optimization from a footnote to the main text and engage it as a primary objection; (2) address the political feasibility constraints on the federal legislative pathway explicitly; (3) disaggregate the 69-majority-minority-district claim by state political context; (4) propose stronger adjustment-period procedural safeguards against proxy partisan manipulation.
