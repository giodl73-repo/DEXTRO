# Review 4 — Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law Lead Reviewer)
**Paper**: D.4 — Adopting Algorithmic Congressional Redistricting: Legal Pathways, Constitutional Constraints, and Model Legislation
**Round**: R2
**Score**: 4/4 (Accept)

## Summary

As lead reviewer for this paper, I confirm that the three Priority 1 statutory deficiencies are adequately addressed in revision: the VRA mode provision (§2(e)) is added; the compactness metric mismatch is addressed in Drafting Notes; the Gundy nondelegation and Miller v. Johnson race-predominance analyses are added to Section 6. My Priority 2 concerns about *Arizona State Legislature v. AIRC*, *Smiley*'s scope, and the Callais evidentiary mechanics are partially but not fully addressed. I am recommending acceptance because the P1 issues are resolved and the paper's core constitutional contribution is sound.

## Response to Round 1 Issues

**P1-A (VRA mode provision — CRITICAL, LEAD).** The §2(e) VRA Adjustment is a well-constructed addition to the model statute. Four points from an election law perspective:

First, the ``minimum necessary'' standard in §2(e)(1) correctly tracks the standard that applies to racial gerrymanders under *Bush v. Vera* and *Shaw v. Hunt* — race may be used to comply with VRA Section 2, but only to the minimum extent necessary. The statutory language now tracks the constitutional standard.

Second, the Gingles justification requirement in §2(e)(2) is correct but incomplete. It requires a written justification documenting satisfaction of the *Gingles* preconditions. However, *Gingles* prongs 2 and 3 (political cohesion and majority bloc voting) require access to election data that the redistricting algorithm does not use. The statute should specify that the Bureau may conduct a separate empirical analysis using election returns to determine whether Gingles prongs 2 and 3 are satisfied, even though that analysis is separate from the certified redistricting algorithm.

Third, the *Callais* citation in §2(e)(3) is appropriate and timely. The provision that ``partisan affiliation data is not used in determining the adjustment'' directly addresses the *Callais* disentanglement concern. A court reviewing a VRA adjustment under §2(e) could point to this provision, the published Gingles justification, and the absence of partisan data to establish that the adjustment was race-motivated (for VRA purposes) rather than partisan-motivated (impermissible under *Rucho* and *Callais*). This is the right statutory design.

Fourth, the publication deadline in §2(e)(4) (15 days before the final map adoption deadline) is appropriate and creates the documentary record the paper's VRA analysis requires.

**P1-B (Compactness mismatch — addressed via Drafting Notes).** The Drafting Notes paragraph is legally defensible. Courts will be able to evaluate compactness challenges by asking whether the algorithm was run correctly on the published adjacency graph, rather than by computing Polsby-Popper scores from shapefiles. This is a more administrable standard. Resolved.

**P2-B (Gundy nondelegation risk — addressed).** The Gundy paragraph in Section 6.1 is the most important doctrinal addition to the paper. It correctly identifies the four justices who have signaled interest in tightening the intelligible-principle standard (*Gundy* dissenters plus Kavanaugh J.'s *Paul v. United States* statement). The response — that the algorithmic redistricting mandate specifies a deterministic computation with no discretionary gap for the Bureau — is the correct doctrinal response. A statute that mandates ``compute districts using these mathematical criteria'' is categorically different from ``regulate in the public interest'' because the agency exercises no policy judgment. This analysis should survive even a reinvigorated nondelegation standard. Resolved.

**P2-C (Miller v. Johnson predominant-factor — addressed).** The Miller paragraph correctly identifies the applicable legal standard and applies it to the DIA's architecture. The argument that race does not ``predominate'' because (1) the base partition uses no demographic data and (2) the VRA adjustment is separately documented and analytically separable is sound under *Miller* and *Bethune-Hill*. Resolved.

## Residual Concerns (Not Blocking)

**P2-A (*Arizona State Legislature* — not addressed).** The paper still does not engage *Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission* (2015). The Elections Clause analysis remains anchored entirely in *Smiley* (1932). *Arizona State Legislature* is directly relevant because it expanded the meaning of ``Legislature thereof'' to encompass citizen initiatives — which raises the question of whether this expansion affects the scope of Congress's override authority under the second sentence of the Clause. The paper should at minimum note that *Arizona State Legislature* does not narrow Congress's Clause authority because the second sentence's grant to Congress is unconditional (``at any time by Law make or alter''). This is one paragraph, not a significant revision.

**P2-D (Gingles prongs 2/3 Bureau authority).** The §2(e)(2) Gingles justification requirement does not address how the Bureau determines whether prongs 2 and 3 are satisfied. These prongs require election data and ecological inference analysis. The statute should explicitly authorize this supplemental analysis even if it is outside the redistricting algorithm's scope.

**P3-A (Geographic sorting footnote — not addressed).** The partisan tilt from compactness optimization remains in footnote 2. As I noted in Round 1, this is the central political objection that legislative opponents of the DIA would raise. The paper would be more persuasive to legislators and legislative counsel if it addressed this directly in the main text.

## Recommendation

Accept. The P1 structural deficiencies are resolved. The Gundy and Miller additions substantially strengthen the constitutional analysis section. This is a major contribution to the redistricting law literature and should be published.
