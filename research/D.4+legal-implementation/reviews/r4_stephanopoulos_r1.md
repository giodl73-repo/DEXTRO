# Review 4 — Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law Lead Reviewer)
**Paper**: D.4 — Adopting Algorithmic Congressional Redistricting: Legal Pathways, Constitutional Constraints, and Model Legislation
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with Minor Revisions)

## Summary

This is a timely and well-constructed legal paper on algorithmic redistricting. The analysis is generally rigorous, the constitutional authority analysis is sound, and the model statute is carefully drafted in correct U.S. Code style. I have substantive concerns about several specific doctrinal claims — particularly the Smiley v. Holm application, the nondelegation argument, and the VRA safe harbor's interaction with Callais — that require revision before publication. None of these concerns fatally undermines the paper's thesis, but several require more careful treatment than the current draft provides.

## Strengths

The Elections Clause analysis is the paper's strongest doctrinal contribution. The argument from Smiley v. Holm (1932) to congressional authority to mandate algorithmic redistricting is well-constructed: Smiley established that Congress may specify not merely that states draw districts but how they draw them, and the proposed algorithmic mandate is precisely an exercise of that "how" authority. The paper correctly identifies that the argument does not require overcoming Rucho — Rucho is a limitation on judicial remedies for partisan gerrymandering, not on congressional authority to mandate redistricting methods.

The Huntington-Hill structural analogy is the paper's most persuasive single argument. The analogy is not just rhetorical: it identifies a direct precedent for exactly the kind of congressional action the paper proposes (specifying a mathematical formula for a politically sensitive calculation in the apportionment-redistricting domain), and the model statute correctly mirrors 2 U.S.C. §2a's structure. This is a significant contribution because it shows that such mandates have an 80-year track record of operating without constitutional controversy.

The state constitutional typology (Types I-V) is a genuine practical contribution. The categorization of 28 states as permissive (Type I), 14 as criteria-based (Type II), 6 as commission-mandated (Type III), 4 as hybrid (Type IV), and a small number as restrictive (Type V) provides actionable guidance that the redistricting reform literature has lacked.

The model statute's drafting quality is high. The three-section structure (definitions/certification, production/adjustment, judicial review) follows established U.S. Code conventions. The definitions subsection (Section 2(a)(1)) correctly specifies algorithmic requirements as properties rather than implementation details, allowing the Bureau to certify improved algorithms over time. The judicial review provision's limitation of challenge grounds to the four enumerated categories — algorithm non-certification, Bureau non-compliance, technical specification failure, partisan data introduction — is an elegant solution to the Rucho manageability problem.

## Concerns

**The Smiley v. Holm precedent is correctly cited but its application is potentially overstated.** Smiley (1932) held that Congress may require states to use single-member congressional districts and that federal law governed how Minnesota drew its districts. The Court's language that the Elections Clause encompasses "the authority to require that congressional elections shall be held in particular districts" supports congressional power over redistricting method. However, Smiley was decided in the context of a specific federal statute that pre-existed Minnesota's challenged action; the Court was not approving an advance mandate over future redistricting cycles. More importantly, the current constitutional landscape includes Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission (2015), which held that "Legislature" in the Elections Clause can encompass citizen initiatives and independent commissions. This expansion of "Legislature" may cut both ways for an algorithmic mandate: it may support the view that Congress can mandate algorithmic methods, but it also suggests the Clause is about process legitimacy, not necessarily about one specific method. The paper should engage Arizona State Legislature more fully to show that Smiley's force survives the post-2015 constitutional landscape.

**The nondelegation argument is addressed but not fully resolved.** The paper's response to the nondelegation challenge correctly identifies that the statute delegates to the Census Bureau (an executive agency), not to the algorithm itself. The algorithmic principle is presented as the "intelligible principle" satisfying J.W. Hampton. However, the paper's argument is more persuasive for the pre-Gorsuch nondelegation doctrine than for the reinvigorated nondelegation concerns signaled in Gundy v. United States (2019) and Justice Gorsuch's dissent. At least four current justices appear ready to tighten the intelligible-principle requirement substantially. The paper should acknowledge this doctrinal risk and strengthen its argument for why the algorithmic redistricting mandate satisfies a more demanding intelligible-principle standard — specifically, why the mathematical specification of the algorithm's constraints is more like a legislative choice of criteria (constitutional under any plausible nondelegation standard) than a delegation of the redistricting judgment itself.

**The VRA safe harbor analysis does not adequately address the Callais racial-sorting problem.** Section 5.3 proposes a safe harbor for states running the VRA mode, with burden shifting to challengers to demonstrate that an alternative configuration would produce additional Section 2 districts. This is the right structural approach. However, the paper does not address the Callais problem directly enough: in Louisiana, the district at issue was defended on VRA Section 2 grounds but challenged as a racial gerrymander under the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court's difficulty was that the record could not disentangle whether race was the predominant factor driving the district's shape. The paper's claim that algorithmic redistricting solves this disentanglement problem by providing a documented baseline plus documented VRA-mode modification is correct in principle, but the paper needs to specify what the court record would actually look like. How would a court determine, from the algorithm's code and parameters, whether racial considerations predominated over compactness in any specific district? The paper asserts that the three-part record (baseline, modification, explanation) resolves this question, but does not walk through the evidentiary mechanics of how a court would use this record under Miller v. Johnson's predominant-factor standard.

**The "intelligible principle" for the VRA mode is not fully stated.** The model statute certifies "a redistricting algorithm" and specifies that it must "not use, directly or indirectly, any data on the partisan affiliation, voting history, or electoral preferences of any person or geographic unit." But Section 2(a)(1) does not address the VRA mode at all: the statute as drafted does not authorize, prohibit, or regulate the use of demographic data for VRA compliance purposes. If the Bureau certifies an algorithm that includes a VRA mode using minority population data, the statute's prohibition on demographic data (which reads "partisan affiliation, voting history, or electoral preferences" but not race) arguably permits this — but a court could interpret the statute's silence on racial data as prohibiting it. The statute needs an explicit provision authorizing the use of minority population data for VRA compliance purposes, specifying the conditions under which that data may be used, and confirming that such use is consistent with Section 5's certification requirements.

**The court-ordered remedy pathway needs more discussion of the special master's relationship to the certified algorithm.** Section 4.3 argues that courts should order algorithmic redistricting as a remedy. The existing remedy practice involves appointment of a special master. But if Congress has not yet enacted the Districting Integrity Act, there is no "certified algorithm" for the court to reference. The paper should address how courts would evaluate competing algorithmic proposals from parties, what standards they would apply to determine which algorithm is "the" appropriate algorithm, and how they would handle arguments that alternative algorithms would produce different but equally valid results.

## Assessment of the Model Statute's Legal Coherence

The model statute is well-drafted and largely legally coherent. Section 1 (Findings and Purpose) correctly cites the constitutional authority, precedents, and policy rationale. Section 2 (Algorithmic Redistricting Method) correctly structures the certification-production-adjustment sequence. Section 3 (Judicial Review) correctly limits challenge grounds and provides administrable standards.

The statute has three technical drafting issues. First, Section 2(b) requires the Bureau to certify an algorithm "not later than 180 days after the date of enactment" without specifying recertification procedures for subsequent census cycles — if algorithmic methods improve, the Bureau should have authority to recertify without statutory amendment. Second, Section 2(d)(1)(A) requires adjustment proposals to be "documented by reference to objective geographic, municipal, or community-of-interest factors" but does not define what makes a factor "objective" or who determines objectivity in disputed cases. Third, Section 3(b)(4) authorizes challenges that "an accepted adjustment introduced data on partisan affiliation, voting history, or electoral preferences" — but the evidentiary standard for proving that an ostensibly geographic adjustment actually introduced partisan data covertly is not specified.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a major contribution to the redistricting law literature. Required revisions: (1) fuller engagement with Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission; (2) acknowledgment and response to post-Gundy nondelegation risk; (3) concrete evidentiary mechanics for the Callais disentanglement argument under Miller v. Johnson; (4) explicit VRA mode authorization provision in the model statute; (5) recertification procedures for the Bureau in the model statute.
