# Review 1 — George Karypis (Algorithmic / Technical Accuracy Lens)
**Paper**: D.4 — Adopting Algorithmic Congressional Redistricting: Legal Pathways, Constitutional Constraints, and Model Legislation
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with Minor Revisions)

## Summary

This is a legally focused paper that argues for the constitutional viability of algorithmic redistricting and proposes model statute text. My review focuses on the technical accuracy of the algorithm descriptions and the technical defensibility of the statutory requirements. The paper's legal analysis is not within my primary expertise, but the technical claims embedded in the legal arguments are within scope, and several require correction or clarification before the paper is ready for publication.

## Strengths

The central algorithmic framing is technically sound. The paper correctly characterizes the recursive bisection algorithm as operating only on population counts and geographic adjacency data from the census, and correctly identifies this data restriction as the structural mechanism that eliminates partisan manipulation opportunities. This is accurate.

The Huntington-Hill analogy is technically apt. The parallel between mandating a mathematical apportionment formula (equal proportions) and mandating a mathematical redistricting algorithm (recursive graph bisection) is structurally correct. Both involve congressional specification of a mathematical procedure that translates population data into political units. The technical depth of the comparison in Section 3.3 is appropriate.

The model statute's technical specifications are generally sound. The definition of "certified redistricting algorithm" in Section 2(a)(1) correctly identifies the required properties: census-and-TIGER-data-only input, no partisan data, population equality ≤±0.5%, contiguity, compactness maximization, and open-source requirement. These are technically necessary and sufficient conditions for the algorithm as described.

## Concerns

**The statute's compactness definition is underspecified for legal enforcement.** Section 2(a)(1)(C)(iii) requires the algorithm to "minimize the ratio of district boundary perimeter to district area (maximize compactness)." This is the inverse Polsby-Popper formulation. However, the statute elsewhere refers to "compactness" without specifying the metric, and the adjustment provision (Section 2(d)(1)(D)) imposes a floor requiring that adjustments not "reduce the compactness of any district more than 5 percent below the compactness of the corresponding district in the preliminary district map." Several technical problems arise: (1) Polsby-Popper is not the only legally recognized compactness metric — Reock, convex hull ratio, and moment of inertia are alternatives; (2) a 5% floor requires a specific metric and a specific mathematical definition of "5 percent below" (absolute or relative reduction?); and (3) METIS (the graph partitioner underlying the algorithm) optimizes edge cut in a graph, not perimeter-to-area ratio in continuous space — these are related but not identical compactness objectives. The statute should specify the exact compactness metric, its mathematical definition, and how the 5% floor is computed.

**The claim about population deviation performance needs qualification.** Section 2 states the algorithm achieves "population deviations of less than ±0.5% of the ideal district population for 98% of districts across all 50 states and three census years." This figure is drawn from the companion technical paper. However, the legal paper should note that this performance was measured under specific algorithmic parameters and census data conditions. Under a federal statute that delegates algorithm certification to the Bureau of the Census, the certified algorithm might differ in parameter settings from the research algorithm, potentially changing performance characteristics. The statute's ±0.5% tolerance is correctly specified as a hard constraint; the empirical performance claim should be presented as evidence that this tolerance is achievable, not as a guarantee of the certified algorithm's performance.

**The algorithmic treatment of VRA mode raises technical concerns the legal analysis does not fully address.** Section 5.1 describes the VRA mode as increasing "the weight on edges connecting census tracts with high minority populations, making it more expensive for the partitioner to cut through minority communities." This is technically accurate as a description of edge-weighting in the METIS graph. However, several technical issues arise. First, "high minority populations" requires a threshold definition: what percentage triggers the weight increase, and how is the weight scaled? The paper refers to della Libera (2026) for the formal description, but a legal paper making the VRA compliance argument should specify these parameters, because the constitutional defensibility of the VRA mode depends on the parameters' narrowness. Second, edge-weighting in METIS operates on a graph where nodes are census tracts and edges represent adjacency — the weighting is a function of the minority populations of the two adjacent tracts, not of any specific geographic target. The paper should confirm this architecture explicitly, as it matters for the Shaw v. Reno "racial motivation" analysis.

**The open-source requirement in the statute is underspecified.** Section 2(a)(1)(E) requires that source code be "published and publicly accessible." For a statute mandating a federal redistricting algorithm, this specification is insufficient. At minimum, the statute should specify: (1) the applicable open-source license (copyleft vs. permissive); (2) the publication timing requirement (before use, not after); (3) version control requirements (so that the certified algorithm can be independently reproduced); and (4) data publication requirements (so that the exact census data inputs used are also publicly available). A statute that publishes code but not reproducible data does not achieve the transparency goals the paper articulates.

**METIS specifically versus "equivalent" methods.** Section 3.2 refers to "recursive graph bisection via METIS or equivalent" as the algorithmic method. The model statute's definition of "certified redistricting algorithm" wisely specifies properties rather than a specific tool, allowing the Bureau to certify the best available algorithm. But the technical paper needs to clarify whether the legal argument depends on METIS specifically or on the class of recursive graph bisection methods. If the Bureau certifies a different algorithm — spectral methods, simulated annealing, Markov-chain approaches — do the legal arguments still hold? The population-balance and compactness claims should be algorithm-class-specific, not METIS-specific.

## Minor Issues

The paper's description of the Census Bureau's role in VRA compliance assessment (Section 5.3) does not address how the Bureau would determine whether the Gingles preconditions are satisfied for a given minority community — determining political cohesion and majority bloc voting requires access to election data the algorithm does not use. The Bureau would need to conduct a separate empirical analysis for VRA purposes. The statute should address this.

The paper does not address algorithmic error handling: what happens if the algorithm fails to converge, produces non-contiguous districts due to geographic irregularities (islands, exclave census tracts), or produces degenerate outputs for states with a single congressional district? A legal mandate needs a fallback procedure for algorithm failure.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The technical accuracy is generally good, but the compactness metric underspecification in the statute is a material deficiency that requires correction, and the VRA mode parameter transparency issue should be addressed.
