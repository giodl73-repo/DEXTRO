# Review 3 — Moon Duchin (Mathematics / Algorithm-Law Interface Lens)
**Paper**: D.4 — Adopting Algorithmic Congressional Redistricting: Legal Pathways, Constitutional Constraints, and Model Legislation
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with Minor Revisions)

## Summary

This paper presents a sophisticated legal analysis of algorithmic redistricting adoption pathways, with model statute text and constitutional argument. My review focuses on the mathematical claims embedded in the legal analysis: specifically, whether the compactness claims are accurate, whether the population-balance claims are technically defensible, and whether the VRA compliance argument is mathematically coherent. The paper is generally careful but has several technical imprecisions that matter for legal defensibility.

## Strengths

The paper's technical framing of the redistricting problem is accurate and sophisticated for a legal paper. The introduction's distinction between the intent problem and the structural problem is particularly valuable: it correctly identifies that algorithmic redistricting solves a different and more fundamental problem than commission-based reform, which addresses intent but not structure. This framing should be useful for courts and legislatures evaluating the reform.

The Huntington-Hill analogy is mathematically apt and legally well-developed. Both the apportionment formula and the redistricting algorithm translate the same input (decennial census population data) into the same type of political unit (geographic constituencies for House representation), and both are specified by mathematical constraints rather than by political judgment. The paper correctly notes that Congress's 1941 choice of Huntington-Hill over other mathematically coherent alternatives (Hamilton, Jefferson, Adams, Webster) establishes that such choices are legislative, not judicial, questions.

The VRA safe harbor provision in the model statute is well-designed. The burden-shifting framework (states that run the VRA mode with documented parameters are presumed Section 2 compliant, with burden on challenger to demonstrate a feasible alternative with more Section 2 districts) is legally coherent and draws appropriately on Allen v. Milligan's analytical framework.

## Concerns

**The compactness claim in the statute is mathematically inconsistent with how METIS works.** Section 2(a)(1)(C)(iii) requires the algorithm to "minimize the ratio of district boundary perimeter to district area (maximize compactness)." The Polsby-Popper score is 4πA/P², where A is area and P is perimeter. But METIS is a graph partitioner that minimizes edge cut — the number of edges (adjacency connections between census tracts) that are severed by the partition. Edge cut in a graph is not equivalent to boundary perimeter in continuous space, because census tracts have varying sizes and shapes. A METIS partition that minimizes edge cut minimizes the number of inter-tract boundaries crossed, but does not minimize the length of those boundaries. The paper claims throughout that the algorithm optimizes "compactness" in the Polsby-Popper sense, but the underlying METIS algorithm optimizes a related but distinct quantity. This inconsistency should be corrected: the statute should either specify graph edge-cut minimization (which is what METIS actually does) or specify the continuous-space compactness metric (which would require a different algorithmic approach). Using imprecise terminology here creates a litigation vulnerability because a challenge could argue that the algorithm does not in fact satisfy the statute's stated compactness requirement.

**The population deviation claim deserves more rigorous treatment.** Section 2 states: "The algorithm analyzed in della Libera (2026) achieves population deviations of less than ±0.5% of the ideal district population for 98% of districts across all 50 states and three census years." The Karcher v. Daggett standard is not merely that deviations be below ±0.5% but that population equality be as nearly as practicable and that remaining deviations be justified by a legitimate state objective. The paper's legal argument is that ±0.5% satisfies Karcher. This is a defensible position (the Court has in practice approved small deviations when they result from adherence to objective criteria), but the 2% of districts exceeding ±0.5% are a legal exposure. The paper should address what happens for these outlier districts — are they concentrated in small states? Do they result from geographic constraints? — and why they satisfy Karcher's "as nearly as practicable" standard.

**The VRA mode's relationship to Callais is argued correctly but could be sharpened.** Section 5.1 argues that algorithmic redistricting resolves the Callais disentanglement problem because it provides "a baseline (no VRA mode), a modification (VRA-weighted mode), and a documented explanation of exactly what data drove each." This is the paper's strongest VRA argument, and it is correct. However, the paper does not explain precisely how a court would use this three-part record. In Callais, the court struggled because it could not determine whether racial or partisan considerations drove the specific boundary decisions. The paper's claim is that the algorithmic record makes this determination possible. The paper should walk through a concrete example: for a specific census tract boundary, what does the record show about whether that boundary was determined by compactness optimization, VRA-mode edge weighting, or neither? This concreteness would substantially strengthen the legal argument.

**The Shaw v. Reno analysis is too brief.** Section 6.4 addresses the VRA-mode Shaw challenge in two paragraphs. The argument is that compact algorithmic districts have a "complete non-racial explanation" (compactness optimization + geographic minority clustering) and therefore do not trigger Shaw's "bizarreness" concern. This is broadly correct, but Shaw and its progeny (Miller v. Johnson, Bethune-Hill v. Virginia State Board of Elections) have produced complex doctrine about when race is a "predominant factor" that is not reducible to district shape bizarreness. Miller v. Johnson's "predominant factor" standard would apply to the VRA mode's edge-weighting: if a court determines that the edge-weighting caused racial considerations to predominate over traditional redistricting principles, the algorithm's district shapes might still face Shaw scrutiny even if they are geometrically compact. The paper should engage Miller v. Johnson's predominant-factor standard directly.

**The algorithmic outputs for very small states are not discussed.** For states with one congressional district (Alaska, Delaware, Montana, North Dakota, South Dakota, Vermont, Wyoming as of 2020), the algorithm trivially produces a single district covering the entire state. The legal analysis focuses on multi-district states. For these states, the "algorithmic redistricting" produces no choices to make, and the model statute's certification and adjustment process is inapplicable in any meaningful sense. The statute should address this explicitly — likely by simply exempting at-large states from the algorithm requirement while maintaining the population-equality constitutional requirement.

## Minor Issues

The claim in Section 2 that "98% of districts" achieve ±0.5% deviation is stated without confidence intervals. Given that this is an empirical claim about a stochastic process over multiple census years, the paper should report this as a point estimate with appropriate uncertainty characterization.

The model statute's definition of "contiguity" (Section 2(a)(1)(C)(ii)) does not address geographic contiguity for states with islands or non-contiguous land masses. Hawaii and Alaska present edge cases. The statute should specify how geographic contiguity is defined for states with water boundaries.

The statute's reference to "section 2284 of title 28" for the three-judge panel requirement is correct, but the paper should note that the current application of §2284 to redistricting challenges involves significant case law (Shapiro v. McManus and its progeny) that may affect which challenges qualify for three-judge panel treatment.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong legal contribution. The required revisions are: (1) correct the mismatch between METIS edge-cut minimization and Polsby-Popper compactness maximization, with corresponding statute revision; (2) address the 2% of districts exceeding ±0.5% under Karcher; (3) engage Miller v. Johnson's predominant-factor standard in the Shaw analysis; (4) address at-large states in the model statute.
