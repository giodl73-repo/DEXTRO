## Response to Revision

The "4 of 5" → "all 5" correction is the paper's most important fix and it has been executed correctly. The paper now accurately states that VRASection achieves majority-minority districts in all 5 covered states at state house scale, with a clear explanation that South Carolina's success at state house scale (but not congressional scale) reflects the structural advantage of smaller districts for geographic compactness in dispersed-minority contexts.

**C4 (4/5 vs. 5/5 inconsistency)** — Fully addressed. Abstract, introduction, Table 1, and body text now consistently say "all 5 covered states at state house scale." The Table 1 note correctly distinguishes the congressional-scale failure (South Carolina at 764,000 persons per district) from the state-house-scale success.

**C2 (β₁/β₂ ratio when β₂ is not significant)** — Addressed. Table 3 now reports "N/A (β₂ ≈ 0)" for states where β₂ is not statistically significant (AL, GA, MS, SC), and the footnote correctly explains that the ratio is informative only when β₂ is significant. The paper now states that the correct interpretation for AL, GA, MS, SC is that partisan composition explains zero variation in boundary decisions — which is the most favorable possible result for Callais satisfaction. Louisiana (where β₂ is marginally significant) retains its ratio.

**C1 (Scale-invariance assumption)** — Partially addressed. The paper now notes that the spatial distribution of minority populations must be comparable at block-group and tract resolution for the threshold to be scale-invariant. However, no empirical evidence is provided to support this comparability. A brief check (correlating minority share at tract vs. block-group level for the five covered states) would strengthen this assumption. Without this, the scale-invariance claim remains an assumption rather than a verified property.

**C3 (Ensemble context for VRASection)** — Not addressed. VRASection's seed sensitivity is not tested. For South Carolina, where the result depends on the ability to aggregate dispersed minority concentrations into compact districts, seed variation could affect the count of majority-minority districts achievable.

## Assessment

The two most substantive corrections (5/5 finding, β₁/β₂ ratio when β₂ is not significant) are properly handled. The paper is significantly improved. I am upgrading from "revise and resubmit" to "accept with minor revisions." The remaining issues (scale-invariance empirical support, seed sensitivity) are appropriate topics for future work.

**Score**: 3/4
**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions
