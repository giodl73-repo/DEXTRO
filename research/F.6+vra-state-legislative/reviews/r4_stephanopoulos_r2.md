## Response to Revision

The key legal accuracy improvement — correcting "4 of 5" to "all 5" — is the right fix. The paper now accurately characterises its primary empirical finding. The Gingles precondition 3 caveat is now in the abstract, which I requested. The Alabama/Allen v. Milligan distinction is addressed. These are important improvements for a paper that will be cited in redistricting litigation.

**C1 (Crossover and coalition districts)** — Addressed. Section 3.3 now includes a subsection on coalition and crossover districts, noting that VRASection's single-minority analysis applies only to majority-minority districts and that coalition district analysis (combining minority groups) is deferred to future work. This is the appropriate disclosure.

**C2 (Allen v. Milligan Alabama disambiguation)** — Addressed. Section 2 now notes that Allen v. Milligan addressed congressional maps specifically, and that F.6's finding of 27 majority-Black state house districts for Alabama is a separate inquiry from the congressional Section 2 obligation. This distinction is important for any litigant or court reading the paper.

**C3 (Louisiana v. Callais citation)** — Addressed. The citation has been updated to include the most current status information available.

**C4 (Categorical Callais satisfaction claim)** — Addressed. The claim that algorithmic maps satisfy Callais "categorically" has been qualified to "categorically with respect to the intent prong — boundary decisions reflect minority population concentration by construction, without partisan input." The paper now appropriately notes that results-based challenges (did the algorithm create too many or too few MM districts?) remain possible, though unlikely given the threshold-based design.

## Assessment

All four of my R1 concerns have been addressed. The paper is now legally accurate and appropriately qualified for a sophisticated legal audience. I am raising my score to 3/4 — a strong accept.

Note: I do not raise to 4/4 because the Duchin C2 concern (β₁/β₂ reporting methodology) was a statistical error that only Duchin identified and which affects the paper's quantitative presentation. The statistical methodology is now improved per Duchin's review.
