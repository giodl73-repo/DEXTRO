# Review 2 — Jonathan Rodden
**Paper**: F.6: Voting Rights Act Compliance for State Legislative Redistricting — The 42% Threshold at Chamber Scale
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4

## Summary

F.6 addresses the most politically consequential question in the F track: can algorithmic redistricting satisfy the Voting Rights Act at state legislative scale? The paper's answer — yes, in 4 of 5 covered states with the same threshold and methodology as congressional redistricting — is a significant positive result. The finding that state legislative VRA compliance is structurally easier than congressional VRA compliance is the paper's most important contribution for redistricting policy.

## Strengths

The finding that South Carolina fails at congressional scale but succeeds at state legislative scale (Section 3, Table 1) is exactly the kind of nuanced result that distinguishes this paper from prior work. South Carolina's Black population is geographically dispersed enough that it cannot meet the Gingles geographic compactness precondition at congressional scale (764,000 persons per district) but can meet it at state house scale (50,000 persons per district). This directly illustrates the structural argument of the paper.

The comparison of absolute versus proportional minority opportunity district representation (Section 5.1) is well-framed: more districts (state house vs. congressional) means more MM districts in absolute terms, but also a higher proportion of MM districts because smaller districts can match minority community boundaries more exactly. The example (60,000-person minority community as 94% of a state house district versus a small fraction of a congressional district) is concrete and compelling.

## Concerns

**C1 — The political relevance of state legislative VRA is understated.** Section 2 (Legal Background) notes that "state legislative Section 2 litigation has been less common than congressional litigation." But the paper does not explain why: state legislative VRA claims were historically less litigated because state legislatures could satisfy Section 2 through explicit race-conscious districting. After Shaw v. Reno and its progeny, race-conscious state legislative mapping became constitutionally suspect. The Callais framework — disentangling race-motivated from partisan-motivated districting — is just as applicable to state legislative maps as congressional maps, and algorithmic state legislative redistricting offers the same Callais advantage as algorithmic congressional redistricting. This connection should be made more explicitly in the introduction.

**C2 — Wisconsin not in analysis.** Wisconsin's state house partisan gerrymandering has been the subject of the most prominent litigation (Gill v. Whitford) and academic study. Wisconsin has a substantial Black population in Milwaukee (26% Black in Milwaukee County). Yet Wisconsin does not appear in the 13-state analysis set, either as a covered state or as an additional state. The paper should explain why Wisconsin is not included or add it to the analysis.

**C3 — Georgia Gingles applicability.** Georgia is listed with 55 majority-minority house districts out of 180 (30.6% of seats from 32.6% Black + 10% Hispanic population). The paper does not discuss whether these 55 districts include Black-majority and Hispanic-majority districts separately, or whether they are combined (Black OR Hispanic majority). Georgia's Hispanic population is geographically concentrated in different areas from its Black population, and Section 2 analysis typically proceeds by group. The paper should clarify the minority group composition of the 55 majority-minority districts.

**C4 — Partisan implications of state VRA compliance.** Section 5 notes that VRA-compliant majority-minority districts are less compact than non-MM districts (5.7% PP penalty at state house scale). But the paper does not discuss the partisan implications of MM district creation: majority-minority state house districts are overwhelmingly Democratic, and their creation may affect the partisan composition of neighboring districts (through the "bleaching" effect documented in Grofman and others). For states where VRA compliance creates many MM districts (55 in Georgia, 48 in Texas), this could affect the overall partisan balance of the state house. The paper should at least note this effect and whether the Callais methodology controls for it.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. C1 (Callais-VRA connection at state scale) and C3 (Georgia minority group composition) should be addressed.
