\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Standard \recom{}~\citep{deford2021} samples spanning tree cuts uniformly at
random, accepting every valid balanced cut without correction.
This biases the chain toward plans reachable by many spanning tree cuts---those
whose district boundaries lie in geometrically accessible regions of the
census-tract graph.
Forest \recom{}~\citep{mccartan2020} corrects this bias using a
Metropolis-Hastings ratio: given a proposal generated from a forward spanning
tree $T_{\mathrm{fwd}}$ with $c_{\mathrm{fwd}}$ valid balanced cuts, the
step is accepted with probability $\min(1, c_{\mathrm{fwd}} / c_{\mathrm{rev}})$,
where $c_{\mathrm{rev}}$ is the count of valid cuts in an independent
reverse spanning tree $T_{\mathrm{rev}}$ drawn from the proposed district pair.
We implement \fr{} in \texttt{redist-ensemble} as \texttt{ForestRecomChain}
and wire it into the compositor as \texttt{--search forest-recom}.
The chain satisfies \emph{expected detailed balance} with the uniform
distribution over valid plans as its stationary target: the ratio
$c_{\mathrm{fwd}} / c_{\mathrm{rev}}$ is an unbiased estimator of the
true transition probability ratio.
This is a weaker guarantee than the exact calibration of sequential Monte
Carlo~\citep{dellaLibera2026smc} (G.7) but a stronger guarantee than standard
\recom{} (unknown stationary distribution).
On North Carolina ($k = 14$), Wisconsin ($k = 8$), and Texas ($k = 38$), \fr{}
achieves acceptance rates of approximately $40$--$60\%$ (versus $\approx\!80\%$
for standard \recom{}) at roughly $2\times$ the per-step computational cost.
Verification on a small synthetic graph (4-node path, $k = 2$, $10{,}000$
steps) confirms convergence to the uniform plan distribution; a chi-squared
test rejects standard \recom{} and accepts \fr{} at $\alpha = 0.05$.
\end{abstract}
