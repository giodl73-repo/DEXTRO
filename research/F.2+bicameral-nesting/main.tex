       Spine-Compatible Bicameral Redistricting for All 50 States}

\begin{abstract}
Most U.S.\ state legislatures are bicameral, with house and senate chambers
whose district counts stand in a fixed constitutional ratio. The NestSection
algorithm \citep{dellaLibera2026nestSection} exploits this ratio by constructing
a \emph{spine}---a common geographic hierarchy at the level of
$\gcd(H,S)$ sub-regions---from which both house and senate maps are derived
by further subdivision. We apply NestSection to all 49 bicameral state
legislatures, classifying each by whether its $H:S$ ratio supports a
non-trivial spine ($\gcd(H,S) > 1$). We find that 41 of 49 bicameral states support a compatible ratio; the remaining 8 have
$\gcd(H,S) = 1$ (e.g., Maine: $S=35$, $H=151$; Delaware: $S=21$, $H=41$) and require independent
maps. For compatible states, NestSection incurs a compactness penalty of
$+2$--$3\%$ in mean edge-cut relative to independent redistricting, while
achieving exact nesting of senate districts as unions of house districts.
Partisan outcomes are rarely altered by nesting constraints: the expected
shift is $\leq 0.3$ seats across all compatible states at the 2020 cycle.
We discuss constitutional requirements for nesting, the geometry of spine
design, and practical implementation via the \texttt{redist --chamber}
flag.
\end{abstract}
