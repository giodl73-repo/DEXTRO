# Review: B.12 ProportionalSection — Round 3
## Reviewer: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Harvard Law School)
**Expertise**: Constitutional law, redistricting law, efficiency gap, voting rights

---

### Summary

The "Scope of Claims" subsection in §7 is exactly what I asked for in Round 2. Its five explicit disclaimers — particularly (1) that courts cannot be ordered to use this method, (2) that no partisan outcome is constitutionally required, and (3) that the algorithm is explicitly partisan — make the legal discussion considerably more responsible. I recommend acceptance.

### Strengths

1. **"Scope of Claims" subsection directly addresses my Round 2 concern.** This is the right addition. The five disclaimers are accurate and appropriately scoped. Item (1) is the most important for preventing misuse: demonstrating that dual-constrained bisection can reduce partisan gaps is not the same as establishing that courts can mandate it. The distinction is legally critical post-*Rucho*.

2. **The interaction with the rest of §7 is well-managed.** The Scope of Claims is positioned before the Federal Prohibition discussion, which means readers encounter the limitations before the aspirational legal applications. This ordering is appropriate.

3. **Nevada and MAUP additions strengthen the empirical grounding.** A legal paper that makes empirical claims about redistricting algorithms needs those claims to be robust to scale and to explain outliers. Both requirements are now better met.

4. **Theorem 2 scope remark is relevant for legal use.** Courts applying the Lorenz feasibility condition need to understand that it characterises contiguous plans only. Non-contiguous solutions (e.g., via multi-member districts) may admit proportional outcomes that the single-member contiguous framework cannot achieve. The remark establishes this correctly.

### Weaknesses

1. **Data source citation for Appendix A (D vote share figures) not yet added.** I asked for a footnote specifying the VEST/Fekrazad interpolation source for the Appendix A σ values. §5.1 cites Fekrazad (2021) for the main empirical table, but Appendix A lacks this citation. This is a P2 item.

2. **"Scope of Claims" item (4) could be strengthened.** Item (4) says the paper does not show that "states with σ < 0.05 are presently gerrymandered." This is correct but incomplete. The paper should also note that σ < 0.05 does not imply that a non-proportional outcome *cannot* result from gerrymandering — it means that if gerrymandering occurred, it operated through channels other than the σ signal. The current framing could be misread as suggesting that σ < 0.05 states are immune to gerrymandering claims.

3. **Callais v. Landry not cited.** *Callais v. Landry* (W.D. La. 2024), which imposed a second majority-minority district in Louisiana, is a recent case where algorithmic districting arguments were directly relevant. The legal discussion would benefit from noting this case as context for the VRA intersection with proportionality claims.

### Questions for Authors

1. Does the "Scope of Claims" disclaimer apply to state legislative redistricting as well? §7.2 discusses state applications but the Scope of Claims appears to be directed at federal applications.

### Suggestions

- **P1**: Strengthen Scope of Claims item (4): note that σ < 0.05 does not immunise a plan from gerrymandering claims — only from the specific claim that the σ mechanism produced the gap.
- **P2**: Add Fekrazad (2021) citation to Appendix A σ values.
- **P3**: Note *Callais v. Landry* in the legal discussion as a recent algorithmic redistricting context.

### Verdict

[X] Accept with Minor Revisions

**Rationale**: The "Scope of Claims" subsection resolves my Round 2 P1 concern. The Theorem 2 scope, Nevada, and MAUP additions are appropriate. The remaining items are P1/P2 at the editorial level.
