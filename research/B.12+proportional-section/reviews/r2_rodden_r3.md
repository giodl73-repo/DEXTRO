# Review: B.12 ProportionalSection — Round 3
## Reviewer: Jonathan Rodden (Stanford University)
**Expertise**: Political geography, urban-rural sorting, redistricting and representation

---

### Summary

The MAUP sensitivity paragraph directly addresses my Round 2 primary concern. The qualitative result — WI improves at η=1.10, NC worsens at all η, both at block-group resolution — is the answer I needed. The monocentric/polycentric disaggregation is not provided, but given the other improvements, I will not block acceptance on it. The paper is ready for publication.

### Strengths

1. **MAUP sensitivity paragraph provides the key result.** "Results are qualitatively identical" at block-group resolution is the answer my Round 2 concern required. The paper correctly identifies that partisan sorting patterns are stable across geographic scales, which is the MAUP robustness claim that matters here.

2. **Nevada explanation closes the most puzzling empirical gap.** The low-k granularity argument (55 tracts/district on average) is mechanistically correct and satisfying. I should have expected this — small k states are exactly where METIS has least flexibility to trade off competing constraints.

3. **"Scope of Claims" subsection is appropriate and well-placed.** Item (5) — "B.12 at any η is not guaranteed to improve proportionality, as Table 2 shows for NC" — is particularly important. The legal section needed this disclaimer to prevent overreach.

4. **Theorem 2 scope remark is correct.** Contiguity is indeed the binding constraint, and the distinction from non-contiguous assignments is important for the legal framing.

### Weaknesses

1. **Monocentric vs. polycentric disaggregation still missing.** I raised this in Round 2 as a theoretical framework question. The paper discusses WI (monocentric, Milwaukee/Madison) and GA (polycentric, Atlanta-suburban) but does not provide an analytical framework for why monocentric states respond differently to the B.12 constraint than polycentric states. This would significantly strengthen the political geography contribution. I accept that it is deferred to future work but note it as a missed opportunity.

2. **Vote data source sensitivity (2020 presidential vs. 2022 Senate) not addressed.** NC and WI had unusual 2022 Senate splits. The paper does not examine whether σ classification changes with the 2022 Senate returns. This remains a P2 item from Round 2.

3. **MAUP paragraph cites Chen & Rodden (2013) correctly but could engage more deeply.** Chen & Rodden (2013) is primarily about unintentional gerrymandering, not MAUP per se. The MAUP robustness literature (Openshaw 1984, Fotheringham & Wong 1991) would be more directly relevant. However, this is a P3 issue.

### Questions for Authors

1. At block-group resolution, does Wisconsin's η=1.10 improvement magnitude change? (The paragraph says "qualitatively identical" — does WI still improve by ~12pp?)

2. Does the σ classification of any state change from "free" to "cheap" or vice versa at block-group resolution?

### Suggestions

- **P2**: Report quantitative improvement magnitude for WI at block-group resolution (not just "qualitatively identical").
- **P2**: Test whether 2022 Senate returns change σ classification for NC and WI.
- **P3**: Replace or supplement Chen & Rodden (2013) with a MAUP-specific citation.
