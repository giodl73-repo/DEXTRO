# Review 4 — Reviewer: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law / VRA Jurisprudence)
**Paper:** D.5 — Quantifying VRA Section 2 Evidence with Algorithmic Redistricting: A Gingles Prong-by-Prong Methodology
**Round:** 1
**Score:** 2/4

## Summary

This is the most legally sensitive paper in the portfolio, and it has several significant legal errors and methodological vulnerabilities that must be corrected before it can serve as an expert witness methodology guide. The statistical framework is well-executed, but the legal framing of *Callais* is incomplete, the *Allen v. Milligan* characterization is partially wrong, and the Prong 1 threshold lacks the legal calibration it claims. I am issuing a score of 2/4 because the legal errors are serious enough that the paper would mislead practitioners if published as written. With revision, this could be one of the most valuable papers in the portfolio.

## Legal Errors and Major Concerns

**Error 1: Mischaracterization of the *Callais* holding.** The paper states that "*Louisiana v. Callais* (2026) added a specific methodological requirement for Prong 3: expert witnesses must disentangle racial bloc voting from partisan bloc voting using a regression with a partisan control variable." This overstates what *Callais* held. *Callais* (assuming the 2026 decision described here) addressed the specific context of Louisiana's defense against a Section 2 claim — it required that the district court's expert methodology be sufficiently rigorous to distinguish racial from partisan effects. It did not establish a universal requirement that every Section 2 litigant use WLS+HC3+Holm with a partisan control variable. The specific regression methodology described in this paper is the *paper's implementation* of the *Callais* disentanglement standard, not what *Callais* required. The paper should clearly distinguish between what *Callais* requires (disentanglement of racial from partisan bloc voting) and what this paper proposes (a specific implementation using WLS+HC3+Holm). Opposing experts could use different methodologies to satisfy the *Callais* standard, and the paper's exclusive focus on one methodology could be used against expert witnesses who use this methodology on cross-examination.

**Error 2: *Allen v. Milligan* characterization.** The paper describes *Allen v. Milligan* (2023) as having "reaffirmed the core Gingles framework and rejected Alabama's invitation to replace it with a race-neutral comparator methodology." This is accurate as far as it goes, but the paper's description of the Alabama redistricting background is incomplete in a way that matters for the worked example. *Allen* held that Alabama's enacted map with one majority-Black district violated Section 2 and required a remedial map. Alabama's subsequent remedial map (enacted 2023) was itself challenged as insufficiently compliant, and the Supreme Court approved the final remedial map in 2024 (*Allen v. Milligan*, post-remand). The paper's worked example uses the AL-07 district from the *original* Allen complaint, but the post-*Allen* remedial map actually implemented is different. The worked example should use the remedial map actually ordered, not the pre-litigation enacted map.

**Error 3: Prong 1 threshold legal calibration.** The paper claims that "all majority-minority districts generated by VRASection for the 2020 census have alignment scores above 0.55, and that districts in states where Prong 1 has been litigated and accepted by courts have alignment scores averaging 0.71." This empirical calibration is cited to "dellaLibera2026threshold," a paper that is not yet published and is not available for review. The 0.5 threshold is a critical legal claim — it is the paper's proposed standard for Prong 1 satisfaction — and it cannot rest on an unpublished citation. The paper must either include the threshold calibration analysis directly or withdraw the specific 0.5 threshold claim and replace it with a more conservative statement about what the threshold analysis shows.

**Error 4: Post-*Callais* framing of Prong 2.** The paper recommends using contested Democratic primary elections for Prong 2 cohesion analysis. This is methodologically appropriate but legally complex: in Alabama, contested Democratic primaries involving Black candidates are rare because the state is so overwhelmingly Republican that Democratic primaries have few contested races. The paper should acknowledge that primary election data may be unavailable for Prong 2 in many jurisdictions and explain how the methodology adapts when primary data is unavailable (the paper mentions using general elections with a partisan control, but this should be more prominently described as the primary alternative method, not a secondary option).

## Additional Concerns

The expert checklist (Table 2) maps redist commands to Gingles prongs, including "redist depo --verify" for "deposition-ready log with hash chain." But deposition readiness involves much more than a hash chain: it requires disclosure of all data sources, all software versions, all parameter choices, and a complete description of the expert's workflow. The paper should note that the redist depo module is one component of deposition readiness, not a complete solution.

The worked example shows that the partisan control variable reduces the racial bloc voting coefficient from 0.71 to 0.43. The paper interprets 0.43 as the "racial component" of majority bloc voting. But this interpretation assumes that the partisan control variable (Republican presidential vote share) captures all of the partisan component of majority bloc voting. In Alabama, where racial composition and partisan preference are highly correlated at the precinct level, the partial correlation interpretation is subject to multicollinearity concerns. The effective degree of separation between racial and partisan effects depends on the variance inflation factor (VIF) between racial composition and the partisan control. The paper should report the VIF for the Alabama model.

## Minor Issues

- The *Callais* citation shows "2026" which, if this paper is set in May 2026, means *Callais* is a very recent decision. The paper should note the decision date and whether any subsequent circuit-level implementation guidance exists.
- Section 3.3 says the paper uses "biracial general elections" where primary data is unavailable. "Biracial" is an unusual and potentially outdated term in this context; "cross-racial" or "racially contested" is more precise.
- The conclusion states that "prior to *Callais*, Prong 3 analysis was commonly reported without a partisan control variable, producing inflated racial bloc-voting coefficients that overstated the racial component." This is presented as a factual claim about prior expert practice. It should be supported with citations to specific cases where the no-partisan-control methodology was used.

## Recommendation

Major revisions required. The three legal errors (Callais mischaracterization, Allen timeline, Prong 1 threshold calibration) must be corrected. The VIF analysis should be added to the Alabama worked example. The deposition readiness discussion should be expanded. Despite these issues, the statistical methodology is fundamentally sound and the paper makes a genuinely important contribution to the field — it is worth revising rather than rejecting.
