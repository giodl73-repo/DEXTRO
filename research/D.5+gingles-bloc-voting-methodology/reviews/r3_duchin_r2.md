# Review 3 — Reviewer: Moon Duchin (Metric Geometry / Ensemble Methods)
**Paper:** D.5 — Quantifying VRA Section 2 Evidence with Algorithmic Redistricting: A Gingles Prong-by-Prong Methodology
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

The revision addresses all of my principal concerns: the ensemble-based Prong 1 robustness check is now clearly framed (option (a) vs. option (b) interpretation is explicit), the Holm vs. B-H discussion has been moved to the methodology section with B-H p-values reported alongside Holm, and the Alabama LOO table is now included in Section 5.3. I maintain my score at 3/4. The paper is a technically sound methodology guide.

## Addressed Issues

The ensemble-based Prong 1 framing is now explicit (Section 2.3): the paper states that the ensemble analysis asks "across ensemble configurations, what fraction have a district whose geographic core matches the minority community and whose alignment score exceeds 0.5?" — option (a), which answers "can a compliant district be drawn?" rather than "does this specific district consistently appear?" The legal implication is correctly stated: option (a) is the Prong 1 question, not option (b). This is the clarification I required.

The Holm vs. B-H discussion (now in Section 4.4 rather than the limitations) includes the justification I requested: Holm's conservatism is desirable in a legal context because a finding that survives a conservative correction is harder to challenge in court. The B-H p-values are reported alongside Holm p-values in Table 1 for Alabama, and the conclusion does not change under either correction. This is the right treatment.

The Alabama LOO table is now included in Section 5.3. For each of the eight elections analyzed, the Prong 3 coefficient β̂_race estimated excluding that election is shown, along with the Holm-corrected p-value for the remaining seven. All eight LOO estimates are in the range [0.38, 0.49] and all are statistically significant at p<0.01 Holm-corrected. The LOO stability claim is now verifiable from the table.

The Prong 2 WLS model is now numbered as Equation 2.1, resolving my minor notation concern.

## Remaining Concerns

The "disentanglement" terminology is now defined in the methodology (Section 4.2): the paper clarifies that "disentanglement" refers to partial-correlation analysis isolating racial from partisan effects, not to other statistical "disentanglement" methods. This is adequate.

The ensemble-based Prong 1 alignment score uncertainty bounds are now reported in the worked example (5th-95th percentile ranges for both Alabama districts). The ranges are both above 0.5 across the full ensemble, which is a strong result.

My residual concern is about the Prong 1 threshold calibration (Stephanopoulos's C2 concern): the claim that "all majority-minority districts generated by VRASection for the 2020 census have alignment scores above 0.55" is a strong empirical statement. Is this calibrated from the 50-state analysis, or from a subset? The revision addresses this partially (see Stephanopoulos's review), but from an ensemble methodology perspective, the threshold calibration should also report whether it is stable across different ensemble seeds — i.e., does every ensemble configuration for every state produce majority-minority districts with alignment > 0.55?

## Minor Issues

- The three "disentanglement" uses that lacked statistical precision are now clarified throughout the paper.
- The B-H supplementary columns are in Table 1 as I suggested in my minor issue from Round 1.

## Recommendation

Accept. The ensemble Prong 1 framing, the Holm vs. B-H treatment, and the LOO table are all genuine improvements that make the methodology reproducible and transparent. The paper is ready for publication.
