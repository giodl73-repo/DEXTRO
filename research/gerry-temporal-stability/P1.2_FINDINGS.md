## What This Means

### Hierarchical Structure Exists ✅
- Dendrograms clearly show binary tree structure
- Each state has log₂(k) tree levels (k = districts)
- Internal nodes represent geographic regions, leaves represent districts

### Hierarchical Structure Persists ✅
- **100% structural stability** - binary split decisions identical 2010→2020
- Same "North/South" splits, same "Urban/Rural" divisions
- Tree STRUCTURE is perfectly stable even though tract assignments change (71.6% disruption)

### Why Perfect Stability?
1. **Edge weights drive splits**: 5x weight for minority-minority tract pairs
2. **Demographics are geographically stable**: Minority populations don't radically relocate
3. **METIS spectral bisection**: Follows strongest eigenvector (most stable geographic division)
4. **Top-down hierarchy**: Coarse-level splits determined by large-scale patterns, less affected by local tract changes

---

## Key Insight: Structure vs. Assignments

**Important Distinction**:
- **Tree structure stability**: 100% (split decisions identical)
- **Tract assignment stability**: 28.4% (71.6% disruption)

**Why the gap?**
- Tree structure = binary decisions (left vs right at each node)
- Tract assignments = which specific tracts end up in which districts
- Census tract redrawing (26% of tracts) forces new assignments
- But the overall splitting pattern (major geographic divisions) remains identical

**Analogy**: Think of it like a city's neighborhood structure:
- The major divisions (North Side, South Side) remain stable (100%)
- But individual buildings and streets change over time (71.6% change)
- The hierarchy persists even though details shift
