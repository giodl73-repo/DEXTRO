## Key Findings

### 1. Similar VRA Compliance (Both Methods Work)
- **Recursive bisection**: 10 MM districts (2020)
- **N-way partitioning**: 12 MM districts (2020)
- **Difference**: +2 MM districts for n-way (20% advantage)

**Conclusion**: Both methods achieve strong VRA compliance. N-way has slight advantage due to simultaneous k-way optimization flexibility.

### 2. Dramatic MM Growth (2010 → 2020)
- **2010**: 1 MM district total (Georgia only)
- **2020**: 10-12 MM districts total
- **Growth**: 900-1100% increase!

**Why**: Substantial demographic shifts in southern states over the decade:
- Black population growth in suburban areas
- Hispanic population growth (+60% in Georgia)
- Increasing racial diversity

### 3. Georgia Case Study: Stability vs. Representation
**Most Important Finding**:

| Method | 2010 MM | 2020 MM | Change |
|--------|---------|---------|--------|
| Recursive | 1 | 7 | +6 (600%) |
| N-way | 1 | 8 | +7 (700%) |

**Implication**: Creating 6-7 new MM districts REQUIRES substantial boundary disruption. Maintaining 2010 boundaries (high stability) would PREVENT formation of these districts.

**Key Insight**: In Georgia's case, **disruption is necessary for representation**. The 1.1% stability advantage becomes a disadvantage when demographics demand new MM districts.

### 4. MM District Persistence
Georgia's original MM district (district 1):
- **Recursive**: 0% persistence (redistributed to create 7 new MM districts)
- **N-way**: 100% persistence (preserved + created 7 new MM districts)

**Interpretation**: N-way balances preservation + creation, recursive prioritizes aggregate MM count.

## Normative Framework: When Does Stability Matter?

### Stability is Valuable When:
✅ Demographics relatively constant (<10% shifts)
✅ Existing MM districts maintain status
✅ Community boundaries stable
✅ Voters benefit from district continuity

**Examples**: Alabama, South Carolina (minimal demographic change)

### Disruption is Necessary When:
✅ Demographics shift substantially (>20% minority growth)
✅ New MM districts become geographically feasible
✅ Old boundaries would dilute minority voting power
✅ VRA compliance requires opportunity districts

**Example**: Georgia (1 → 7-8 MM districts)

### The Balance
- **Stable contexts**: 1.1% stability advantage is beneficial
- **Dynamic contexts**: Stability advantage irrelevant or harmful
- **Representation > Stability**: When they conflict, representation prevails

---

## Policy Implications

### 1. Demographic Trend Analysis First
Before prioritizing stability, analyze demographic trends:
- If minority populations grew >20% or shifted geographically
- Expect new MM districts will require disruption regardless of method

### 2. Context-Dependent Method Selection
- **Stable demographics** → Use recursive bisection (1.1% stability advantage)
- **Dynamic demographics** → Use n-way (2 MM district advantage + flexibility)
- **Mixed contexts** → Consider hybrid or regional approaches

### 3. Stability is a Means, Not an End
- Temporal stability serves voters (district continuity)
- But representation is paramount
- VRA doesn't recognize "grandfathering" when demographics shift
