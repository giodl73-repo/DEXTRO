\begin{abstract}
Algorithmic redistricting replaces human map-drawing with geometric
optimization, promising immunity from partisan manipulation. Yet
immunity from gerrymandering is not the same as validity: an algorithm
that produces compact, population-balanced districts across one census
year under one spatial resolution may fail silently under other
conditions. This paper synthesizes five empirical validation studies
(C.1--C.5) that together establish the robustness of METIS recursive
bisection redistricting across four dimensions: spatial resolution,
temporal continuity, demographic stability, and partisan neutrality.
Key findings across the C-track are: (1)~geography drives outcomes
more than resolution---Polsby-Popper compactness varies by $< 0.005$
across the 130$\times$ unit-count range from census tracts to blocks;
(2)~the algorithm is temporally stable across three census decades,
with compactness varying by $\approx 10\%$ nationally between 2000 and
2020; (3)~district boundaries show geographic clustering stability of
$r = 0.73$ across census cycles; (4)~the mean absolute efficiency gap
of algorithmic plans is $0.04$, less than half the $0.08$ mean for
enacted plans. Together these properties constitute a validation
portfolio that satisfies the reproducibility and transparency
requirements of the Districting Integrity Act (DIA).
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} redistricting, validation, robustness, MAUP,
temporal stability, efficiency gap, partisan fairness, DIA
