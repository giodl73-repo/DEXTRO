## Summary

The revisions address the most important of my Round 1 concerns: the definitional paragraph distinguishing metric stability from boundary stability is exactly what was needed, and it is placed at precisely the right location (the opening of Section 4 before the empirical discussion begins). The definitions are tight — "mean PP score varies less than 0.010 across census years" and "Jaccard similarity ≥ 0.70" are operationally precise, not vague conceptual gestures. The VRA property addition (Property 5) is appropriately scoped for covered states and handles the Louisiana exception honestly. My Round 1 concern about the logical chain between properties (Section 6.2) has not been directly revised, but the new definitional structure in Section 4 partially addresses it by making the distinctions cleaner — the implicit logical dependencies between the properties become easier to follow now that metric and boundary stability are formally separated.

## Evaluation of Revisions

**P1.1 (Metric/boundary conflation)**: Fully resolved. The new paragraph is the most important addition in this revision. The explicit statement that "C.3 measures metric stability; C.4 measures boundary stability" converts what was an implicit organizational choice into a stated structural decision that readers can verify. The note that "both are necessary" and that they "can diverge" is correct and important — a plan with stable PP and unstable boundaries would satisfy C.3 but not C.4, and vice versa. Courts reading this paper will now understand which paper answers which stability challenge.

**P1.4 (DIA legal status)**: Resolved. The footnote is appropriately placed at first mention and correctly characterises the DIA's non-enacted status without undermining the paper's use of DIA as a research framework.

**P1.5 (VRA as fifth validated property)**: Well executed. Property 5 is confined to the 5 covered states where VRA Section 5 is most legally salient, which avoids overreach. The 4/5 success rate is honest. The Louisiana failure is identified as a "dispersed geography" case — a geographic characterisation rather than an algorithmic failure, which is consistent with Property 3's geographic-determinism framing.

**P1 items 1 and 2 from Round 1 (logical chain; EG limitations)**: Not directly revised. The logical chain argument in Section 6.2 remains as stated in Round 1. The EG limitations (Tausanovitch-Warshaw pathologies, partial metric interdependence) are not discussed. I am raising my score despite these omissions because (a) the definitional work in Section 4 partially addresses the conceptual confusion underlying my P1.1, and (b) the EG limitations point was a P1 in my Round 1 but the paper's multi-metric convergence (five metrics, r > 0.89) is a partial mitigation that I should credit more than I did in Round 1.

## Remaining Minor Concerns

The Section 6.2 property-chain argument remains an assertion rather than a demonstrated logical entailment. This is not a new problem, and it does not rise to a blocking concern given the overall quality of the revision. If this paper is revised for a subsequent round or for journal submission, revisiting the "prerequisite" framing in Section 6.2 would be worthwhile.
