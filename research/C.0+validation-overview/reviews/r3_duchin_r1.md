## Summary

C.0 organises a five-paper validation program into four analytical properties and a DIA compliance mapping. The organisational contribution is genuine: redistricting research too rarely articulates what it means for an algorithm to be "validated," and this paper's four-property framework (spatial robustness, temporal stability, demographic neutrality, partisan neutrality) fills that gap. The paper is at its strongest when it presents the MAUP robustness finding (Section 2) and the multi-metric partisan fairness convergence (Section 5); it is at its weakest in its treatment of the mathematical relationship between the four properties (Section 6.2). The claim that the four properties form a logical chain of dependency ("P1 is a prerequisite for P2," etc.) is asserted but not proven, and closer analysis reveals the logical relationships are weaker than presented. The paper would benefit from a more careful treatment of what validation means mathematically, not just empirically.

## Strengths

- **The MAUP result is genuinely important for the redistricting literature.** The finding that PP varies by $< 0.005$ across a 130x resolution range is significant evidence that compactness scores are measuring a real geographic property (shape complexity relative to boundaries) rather than an artifact of data resolution. This validates the use of census tracts as a computational shortcut, which has been an implicit assumption in virtually all computational redistricting research. Making this explicit and empirically testing it is a contribution.
- **The slice-based validation framework (C.2) is a methodological innovation worth highlighting.** The idea of constructing persistent geographic partitions ("slices") that remain comparable across census cycles --- using 2010 centroids as the reference and matching 2000/2020 tracts by nearest centroid --- is a clean solution to the cross-census comparison confound. This framework deserves a clearer description in C.0, as it is the methodological foundation for the temporal stability claims.
- **Acknowledging the 71% IoU as a structural consequence (not an optimisation target) is intellectually important.** Section 4.3's observation that "district stability is not a goal the algorithm explicitly optimises" and that the 71% IoU is "a structural consequence of geographic stability" is exactly the right framing. This distinguishes between designed stability (which could be gamed) and emergent stability (which reflects genuine geographic coherence).

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The logical chain between properties (Section 6.2) is asserted, not demonstrated.** Section 6.2 claims that P1 (spatial robustness) is "a prerequisite" for P2 (temporal stability) because "changing spatial resolution could also change behavior between census years." This is a plausible concern, but it is presented as a logical entailment when it is actually a conditional claim. The argument would need: (a) evidence that tract boundaries change meaningfully between census years (which they do --- tracts are redrawn after each census), and (b) evidence that this boundary change is similar in magnitude to the resolution change tested in C.1. Without both, the logical prerequisite claim is unestablished. Similarly, the claim that P3 (demographic neutrality) strengthens the P4 (partisan neutrality) claim requires showing that demographic composition is a confounder for partisan bias, which is true but unstated. Section 6.2 should be rewritten as "connections between the properties" that explains the logical relationships more carefully, rather than asserting a strict dependency chain.
- **The efficiency gap's limitations as a fairness metric are not acknowledged.** The paper uses EG throughout as if it were a settled measure of partisan fairness. But the EG literature documents well-known pathologies: it is highly sensitive to the number of competitive districts (Tausanovitch and Warshaw 2017), it can produce misleading results when parties win districts by very different margins, and it conflates packing and cracking effects that may have different legal significance. A synthesis paper that claims partisan neutrality as a validated property should engage with these limitations, even briefly. The convergence of five metrics (Section 5.4) partially addresses this, but the paper does not note that mean-median difference, declination, and EG are not fully independent (they share the election-result inputs).
- **The "impossible defense" framing (Section 5.6) is a legal argument, not a validation claim, and should be clearly labelled as such.** Section 5.6 argues that algorithmic redistricting constitutes the "impossibility defense" --- the algorithm cannot gerrymander because it cannot access partisan data. This is a legal argument about legislative intent, not an empirical validation claim. It belongs in a policy implications section, not in a section labeled "partisan fairness as a validation criterion." Conflating the validation argument (the algorithm produces low EG by design) with the legal argument (the algorithm cannot produce high EG by design) muddies both claims.

## P2 Items (Suggestions)

- **Provide a formal definition of "algorithmic validity" in the introduction.** The paper uses "valid" and "validated" throughout without defining what it means for an algorithm to be validated in the redistricting context. A brief formal definition --- perhaps "an algorithm is validated for a property P if it demonstrates P empirically across the full range of inputs for which it is claimed to satisfy P" --- would sharpen the paper's contribution and provide a standard against which the C-track evidence can be assessed.
- **Consider whether the four properties are exhaustive or whether a fifth property is needed.** The paper validates spatial robustness, temporal stability, demographic neutrality, and partisan neutrality. Absent from this list is contiguity stability --- the property that all districts produced by the algorithm are contiguous (no islands). This is a legal requirement and a potential failure mode for METIS (the graph partitioner does not guarantee contiguity). If contiguity is handled in B.x papers, a sentence acknowledging this would complete the validation portfolio.

## Score: 3 — Minor Revision

The paper makes a genuine contribution in framing validation as a multi-property empirical program rather than a single-criterion test. The three P1 items address real methodological gaps: the unvalidated property chain, the unacknowledged EG limitations, and the legal/validation conflation. These are conceptual fixes that do not require additional empirical work.
