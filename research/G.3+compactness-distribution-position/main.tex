\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Courts have long treated compactness as a proxy for non-gerrymandering:
a compact plan is presumed to reflect geographic rather than political logic.
But compactness is not neutral---in geographically sorted states, more compact
plans tend to produce more Republican seats.  This paper situates the \ar{}
plan in the compactness distribution of the \recom{} ensemble, finding that
the plan occupies the $61$st--$72$nd percentile range across six states:
more compact than average but not at the extreme.  We document the right-skewed
shape of the \recom{} compactness distribution, explain why the \ar{} plan
is not at the maximum (the METIS objective minimises edge cuts, not
Polsby-Popper directly), examine the compactness-partisan correlation within
the ensemble (higher compactness tends to favor Republicans in sorted states---
the Rodden effect), and argue that the \ar{} plan's compactness is legally
defensible precisely because it is not at the extreme of any distribution.
\end{abstract}
