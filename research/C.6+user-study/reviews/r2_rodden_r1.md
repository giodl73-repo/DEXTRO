# Review 2 — Jonathan Rodden (Political Science / Electoral Geography Lens)
**Paper**: C.6 — Public Perceptions of Algorithmic vs. Human-Drawn Congressional Districts: A Survey Experiment
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with Minor Revisions)

## Summary

This is a well-designed and well-executed survey experiment that makes a genuine contribution to the redistricting opinion literature. The finding that algorithmic maps are perceived as substantially fairer than enacted maps — with the gap surviving across partisan subgroups and amplified by process information — is important for understanding the political feasibility of reform. The paper is clearly written and the pre-registered design is a genuine methodological strength. I have concerns about several political science specifics that should be addressed in revision.

## Strengths

The study design is sophisticated in its handling of partisan symmetry. Showing respondents maps from two states — one with a Republican-favoring gerrymander (North Carolina) and one with a Democratic-favoring gerrymander (Maryland) — is an elegant way to prevent partisanship from serving as a pure response proxy. Each respondent has a reason to favor the algorithmic alternative regardless of party, which may explain the near-partisan-symmetry in results. This design choice is sound and should be emphasized more prominently.

The operationalization of "in-party" versus "out-party" status is one of the paper's methodological highlights. The finding that even in-party respondents (those whose party benefits from the enacted map) rate algorithmic maps 0.29 SD higher than enacted maps is remarkable — it suggests that the perceived legitimacy of algorithmic redistricting extends beyond instrumental self-interest.

The secondary outcomes support the primary finding: algorithmic maps are perceived as neutral by 64% of respondents (vs. 31% for enacted maps), process trust is substantially higher, and willingness to accept the map even under adverse conditions is strong. This convergent evidence from multiple operationalizations of legitimacy makes the findings more credible.

## Concerns

**The two-state design limits generalizability more than the paper acknowledges.** North Carolina and Maryland are both high-salience gerrymandering cases that have received extensive news coverage and litigation attention. Survey respondents — especially the politically engaged MTurk sample — are more likely than average Americans to have pre-existing negative opinions about the enacted maps in these states. This selection of states may inflate the baseline disadvantage of enacted maps (the reference category), making the algorithmic advantage appear larger than it would in states with less prominent gerrymandering histories. The paper notes this limitation in the discussion but does not adequately quantify its implications. A sensitivity analysis using only Lucid respondents who report low prior awareness of North Carolina and Maryland redistricting would help.

**Partisan moderation effect is substantively important but statistically fragile.** The 0.18 SD difference between strong Democrats and strong Republicans (p=0.04) is the paper's third headline finding. However, p=0.04 is not far from the conventional significance threshold, and the paper does not report whether this interaction was pre-specified with adequate power. The corresponding in-party/out-party contrast (0.19 SD, p=0.01) is somewhat more robust. The discussion of partisan moderation would benefit from Bayesian or equivalence-testing framing: the paper should ask whether the partisan gap is small enough to be practically irrelevant (i.e., is there adequate evidence for near-equivalence?) rather than just whether it reaches significance.

**The "algorithm equals commission" finding deserves more theoretical development.** The null result on H4 — no significant difference between algorithmic and commission-drawn maps in perceived fairness — is arguably the most policy-relevant finding in the paper. But the theoretical account of why this equivalence holds is underdeveloped. The paper suggests that "what matters is that the process is not legislative control." This is plausible but not fully examined. Were commission maps in the experiment actually drawn more fairly (by compactness or partisan-neutrality metrics) than the enacted maps? If commission maps are also substantially better than enacted maps on objective criteria, the equivalence finding may reflect that respondents perceive them as similar in quality, not merely similar in process type.

**Elite cueing is mentioned but not addressed.** The paper notes that partisan elite opinion may differ from mass opinion, and that elite politicians who benefit from gerrymandering may oppose reform in ways that shift mass opinion through partisan cueing. This concern is real. The existing political science literature (Zaller 1992, Levendusky 2009) suggests that elite-led partisan polarization can move mass opinion substantially on salient political issues. The paper does not test whether elite partisan framing of algorithmic redistricting as "rigged by computers" would reduce its perceived legitimacy. This is a significant external validity concern for the paper's policy conclusions.

**MTurk convenience sample issue.** The paper acknowledges that MTurk respondents are more educated and politically engaged than the general population, and reports that MTurk and Lucid results did not diverge by more than 0.30 SD (the pre-registered threshold). However, the paper does not report the actual within-study cross-platform effect size differences. Reporting these would allow readers to calibrate whether the pooled results are dominated by the more engaged MTurk sample or whether the Lucid quota sample yields substantively similar results.

## Minor Issues

The discussion's explanation for why strong Republicans show positive evaluations of algorithmic maps ("because both states include a map that disadvantages them") is compelling but somewhat post-hoc. The pre-registration should have specified that the two-state design was intended to produce this symmetry, if that was the design intention.

The conclusion recommends "pairing algorithmic methods with existing commission structures" as the most politically viable path. This is a reasonable policy recommendation but goes beyond what the experiment directly tests. The experiment does not vary the implementing institution; it varies map type. Whether algorithm-plus-commission is better received than algorithm-alone is an empirical question the current data cannot answer.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The study makes a genuine empirical contribution. Required revisions: (1) sensitivity analysis for high-awareness respondents; (2) explicit reporting of cross-platform effect size differences; (3) clearer discussion of the theoretical mechanism behind algorithmic-commission equivalence; (4) more careful treatment of the partisan moderation finding's statistical fragility.
