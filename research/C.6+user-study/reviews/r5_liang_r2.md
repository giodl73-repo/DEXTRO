# Review 5 — Leah Liang (Experimental Methods / Survey Design Lens)
**Paper**: C.6 — Public Perceptions of Algorithmic vs. Human-Drawn Congressional Districts: A Survey Experiment
**Round**: R2
**Score**: 4/4 (Accept)

## Summary

As lead reviewer on the two critical P1 issues (power adequacy and H2 testability), I confirm that both are now adequately addressed. The treatment text is reproduced in Section 3. The four remaining Priority 4 methodological issues (Stage 4 sequencing, viewing time distributions, standardization procedure, and Likert item validity in the algorithmic condition) are unaddressed, but these are not structural concerns that prevent acceptance. I recommend acceptance.

## Response to Round 1 Issues

**P1-A (Power adequacy — LEAD).** The revision addresses this issue at both levels where it needed to be addressed: in the hypothesis statement and in the results. H3 is now labeled ``Exploratory'' with an explicit minimum detectable effect statement ($\geq 0.36$ SD at 80\% power). Section 5.3 adds the caveat directly in the results: ``we caution against over-interpreting this modest partisan moderation effect before replication with adequate power.'' The two-sentence caveat is appropriately calibrated — it does not retract the finding (the $0.18$ SD difference is real and statistically significant at $p = 0.04$) but contextualizes it against the detection threshold. This is exactly the right treatment of an underpowered exploratory analysis. Resolved.

**P1-B (H2 testability — LEAD).** The revised H2 is now coherently testable from the existing design. The equivalence test between algorithmic and commission maps is directly supported by Table 2 ($\Delta = 0.03$ SD, $p = 0.68$). The transparency claim is supported by the interaction terms. The ``but not enacted maps'' clause, which was un-testable because there is no enacted × information condition in the design, is removed. The revision correctly identifies that the main effect of the process description variable ($\hat{\beta} = 0.08$, $p = 0.17$) is not an estimate of the information effect on enacted maps specifically, but rather the main effect of process description across the reference condition. Resolved.

**P1-C (Treatment text).** Figure 2 reproduces both treatment texts. From a survey design perspective, both texts are appropriately constructed: they are brief (around 90 words each), use plain language accessible to a general audience, and emphasize procedural properties rather than outcome claims. The parallel structure between the algorithmic and commission texts prevents differential demand effects from asymmetric length or tone. Resolved.

## Residual Concerns (Not Blocking)

**P4-A (Stage 4 sequencing).** The paper still does not specify the exact order of Stage 4 items. If the manipulation check question (``were the maps drawn by a computer program, a commission of citizens, or state legislators?'') appears before debriefing, respondents in the no-information condition may infer the experimental conditions from the response options. This is a potential demand artifact, but it affects only post-outcome measures (Stage 4 follows Stage 3 outcome measurement), so it cannot contaminate the primary results. A clarifying sentence would be sufficient.

**P4-B (Viewing time distributions).** Actual viewing time distributions are still not reported. The 30-second minimum is low for geographic map evaluation. Without the distributions, readers cannot assess whether inattentive rushing exceeded what the attention-check exclusions capture. A supplementary table or footnote reporting mean, SD, and percent at-minimum for each condition would strengthen the robustness claims.

**P4-C (Standardization procedure).** The paper still does not specify exactly how the primary outcome was standardized — whether at the respondent level or the respondent-map observation level. This should be one sentence in Section 4.1.

**P4-D (Likert item validity in algorithmic condition).** Item 2 (``the people who drew these districts treated different communities equally'') refers to ``people'' in a condition where no people drew the districts. This is a scale construct validity issue in the algorithmic condition. Given the high α ($= 0.87$) and CFA fit, the item probably does not substantially compromise the scale, but a footnote noting this potential issue and reporting whether item 2 shows atypical response patterns in the algorithmic condition would address the concern.

## Recommendation

Accept. The two P1 issues for which I was lead reviewer are resolved correctly. The P4 methodological concerns are real but do not threaten the paper's core findings or the validity of its primary conclusions.
