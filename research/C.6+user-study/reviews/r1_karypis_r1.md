# Review 1 — George Karypis (Algorithmic / Computer Science Lens)
**Paper**: C.6 — Public Perceptions of Algorithmic vs. Human-Drawn Congressional Districts: A Survey Experiment
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with Minor Revisions)

## Summary

This paper reports a pre-registered 2×3 survey experiment (N=2,400) examining public perceptions of algorithmic, commission-drawn, and enacted congressional district maps. The primary result — that algorithmic maps are perceived 0.41 SD fairer than enacted maps, and that this advantage grows to 0.63 SD with process description — is well-executed experimentally and scientifically credible. From a computer science and algorithmic framing perspective, there are several points that require tightening before this paper is fully ready for publication.

## Strengths

The algorithmic framing is clearly stated: the paper correctly emphasizes that the algorithm's structural inability to access partisan data is a mathematical property, not a normative aspiration. This is an important and accurate distinction. The reference to the open-source recursive bisection method grounded in the METIS graph partitioner is appropriate and gives technically literate readers a clear picture of what "algorithmic redistricting" means in this context.

The experimental stimuli are appropriately grounded. Using maps generated by an actual algorithmic procedure rather than hypothetical or artist-rendered maps is methodologically important — it means subjects are responding to the genuine outputs of the algorithm, not an idealized abstraction. The visual design protocol (consistent color, no partisan overlays, professional rendering) is well-specified.

The manipulation check results (89.4% correct identification in information conditions) are strong and appropriately reported.

## Concerns

**Algorithmic definition under-specified for replication.** The paper refers to the "recursive bisection algorithm described in della Libera (2026)" for the map stimuli, but readers cannot evaluate the fairness experiment without knowing what parameters were used. The population tolerance (±0.5%?), the edge-weighting scheme, the specific METIS configuration, the random seed — all of these affect map outputs. A technical appendix specifying algorithm parameters used for stimulus generation is necessary for full scientific transparency. The paper correctly notes that reproducibility is a key property of algorithmic redistricting; the paper itself should be reproducible in this respect.

**Commission maps need equivalent specification.** The paper uses "the final plans adopted by the North Carolina Bipartisan Redistricting Committee (2022) and the Maryland Citizens Redistricting Commission (2022)" as commission-drawn stimuli. These are real plans; however, the paper does not report how they were selected, whether alternative commission plans existed for either state, and whether the selected plans are representative. Treating the commission condition as internally homogeneous when there may be substantial variation in commission outputs introduces a form of stimuli selection bias.

**The comparison condition is structurally asymmetric.** The design table (Table 1) shows that enacted maps appear only in the "None" information condition — there are no Enacted × Process Description or Enacted × Full Description cells. This is explained as avoiding "directing attention to the maps' provenance in ways that confound treatment effects," but it means the information treatment effects for enacted maps cannot be estimated. A reader cannot tell whether providing process information about enacted maps would have increased or decreased fairness ratings, which matters for understanding whether the transparency effect is specific to algorithmic and commission processes or is a general information-about-process effect. The framing of H2 ("A plain-language description of the algorithmic process will increase perceived fairness of algorithmic maps, but not enacted maps") cannot be directly tested as stated.

**N=400 per cell and moderation analysis power.** The paper reports a partisan moderation effect of 0.18 SD (p=0.04) between strong Democrats and strong Republicans. The pre-registration specifies a minimum detectable effect of d=0.20 for the primary outcome at 80% power with N=394 per cell. The moderation comparison, however, is not between full cells — it compares partisan subgroups within cells. If strong Democrats constitute ~37% of a cell (N≈148) and strong Republicans ~31% (N≈124), the subgroup N is approximately 148 vs. 124. Detecting a 0.18 SD difference with these Ns at 80% power requires the test to have been pre-specified with appropriate subgroup power calculations. The paper should confirm whether this moderation test was pre-powered or is exploratory.

**Algorithm aversion literature integration.** The paper correctly cites Dietvorst et al. (2015) but does not engage with the distinction between algorithm aversion in error-observation contexts (watching an algorithm fail) and algorithm aversion in evaluation contexts (rating outputs without observing errors). The Dietvorst paradigm involves subjects watching the algorithm underperform; subjects in this experiment have no basis for forming error-based aversion. This difference should be discussed more explicitly — the absence of aversion here may be partially explained by the absence of error exposure, not just by the characteristics of the redistricting domain.

## Minor Issues

The description of the algorithmic process provided to subjects (90 words) is not reproduced in the paper. The manipulation check shows 89.4% recognition, but without knowing what subjects were told, readers cannot assess whether the treatment accurately represents the algorithm's properties.

The OSF pre-registration URL is redacted ("[redacted]"). While understandable for blind review, this should be restored before publication.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The core experimental design and results are solid. The primary revision requirements are: (1) technical appendix specifying stimulus generation parameters; (2) explicit discussion of the power adequacy of the partisan moderation test; (3) reproduction of the process-description treatment text; (4) more precise engagement with why the Dietvorst error-exposure paradigm does not apply here.
