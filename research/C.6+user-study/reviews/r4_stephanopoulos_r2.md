# Review 4 — Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law / Policy Implications Lens)
**Paper**: C.6 — Public Perceptions of Algorithmic vs. Human-Drawn Congressional Districts: A Survey Experiment
**Round**: R2
**Score**: 4/4 (Accept)

## Summary

The three Priority 1 revisions are adequate. The treatment text is now reproduced in Section 3. H2 is reformulated to be testable. H3 is correctly demoted to exploratory with an explicit power statement. My Round 1 concerns about legal precision (Rucho characterization), commission condition accuracy, and voice-in-process were Priority 3 issues and remain largely unaddressed, but these are not blocking. I am recommending acceptance.

## Response to Round 1 Issues

**P1-A (Power adequacy).** The power statement in Section 5.3 is appropriate and the exploratory label in H3 is correct. From an election law perspective, the key policy claim is that the partisan gap is small enough not to be a barrier to reform — and the framing now correctly hedges this claim by noting that the $0.18$ SD effect is below the minimum detectable effect for the subgroup comparisons. This is the right posture: the paper reports what it finds without over-claiming.

**P1-B (H2 testability).** The revised H2 is well-suited to the design. Testing equivalence between algorithmic and commission maps at baseline is directly supported by the no-information condition data. The transparency claim is tested by the interaction terms. The revision removes the design inconsistency that prevented direct testing of the original H2.

**P1-C (Treatment text).** Figure 2 includes the full text for both the algorithmic and commission process-description conditions. For election law purposes, the most important aspect of this text is the statement that ``the algorithm uses no information about voters' political preferences or racial identity'' — this directly addresses the core constitutional concern under *Rucho* and *Callais*. The fact that respondents who received this text rated algorithmic maps $0.22$ SD more fair is a meaningful empirical result with direct policy relevance.

## Residual Concerns (Not Blocking)

**P3-D (Rucho characterization).** The introduction still states that Rucho ``removed the federal judiciary as a check on partisan gerrymandering.'' This remains imprecise. *Rucho* removed the federal judiciary as a check on federal constitutional claims, but preserved state court remedies and explicitly invited congressional action. This distinction matters because the paper's policy recommendations build on the post-*Rucho* landscape. I would prefer a correction, but I will not condition acceptance on it.

**P3-C (Commission condition legal specificity).** The North Carolina ``Bipartisan Redistricting Committee (2022)'' was a legislative committee, not an independent citizen commission. This factual characterization is still not corrected. A footnote noting this distinction and clarifying that the paper's ``commission'' condition operationalizes ``non-legislative process reform'' rather than ``independent citizen commission'' specifically would be appropriate. Again, not blocking.

**P3-E (Voice-in-process).** The discussion continues to note that algorithmic redistricting eliminates the ``voice'' component of procedural fairness without testing whether this affects perceived legitimacy. The paper correctly identifies this as a limitation. A recommendation for future research explicitly targeting voice-deprivation effects would be a useful addition, but is not required.

## Recommendation

Accept. The P1 structural issues are resolved. The paper is a significant empirical contribution to the understanding of public legitimacy in redistricting reform.
