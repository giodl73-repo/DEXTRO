# Review 4 — Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law / Policy Implications Lens)
**Paper**: C.6 — Public Perceptions of Algorithmic vs. Human-Drawn Congressional Districts: A Survey Experiment
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4 (Accept with Minor Revisions)

## Summary

This paper provides the first large-scale pre-registered experimental evidence on public perceptions of algorithmic redistricting. From an election law and policy perspective, the findings are significant and the study design is sophisticated. I am recommending acceptance with revisions focused on the legal and policy implications sections, which are somewhat underdeveloped given the substantial policy claims the paper makes. The experimental findings themselves are credible and well-reported.

## Strengths

The study is timely and fills a genuine gap. The redistricting reform literature has extensive theoretical and doctrinal work, but very little experimental evidence on whether the public would accept algorithmic redistricting as legitimate. The paper correctly identifies this as the key political feasibility question: technical merit does not translate into political viability without public legitimacy.

The research design reflects a sophisticated understanding of the reform landscape. The decision to compare algorithmic maps against both commission-drawn maps and enacted maps — rather than just one comparator — allows the paper to situate algorithmic redistricting within the existing reform ecosystem. The finding that the public perceives algorithmic and commission-drawn maps as equivalently legitimate is perhaps the most important result for election law purposes.

The pre-registration and multi-platform recruitment strategy strengthen the paper's credibility substantially. Survey experiments in this domain have historically suffered from convenience samples and undisclosed analysis flexibility; this paper's methodological rigor is commendable.

## Concerns

**The policy implications overreach what the experiment can support.** The conclusion recommends that reform advocates "invest in communication infrastructure" and "pair algorithmic methods with existing commission structures." These are reasonable recommendations, but they go beyond what the experimental findings directly support. The experiment tests whether survey respondents rate maps as fair in a hypothetical evaluation context; it does not test whether communication campaigns would shift public opinion, whether algorithmic redistricting would survive political opposition in a real legislative context, or whether the algorithm-plus-commission hybrid would be perceived differently than either alone. The conclusion should be more careful to distinguish what the findings show from what policy might follow from them.

**The legal-political context is described imprecisely in places.** The introduction states that "the Supreme Court's decision in Rucho v. Common Cause (2019) removed the federal judiciary as a check on partisan gerrymandering." This is not quite right: Rucho held that federal courts lack judicially manageable standards for partisan gerrymandering claims under the federal Constitution, but it explicitly preserved state court remedies under state constitutions and identified federal legislation as a pathway. The paper should correct this characterization; an accurate statement of what Rucho does and does not do matters because several of the paper's policy claims hinge on the correct understanding of post-Rucho reform options.

**The commission condition needs more legal specificity.** The paper uses "the final plans adopted by the North Carolina Bipartisan Redistricting Committee (2022) and the Maryland Citizens Redistricting Commission (2022)" as commission-drawn stimuli. In reality, North Carolina does not have a permanent independent redistricting commission for congressional districts — the "Bipartisan Redistricting Committee" was a legislative committee convened under court order, not a citizen commission analogous to those in California or Arizona. This factual imprecision may not affect the experimental results, but it matters for the policy discussion: the paper treats "commission-drawn" as a unified category when in reality there is substantial variation in commission independence, authority, and design across states. A paper making recommendations about commission-plus-algorithm hybrids should grapple with this variation.

**The discussion undersells the normative debate about algorithmic redistricting criteria.** The paper frames algorithmic redistricting as optimizing "objective criteria" (population equality, contiguity, compactness) and contrasts this with "partisan" map-drawing. But there is a substantial normative debate in the redistricting literature — including Stephanopoulos and McGhee (2015), McGhee (2020), and the gerrymandering literature more broadly — about whether compactness-only optimization is normatively appropriate. Compactness optimization tends to dilute urban concentration; it can produce outcomes that disadvantage political parties whose supporters are geographically concentrated in cities. The paper acknowledges that algorithmic maps "may still correlate with partisan advantage" due to geographic sorting, but does not engage with the normative question of whether this correlation is problematic. Readers from the election law field will expect this engagement.

**The voice-in-process concern deserves more direct treatment.** Section 2 correctly notes that algorithmic redistricting's structural elimination of partisan data also eliminates most opportunities for public input into map design. The procedural fairness literature (Tyler 2006) identifies voice — the ability to participate in the process — as a central legitimacy criterion. But the paper does not test whether respondents who are told the algorithm did not incorporate public input rate it as less fair. The adjustment process described in the companion legal paper (D.4) addresses this concern institutionally, but the experimental design does not test whether voice-deprivation reduces the algorithm's perceived legitimacy.

## Minor Issues

The paper's open-ended question ("what, if anything, would you want to change about how congressional districts are drawn?") appears in Stage 4 but its results are not reported anywhere. If this generated policy-relevant qualitative data, even a brief summary would be valuable.

The conclusion states that "68% of respondents supported commission redistricting." This figure is not directly derived from the experiment (which compared maps, not processes); it appears to come from the post-treatment support question. Its provenance and method of measurement should be clarified.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The experimental contribution is solid and the findings are policy-relevant. Required revisions: (1) correct the characterization of Rucho; (2) add legal specificity to the commission condition description; (3) engage with the normative debate about compactness-only optimization; (4) temper the policy conclusions to match what the experiment actually demonstrates.
