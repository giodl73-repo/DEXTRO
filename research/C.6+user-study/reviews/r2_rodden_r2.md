# Review 2 — Jonathan Rodden (Political Science / Electoral Geography Lens)
**Paper**: C.6 — Public Perceptions of Algorithmic vs. Human-Drawn Congressional Districts: A Survey Experiment
**Round**: R2
**Score**: 4/4 (Accept)

## Summary

The revision resolves the three Priority 1 methodological concerns identified across the panel in Round 1. My own Priority 3 concerns (sensitivity analysis for high-awareness respondents, reporting of cross-platform differences, and the theoretical account of algorithmic-commission equivalence) were not P1 items and remain largely unaddressed, but these do not prevent acceptance. The paper is ready for publication.

## Response to Round 1 Issues

**P1-A (Power adequacy — addressed).** The H3 reframing is handled correctly. The exploratory label in Section 2.4 and the power caveat in Section 5.3 together give readers the information they need to interpret the partisan moderation analysis appropriately. The statement that ``the $0.18$ SD estimate should be treated as descriptive rather than confirmatory'' is exactly right. The caveat in Section 5.3 is placed where it matters — in the results, not relegated to a footnote or limitations section. This addresses my Round 1 concern that the paper presented an underpowered test as confirmatory evidence for H3.

**P1-B (H2 testability — addressed).** The revised H2 is now testable: the equivalence test between algorithmic and commission maps at baseline is directly supported by the Table 2 estimates ($\Delta = 0.03$ SD, $p = 0.68$), and the claim about process description increasing fairness for algorithmic and commission maps relative to the enacted baseline is clearly supported by the interaction terms. The revision removes the un-testable ``but not enacted maps'' clause. Resolved.

**P1-C (Treatment text — addressed).** Figure 2 reproduces both treatment texts. The algorithmic text is plain-language and neutral: it does not advocate for algorithmic redistricting, it simply explains the process. A political scientist reading this can now assess whether the treatment would plausibly produce the measured effects — the answer is yes, this is a reasonable process description that emphasizes neutrality and reproducibility without overclaiming.

## Residual Concerns (Not Blocking)

**P3-A (Sensitivity analysis for high-awareness respondents).** This remains undone. I understand it may not be feasible if the high-awareness screener was not embedded in the survey, but the paper should at least acknowledge more quantitatively that North Carolina and Maryland are high-salience cases. A sentence noting that the effect may be larger than it would be in lower-salience gerrymandering states, with a reference to the limitation already in Section 6.4, would suffice.

**P3-B (Cross-platform differences).** The paper reports that MTurk and Lucid did not diverge by more than 0.30 SD but still does not report the actual point estimates by platform. This is a transparent reporting gap. The paper could add a supplementary table or a parenthetical in Section 5.5 (Robustness) giving the platform-specific primary effect estimates. I would prefer to see this before final publication, but I will not condition acceptance on it.

**P3-C (Commission condition legal specificity).** The paper still describes North Carolina's commission as a citizen commission rather than a legislative committee. This is a factual imprecision, but it does not threaten the internal validity of the experiment or the primary findings.

## Recommendation

Accept. The three P1 structural issues are resolved. The paper makes a genuine empirical contribution to the redistricting opinion literature and should be published.
