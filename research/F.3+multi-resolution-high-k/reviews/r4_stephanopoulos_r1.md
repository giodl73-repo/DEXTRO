# Review 4 — Nicholas Stephanopoulos
**Paper**: F.3: Resolution Selection for State Legislative Redistricting: When Census Tracts Are Too Coarse
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4

## Summary

F.3 is primarily a methods paper, but the MAUP finding has legal significance: the paper establishes that resolution choice is a substantively political decision for high-k/n chambers (±2 seats per 98 districts). This has implications for whether resolution selection must be disclosed and specified in advance of any redistricting process claiming algorithmic neutrality.

## Strengths

The policy implication in Section 5.4 is the strongest legally relevant passage in the paper: "For algorithmic redistricting to be credibly non-partisan, the resolution selection rule must be stated in advance and applied consistently — which is precisely what the k/n > 0.05 rule provides." This is legally precise. It mirrors the procedural transparency requirements that courts have used to evaluate redistricting criteria: a criterion applied post-hoc (after seeing the partisan outcome) is suspect; a criterion stated in advance and applied mechanically is defensible.

## Concerns

**C1 — Legal significance of resolution as a "political decision" understated.** Section 5.4 notes that "a partisan redistricting commission that chose tract resolution for a high-k/n chamber would produce different partisan outcomes." This is a significant legal claim: it implies that resolution choice, in the absence of a pre-specified rule, could be used as a tool of partisan manipulation. In a state with a partisan redistricting commission, choosing tract instead of block-group resolution for WA House would yield 2 fewer Democratic seats. This is not merely a technical note — it is a finding about a potential manipulation vector. The paper should discuss whether this would be legally cognizable as partisan gerrymandering under state court doctrine.

**C2 — State laws governing redistricting methodology.** The paper does not discuss whether any state laws or regulations govern the choice of geographic resolution in redistricting. Some state statutes specify that redistricting must be performed using "census data" without specifying resolution. Others refer to "census tracts" explicitly. If a state statute requires redistricting at "census tract" resolution, the k/n > 0.05 rule would conflict with that requirement for high-k/n states. The paper should survey whether such specifications exist in the 12 block-group-resolution states.

**C3 — Expert witness applicability.** F.3's k/n > 0.05 rule is the kind of principled threshold that a redistricting expert witness might offer in litigation: "the standard practice is to use block-group resolution when k/n > 0.05, because [reasons in Section 3.1]." For this application, the paper needs to cite or acknowledge whether there is existing practice (in legislative or commission redistricting) of using block-group resolution, or whether this rule is entirely novel. If no other jurisdiction uses this rule, its status as a "standard" is undermined.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The methods contribution is clear. C1 (manipulation vector implication) should be addressed in the MAUP policy discussion; C3 (novelty of the rule) should be acknowledged.
