## Response to Revision

**C1 (Resolution choice as manipulation vector)** — Addressed. Section 5.4 now includes the paragraph I requested about the partisan neutrality implications of resolution choice: "The finding that resolution choice is a substantively political decision for high-k/n chambers has implications for any state redistricting law or administrative rule that does not pre-specify resolution. A redistricting commission that chose resolution post-hoc, after observing partisan outcomes at each resolution, would have engaged in partisan manipulation equivalent to other forms of boundary manipulation. Pre-specification of the k/n > 0.05 rule eliminates this manipulation vector." This is legally precise and important.

**C2 (State laws governing redistricting methodology)** — Not addressed. The paper still does not survey whether any of the 12 block-group-resolution states have statutes specifying "census tract" resolution in their redistricting laws. This is a practical legal gap that could affect whether the k/n > 0.05 rule is actionable in any given state.

**C3 (Expert witness applicability)** — Addressed. A note has been added acknowledging that the k/n > 0.05 rule is proposed in this paper and no prior jurisdiction is known to have formally adopted it. The paper correctly frames the rule as a principled proposal, not a pre-existing standard.

## Assessment

The manipulation vector paragraph is exactly what this paper needed from a legal perspective. I maintain 3/4.
