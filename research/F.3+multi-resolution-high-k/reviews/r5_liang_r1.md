# Review 5 — Christina Liang
**Paper**: F.3: Resolution Selection for State Legislative Redistricting: When Census Tracts Are Too Coarse
**Round**: R1
**Score**: 3/4

## Summary

F.3 is the methods paper for resolution selection. From a reproducibility perspective, this paper has the most concrete data requirements specification of any paper in the F track: it clearly distinguishes between states requiring block-group data (with download and build instructions) and states where tract data suffices. My review focuses on whether the described data requirements are met by the available infrastructure.

## Strengths

The data description (Section 1, resolution levels section) correctly characterises the three Census resolution levels: tract (~73,000 nationally), block group (~220,000), and block (~8,100,000). The population ranges are approximately correct: tracts 1,200-8,000 persons, block groups 600-3,000 persons, blocks 0-600 persons. These are consistent with the 2020 Census design targets.

The recommendation table approach (Section 7, previewed) is exactly the right format for reproducibility: a table mapping all 50 states and all lower chambers to a recommended resolution is independently checkable against TIGER data.

## Concerns

**C1 — Block-group adjacency data confirmed available for WA, TX, CA only.** The paper validates the k/n > 0.05 rule on three states: WA, TX, and CA. Only WA actually requires block-group data (k/n = 0.067 > 0.05). TX and CA use tract resolution in the recommended configuration. For reproducibility of the paper's primary results, only WA block-group adjacency data is strictly needed. This is more achievable than the 12-state requirement in F.1. The paper should state this explicitly: "The empirical results in this paper require block-group adjacency data for Washington state only; this file can be generated by [command]."

**C2 — MAUP analysis data source.** Section 5 uses "2020 presidential precinct returns interpolated to the relevant resolution level (Kuriwaki 2023)" for the MAUP analysis. The interpolation methodology — from precinct to census block group — is not described. Precinct-to-block-group interpolation requires areal interpolation or dasymetric mapping, which introduces assumptions about the distribution of votes within precincts. The direction of interpolation error is not random: in precincts that span census block group boundaries, votes are typically allocated proportionally to population, which may slightly overallocate votes to denser block groups (more Democratic in most states). This could contribute to the observed +2 Democratic seat MAUP effect. The paper should discuss the interpolation method and its potential direction of bias.

**C3 — Runtime table (Section 6) data source.** The paper presents runtimes (45s for WA tract, 180s for WA block-group, 95s for TX tract, 380s for TX block-group, 140s for CA tract, 520s for CA block-group). These are single-run measurements. The paper does not specify: the hardware (CPU type, RAM), whether these are single-threaded or multi-threaded runs, and what the measurement methodology is (wall-clock? CPU time?). Without this information, the runtime figures cannot be reproduced on different hardware.

**C4 — Recommendation table (Section 7) not shown.** The abstract and introduction promise a "resolution recommendation table for all 50 states and all lower chambers." This table is referenced in Sections 4 and 7 but is not shown in the paper text (it may be in a separate appendix that was not provided for review). This table is the paper's most practically useful output and should be included in the main paper or as a numbered appendix.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The methods are sound and the reproducibility situation is better than in F.1. C1 (scope of block-group data requirement) and C4 (missing recommendation table) should be addressed before final submission.
