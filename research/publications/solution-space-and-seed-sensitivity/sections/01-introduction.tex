\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The proposed Districting Integrity Act (\S~107(c)(1)) publishes a single random
seed that all states use as input to the METIS graph-partitioning algorithm.
This seed is the statute's reproducibility guarantee: anyone who runs the
reference implementation with the published seed and TIGER inputs gets the
identical map.

But METIS is a heuristic. Graph minimum $k$-bisection is NP-hard in general
\citep{garey1979computers}; METIS finds a local optimum via multilevel
coarsening and refinement, not the global minimum \citep{karypis1998fast}.
The seed controls which local optimum is reached. A well-prepared challenger
will argue that the published seed was chosen after its partisan consequences
were known --- or that a different seed would produce a materially different
partisan outcome.

This paper addresses that challenge empirically and theoretically. We ask three
questions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Approximation quality}: How close to the true minimum edge cut
        does METIS get in practice on U.S.\ state census-tract graphs?
  \item \textbf{Solution-space size}: How large is the set of plans within
        $\varepsilon\%$ of the minimum cut for a typical state?
  \item \textbf{Partisan variance}: Across 1{,}000 seeds per state, what is the
        distribution of partisan outcomes (proportionality gap, efficiency gap,
        seat allocation)?
\end{enumerate}

If the partisan variance across seeds is small (e.g., $\leq 2$ percentage
points in proportionality gap for all states), then the seed is procedurally
irrelevant --- any seed produces a politically equivalent map --- and the
statute's reproducibility design is fully defensible. If variance is large in
competitive states, the statute requires a seed-selection procedure that is
provably non-partisan.
