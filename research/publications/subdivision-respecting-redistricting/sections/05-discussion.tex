\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Choosing $\alpha$: The Pareto Frontier}
\label{sec:pareto}

The stickiness parameter $\alpha$ traces a Pareto frontier between two objectives:
minimising boundary length (compactness, $\alpha = 0$) and minimising county
splits (subdivision preservation, $\alpha \to \infty$).
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} plots county splits and the splits-vs-EC Pareto frontier
for five focal states across $\alpha \in \{0, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20\}$ (10 seeds each).
Table~\ref{tab:pareto} reports the underlying means.

The frontier has a characteristic shape: steep improvement from $\alpha = 0$ to
$\alpha \approx 2$ (most of the split reduction occurs here at modest EC cost),
followed by a plateau from $\alpha = 2$ to $\alpha = 10$ (diminishing split returns
at continued EC growth), and a long tail beyond $\alpha = 10$ where marginal splits
become structurally unavoidable (large counties that must be split for population balance).

\paragraph{Justification for $\alpha = 5$.}
We recommend $\alpha = 5$ as a default for the following reasons:
\begin{itemize}
  \item It lies past the steep portion of the curve (near the knee) for all focal states.
    At $\alpha = 5$, GA achieves 12.3 splits vs.\ 10.9 at $\alpha = 20$ --- only 1.4
    additional splits remain at three times the EC cost.
  \item Edge cut at $\alpha = 5$ is approximately 2--2.5$\times$ the MEC value,
    comparable to the increase accepted when using edge-weighted vs.\ unweighted bisection.
  \item The parameter is interpretable: an intra-county tract boundary is penalised
    $6\times$ relative to a cross-county boundary, reflecting a clear statutory preference.
  \item No partisan bias is introduced (§\ref{sec:partisan}).
\end{itemize}

Statutory language varies: California says ``respect the geographic integrity''
of subdivisions; North Carolina requires using whole counties wherever possible;
Florida requires utilising existing political and geographical boundaries where
feasible.
A redistricting body with a stricter statutory mandate could choose $\alpha = 10$
(reduces remaining splits by $\sim$5\% relative to $\alpha = 5$ at $\sim$2$\times$ additional EC cost).

\subsection{Why County Splits Are Not Zero at $\alpha = 5$}

Even with $\alpha = 5$, some county splits remain.
This is a consequence of the population balance constraint ($\pm 0.5\%$ for
congressional districts).
When a county has population well above the ideal district size (e.g.,
Los Angeles County, population 10M, contains about 15 congressional districts),
it must be split regardless of $\alpha$.
The floor on unavoidable splits is approximately:
\[
  \text{min\_splits}(\text{state}) \approx \sum_c \max(0, \lceil P_c / P^* \rceil - 1)
\]
where $P_c$ is county population and $P^*$ is the ideal district population.
At $\alpha = 5$, many states approach this theoretical minimum.

\subsection{Partisan Neutrality}

The absence of systematic partisan bias at $\alpha = 5$ is not obvious.
Counties in the United States are not politically neutral geographic units:
urban counties tend to vote Democratic, rural counties Republican.
One might expect that respecting county lines would ``pack'' urban Democratic
voters into fewer districts.

The data shows otherwise.
The reason is that METIS with $\alpha > 0$ does not simply consolidate counties;
it makes county-crossing cuts more expensive while still minimising total cut
weight subject to balance.
The algorithm finds the most compact county-respecting partition, which does
not systematically favour either party because county shapes and populations
are not themselves gerrymandered.

This is the key legal point: $\alpha$ operationalises a traditional criterion
that courts have consistently upheld as a legitimate redistricting objective,
and it does so without introducing partisan information.

\subsection{Extension to Finer Levels}

The framework extends to county subdivisions (townships, MCDs), incorporated
places (cities, towns), and voting tabulation districts (precincts).
Each level requires an additional TIGER shapefile download and a spatial join
to assign each census tract to its containing jurisdiction.
We defer this to future work; the county level alone (derivable from existing
data) produces the majority of the reduction in splits.

\subsection{Remaining Limitations}

\paragraph{50-state ensemble.}
The ensemble analysis (§\ref{sec:ensemble}) covers 7 focal states.
The 44-state Table~\ref{tab:splits} uses a single seed, so per-state values
carry seed variance of approximately $\pm 2$--$4$ splits for states with
$k \geq 10$ districts.
The \emph{aggregate} finding (45\% average reduction) is robust because the
seed variance is symmetric across states.

\paragraph{Minimum splits floor.}
Even at $\alpha \to \infty$, some county splits are unavoidable: any county with
population above the ideal district size must be split.
The theoretical minimum is $\text{min\_splits}(s) \approx \sum_c \max(0, \lceil P_c / P^* \rceil - 1)$.
At $\alpha = 20$, most focal states approach this floor, confirming that further
increase in $\alpha$ yields no meaningful improvement.

\paragraph{Finer-level hierarchy.}
MCD, place, and VTD stickiness are not evaluated here.
Their data requires additional TIGER downloads and spatial joins (§\ref{sec:method}).
We expect the same qualitative Pareto tradeoff to hold at each level, with
decreasing marginal gain as the hierarchy becomes finer.
