\title{Preserving Political Subdivisions in Algorithmic Redistricting:\\
       Governmental Hierarchy as Edge-Weight Signal}

\author{Gio Della-Libera}

\begin{abstract}
Most state redistricting statutes list ``preserving political subdivisions''
as an explicit criterion alongside compactness and population equality.
Yet no algorithmic redistricting system operationalizes this criterion
as a formal, tunable parameter.
We introduce \emph{subdivision-respecting redistricting}: a single real-valued
stickiness parameter $\alpha$ that makes intra-jurisdiction edges more expensive
to cut in the METIS graph bisection framework.
When $\alpha = 0$ the algorithm reduces to standard minimum-edge-cut bisection;
as $\alpha \to \infty$ the algorithm will only split a county when population
balance requires it.
We derive the edge-weight formula from first principles, show that county
affiliation is freely available from Census TIGER tract GEOIDs (no additional
data download), and present 50-state empirical results for $\alpha \in \{0, 5\}$.
At $\alpha = 5$, county splits fall by 45\% on average (14.3 $\to$ 7.8 per state)
at a 2.5$\times$ increase in total boundary length, with no systematic partisan effect
($+D$ in 13 states, $-D$ in 8, unchanged in 23).
The stickiness parameter is a single published number that fully determines
the algorithm's behavior and can be independently verified from TIGER data.
\end{abstract}
