## Summary

This paper establishes T=600 as the statutory stopping criterion for the ConvergenceSweep algorithm and provides the SHA-256 content-derived seed formula for the proposed Districting Integrity Act. As a legal scholar, I am primarily interested in whether this paper closes the litigation vulnerabilities in the DIA that a well-prepared opponent would exploit. The paper does this well, and the resulting Certificate of Seed Neutrality (Section 4.4) is a genuine legal innovation that no prior redistricting proposal has offered.

## Strengths

- **The Certificate of Seed Neutrality is a genuinely useful litigation artifact.** The structured statement — "This value was not known to any party until the Census Bureau published the dataset identifier... No human selected this seed... The partisan consequences of this seed were not knowable before that date" — is a clean, court-ready affidavit paragraph that closes the seed-manipulation litigation avenue. No prior redistricting algorithm has been able to make this certificate, because prior proposals used fixed integers or operator-chosen seeds. This is the paper's most valuable contribution from a legal standpoint.
- **The distinction between "the algorithm happens to return the correct plan" and "the algorithm certifiably returns the optimal plan" is exactly the right legal standard.** Courts do not accept expert testimony that says "our method was probably correct." They require certification procedures. The paper's analysis of the Georgia T=500 failure mode — the plan returned is the global minimum, but the algorithm cannot certify it because it halted 11 seeds early — correctly identifies the legal problem: the map is right but the process is not certifiable. T=600 closes this gap.
- **The statutory text recommendation (Section 5.4) is complete and correctly integrated with the DIA.** Providing the exact proposed statutory language for §2a(c)(2)(A)-(C) — including the seed computation, the convergence criterion, and the enforcement clause for legal-effect maps — gives Congress something to enact rather than a design specification. The clause that "values below 600... shall not be used to generate maps with legal effect" is the right enforcement mechanism.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The paper does not address the "EAC administrative process to raise T_stat" mentioned in Section 6.1 (Open Questions) in the statutory text.** The conclusion mentions that "the statute provides for an administrative process to raise T_stat without full legislative amendment (see Section 5, clause (C))." But Section 5.4 clause (C) does not actually specify an administrative process — it says the implementation shall expose a `--convergence-threshold` flag and that values below 600 shall not generate legally effective maps, but it does not specify how T_stat can be raised administratively if the 2030 census produces a state with a tail exceeding 600. This is a real legislative gap: if Georgia-2030 has a tail of 650, what happens? The statute needs either (a) a clause authorising the EAC to raise T_stat by regulation without congressional action, subject to a public comment period, or (b) a clause requiring congressional action to raise T_stat, with an interim provision for states that cannot be certified at T=600.
- **The "version string necessity" argument (Section 4.3) does not address the versioning problem it creates.** The paper correctly argues that "DIA_SEED_V1" is needed for domain separation. But it also notes that "a future DIA amendment adopting a different formula would use 'DIA_SEED_V2'." This creates a legal complexity: if Congress amends the DIA in 2031 to use v2, maps generated in 2031 use a different seed than maps that would have been generated in 2021 under the same census data. The version string means that two different maps can both be "the DIA-compliant map" for the same census year under different statute versions. The paper should specify whether the version string is pinned to the apportionment cycle (v1 for 2020, v2 for 2030 if amended) or to the statute version, and whether maps generated under v1 and v2 can be compared in litigation.
- **The "24-hour statutory window" mentioned in Section 2.4 (Complexity) is inconsistent with the "30 days" specified elsewhere.** Section 2.4 states the full sweep for Texas takes "approximately 8.1 minutes — well within the statutory 24-hour window." But Section 5.1 of B.02 specifies "within 30 days of the release of the decennial census data" as the deadline for map submission. If the statutory deadline is 30 days (not 24 hours), the 24-hour comparison is addressing the wrong constraint. Either the DIA has a 24-hour computation window as a sub-requirement within the 30-day submission deadline (which should be stated in the statute), or the 24-hour comparison is simply the wrong benchmark.

## P2 Items (Suggested Improvements)

- **The litigation posture section (Section 4.4) should address what happens if an opponent runs the sweep with the same parameters and finds a lower EC_norm.** The Certificate of Seed Neutrality says "If you believe we have missed a better plan, run the sweep — with the same public seed formula and the same census data — and show us the lower edge cut." But the T_stat=600 algorithm is certified as finding the best plan seen in seeds s_0 through s_0+j_stop. If an opponent runs the same sweep and finds a lower EC_norm at seed s_0+650 (which T=600 would have missed), what is the legal consequence? The paper implies this is impossible under P(τ > 600) < 0.001, but it should address the legal procedure if it nevertheless occurs.
- **The audit trail (Section 5.3) should specify which fields in the depo_log.jsonl are mandatory for legal compliance vs. optional for research.** The log includes EC_norm at every seed tried — for Texas with 1,623 seeds, this is a 1,623-entry JSONL file. Courts reviewing plans do not need seed-level granularity; they need the starting seed, the winning seed, and the final EC_norm. Specifying which fields are legally required for the EAC submission would reduce the verification burden.

## Score: 4 / 4 — Accept

The paper's legal contributions — the Certificate of Seed Neutrality and the correct identification of T=500's certification failure — are the strongest procedural safeguards in the DIA package. The P1 items are important statutory clarifications (T_stat administrative update procedure, version string versioning, 24-hour vs. 30-day consistency) that should be addressed before publication, but they do not undermine the paper's central findings. This is the DIA's most legally complete paper.
