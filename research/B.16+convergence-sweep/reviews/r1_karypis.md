## Summary

This paper introduces the ConvergenceSweep algorithm for certifying that a METIS-based redistricting run has found a near-optimal minimum edge-cut partition, identifies T=600 as the statutory stopping criterion based on 50-state empirical data, and establishes the SHA-256 content-derived seed formula. As the developer of METIS, I find the paper's characterisation of METIS's convergence behaviour largely accurate and the algorithmic design sound. Several implementation details need correction and the Gumbel model choice requires better justification.

## Strengths

- **The paper correctly identifies that METIS is a heuristic and that a fixed seed does not certify optimality.** This is a point that redistricting researchers frequently overlook — they treat the METIS output as "the optimal partition" rather than "a locally optimal partition." The convergence sweep's design addresses this honestly: by running until T consecutive seeds produce no improvement, and taking the best-seen partition, the algorithm provides the strongest practical guarantee available for a heuristic. The paper does not overclaim; it certifies that T=600 suffices for 50-state congressional graphs under the 2020 census, not that the result is the global optimum.
- **The Proposition on Determinism (Proposition 2.1) is correctly stated and proved.** The proof correctly identifies the two sources of non-determinism that must be controlled: (a) the seed sequence (handled by the content-derived formula and sequential increment), and (b) floating-point non-associativity in METIS's refinement (handled by the `-fno-fast-math` compile flag). The remark about the vendored METIS with pinned source hash is exactly the right engineering control for a system whose outputs will be used in court.
- **Georgia as the worst-case state is well-characterised.** The finding that Georgia's convergence tail is 511 — specifically, the algorithm last improves at seed s_0+489 and then runs 511 consecutive non-improving seeds — is a concrete, verifiable empirical result. The comparison to Wisconsin (tail 377) and Florida (tail 329) situates Georgia in the distribution and explains why 14-district states with large metropolitan areas (Atlanta, Charlotte-Raleigh) have longer tails than simpler geographic structures.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The Finite Effective Seed Space theorem (Theorem 3.1) has an error in the proof sketch.** The theorem claims the number of distinct k-partitions of a planar graph is O(n^{2k}), based on: (a) a planar graph has O(n²) cuts and (b) a k-partition requires k-1 bisection cuts, so the number of ordered (k-1)-tuples is O(n²)^{k-1} = O(n^{2(k-1)}). This proof has two errors. First, the bound "O(n²) cuts in a planar graph via Euler formula" is the bound on the number of s-t cuts, not on the number of possible bisection cuts. The number of balanced k-way partitions of a planar graph is bounded by different arguments (the planar separator theorem gives O(n) separators of size O(√n), but balanced cuts are a subset of all cuts). Second, the conclusion "including the choice of root partition, the total is O(n^{2k})" adds one power of n^2 incorrectly — if the tree has k-1 levels and each level has O(n²) choices, the total is O(n^{2(k-1)}), not O(n^{2k}). The remark acknowledges the bound is not tight, but an incorrect proof sketch is worse than no proof. Either fix the proof or replace it with the correct statement (e.g., "the number of distinct balanced bisections of a planar graph is polynomial in n by the planar separator theorem, and therefore the METIS seed space is finite").
- **The Gumbel distribution is not motivated for convergence tails.** The paper states "the Gumbel distribution is the natural extreme-value model for the maximum of a sequence of i.i.d. random variables." But the 50 observed convergence tails are not i.i.d. samples from the same distribution — they are 50 observations from 50 different graphs (different states) with different n and k. The Gumbel extreme-value theory applies when fitting the distribution of the maximum of many samples from the same distribution. Fitting a Gumbel to the 50-state tail distribution and then computing P(τ > 600) for "any US state" treats the 50 observations as a random sample from the distribution of "US congressional graph convergence tails" — which requires the assumption that all future US congressional graphs are drawn from the same distribution as the 50 current states. This is an empirical assumption, not a consequence of extreme value theory. The paper must either (a) justify why the Gumbel family is appropriate for the tail distribution of congressional graph convergence (providing a generative model or an empirical goodness-of-fit test), or (b) use a non-parametric approach (e.g., empirical worst-case plus a safety margin, as the paper already provides: T=600 with 89-seed margin above Georgia's 511).
- **The normalised edge-cut formula EC_norm(Π) = EC(Π) / sqrt(min(i, k-i)) is applied at the partition level but defined at the bisection level.** In Algorithm 1 (ConvergenceSweep), the comparison `e_j < EC*` uses EC_norm(Π_j) where Π_j is the full k-way partition. But the EC_norm formula includes `min(i, k-i)` where "i ranges over all bisection levels in the recursive tree." A full k-way partition produced by METIS in a single call (not via recursive bisection) does not have a recursive tree structure — it has one set of k parts with no hierarchical ordering. The paper needs to specify how EC_norm is computed for a full partition: is it the minimum over all possible bisection trees of the maximum EC_norm at any level? Or is it the EC of the full partition divided by sqrt(min(1, k-1)) = 1? This must be resolved because it affects how the sweep compares partitions generated by the convergence search.

## P2 Items (Suggested Improvements)

- **The practical threshold T_prac=500 "certifies 49 of 50 states" claim should include a computational cost comparison.** If T_stat=600 costs 20% more computation than T_prac=500 for Georgia-class states, practitioners will want to know this before choosing to use T_prac. Adding a column to Table 5 showing the seed count and runtime at T=500 vs. T=600 for the five worst-case states would make the T_prac recommendation more practically actionable.
- **The "block-level resolution" open question should be bounded.** The paper correctly identifies that block-level graphs (n ~ 1.5M) have not been tested. Since METIS complexity is O(m log n), a factor of 100× in n produces a factor of ~100× in per-seed time and an unknown factor in convergence tail. Even a theoretical argument about how the convergence tail scales with n (e.g., more complex graphs → more distinct local optima → potentially longer tails) would be useful for practitioners planning a block-level run.

## Score: 3 / 4 — Minor Revision

The paper's central empirical contribution — Georgia's 511-seed tail showing T=500 is insufficient, T=600 provides adequate margin — is clear and well-supported by the B.7 data. The Gumbel model issues (P1.2) undermine the theoretical certification but the empirical argument is strong on its own. The EC_norm definition gap (P1.3) must be resolved for the algorithm to be implementable as specified. The theorem proof error (P1.1) should be corrected to avoid embarrassment if cited in court. Revision is straightforward.
