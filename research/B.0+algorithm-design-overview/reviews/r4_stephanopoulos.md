## Summary

This paper provides a comprehensive algorithmic taxonomy for congressional redistricting and validates it against four legally contested states, with special attention to Callais compliance architecture. For a law professor working at the intersection of election law and algorithmic redistricting, this is the most operationally useful synthesis I have seen — it directly maps legal requirements to implementable parameters, which is what practitioners need. Several legal characterisations require correction, but the overall framework is sound.

## Strengths

- **The legal requirements taxonomy (R1–R11) is the most complete and accurate summary of the post-Callais redistricting landscape I have encountered in the technical literature.** Most CS papers on redistricting treat "population equality + compactness + contiguity" as the complete legal requirement set. This paper correctly identifies eight additional requirements — county preservation, VRA §2 Gingles Prong 1, racial non-predominance, state-court partisan neutrality, census stability, seed auditability, multi-chamber consistency, and HH apportionment linkage — and provides accurate legal citations for each. This taxonomy alone is a contribution.
- **The treatment of Callais p. 36 as an architectural constraint rather than a legal hurdle is exactly right.** The paper correctly observes that the disentanglement requirement does not prohibit either VRASection or ProportionalSection but prohibits combining them in the same run. Formalising this as a CLI mutex gate that produces an auditable manifest is the right systems-level response to a constitutional requirement. Courts need to be able to read a single document and determine which signals were used; the plan manifest design delivers this.
- **The strong-inference procedure (Section 6.4) — run GeoSection, run VRASection, compare — directly operationalises the Callais holding 2 framework for §2 challengers.** This is genuinely useful for litigation. The Alabama example (GeoSection: 0D/1MM; VRASection: 1D/2MM) provides a concrete demonstration that the state's stated political goals (compact, population-balanced districts) could have been achieved with better minority outcomes. This is exactly what a §2 plaintiff needs to show post-Callais, and the toolbox provides the computational infrastructure to generate it.

## Weaknesses / P1 Items (Required Fixes)

- **The characterisation of Callais at p. 36 as a "majority holding" needs to be verified and cited more precisely.** The paper repeatedly cites "Callais p. 36, the majority" as establishing the disentanglement requirement. The actual opinion is cited as "608 U.S. ___ (2026)" — a slip-opinion citation with no paragraph or section number. The "p. 36" reference is to the slip opinion's page 36, which is a format-dependent citation that will change when the official U.S. Reports volume is published. More importantly, the paper should specify whether p. 36 reflects the majority opinion, a concurrence, or a plurality — this matters enormously for stare decisis purposes. If Callais's disentanglement language is a plurality opinion rather than a majority, the constitutional architecture built on it is far shakier. This needs to be resolved and the opinion structure clarified throughout the paper.
- **The paper's treatment of R7 (Partisan Neutrality) is legally incomplete.** The paper characterises state-court partisan neutrality standards through LWV v. Pennsylvania, Harper v. Hall, and Harkenrider, but does not engage with the specific legal standards those courts applied. Pennsylvania's "free and equal elections" clause standard, North Carolina's initial Harper holding (later vacated by the new NC Supreme Court after the 2022 election changed its composition), and New York's outcome-based standard are materially different. The paper's claim that AreaSection's Rodden-null proof satisfies R7 needs to be qualified: it satisfies the Pennsylvania-style process claim (the algorithm has no partisan inputs) but may not satisfy an outcome-based proportionality standard. A table mapping the three state standards to what the toolbox does and does not provide would resolve this ambiguity.
- **The county-preservation discussion (R4, and the B10 alpha parameter) does not engage with the legal standards courts have used to evaluate county splits.** North Carolina's Stephenson v. Bartlett established a county-grouping requirement that goes beyond the general "preserving political subdivisions" preference — it mandates a specific algorithm for grouping counties before drawing districts. Iowa Code §42.4 specifies a sequential criterion (compactness, then population equality, then county lines). The paper treats all county-preservation requirements as equivalent to a soft edge-weight parameter, but Stephenson-style requirements are hard constraints (no county split allowed in groups of fewer than a certain size). The alpha parameter cannot satisfy a hard constraint; the paper needs to acknowledge this and specify which county-preservation legal contexts the soft-weight approach does and does not handle.

## P2 Items (Suggested Improvements)

- **The paper should engage with the redistricting commission context.** Seventeen states use independent redistricting commissions, and the toolbox is positioned as a tool for commissions and courts (not just for expert witnesses). But commissions have deliberative mandates — they are required to hold public hearings, consider community of interest submissions, and apply criteria in a statutory priority order. The toolbox's deterministic output may conflict with a commission's legal obligation to exercise discretion. A brief discussion of how the toolbox fits into (vs. replaces) commission deliberation would make the paper more useful for commission practice.
- **The efficiency gap metric should appear alongside the proportionality gap.** The paper uses "proportionality gap" (seats-votes gap) as the primary partisan metric throughout. The efficiency gap (Stephanopoulos & McGhee 2015) is the dominant metric in redistricting litigation and is measured differently. Reporting both would allow readers to compare the bakeoff results against the litigation-relevant threshold (EG > 8pp is the plaintiff-friendly threshold identified in the empirical literature).

## Score: 3 / 4 — Minor Revision

The paper makes a genuine and important contribution to the intersection of redistricting law and algorithm design. The P1 items are substantive legal accuracy issues — the Callais opinion structure question (P1.1) could affect the entire constitutional architecture the paper builds on top of it. The county-preservation legal nuance (P1.3) affects the paper's claim to satisfy R4. Both can be addressed in revision without restructuring. Recommended for publication after revision.
