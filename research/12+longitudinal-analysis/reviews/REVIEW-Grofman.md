# Review: Twenty Years of Congressional Redistricting

**Reviewer**: Bernard Grofman (UC Irvine)
**Expertise**: Electoral systems, representation theory, empirical political science
**Date**: 2026-02-08
**Round**: 1

---

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a solid empirical contribution by documenting compactness trends across 2000-2020 and quantifying commission effectiveness. The longitudinal design is well-executed and the findings are interesting. However, the theoretical framing is weak, the literature review is thin, and the representation implications are underexplored. The paper reads more like a technical report than a scholarly article suitable for Science or APSR.

**Verdict**: Accept with major revisions

---

## Score

**3.0 / 4.0** — Good empirical work but needs stronger theoretical grounding

---

## Major Issues

### M1. Theoretical Contribution Unclear

**Issue**: The paper claims to make "theoretical contributions" (Discussion, Section 7.3) but doesn't actually advance representation theory. The three claimed contributions are:
1. "Temporal stability" — this is an empirical finding, not a theory
2. "Gerrymandering evolution" — descriptive trend, not theoretical
3. "Reform measurement" — methodological contribution, not theoretical

**Evidence**: No formal model, no testable hypotheses beyond descriptive correlations, no engagement with representation theory literature (Pitkin, Mansbridge, Dahl).

**Impact**: The paper lacks theoretical depth expected of Science/APSR. It answers "what happened?" (compactness declined) but not "why?" or "so what for representation?"

**Recommendation**:
1. Develop theoretical framework: What is the relationship between compactness and representation quality?
2. Engage with representation theory: Does geometric compactness improve descriptive representation? Substantive representation?
3. Formulate testable hypotheses: e.g., "Compact districts will show higher voter turnout" or "Commission states will have more competitive elections"
4. Or: reframe as purely empirical paper and drop "theoretical contributions" claim

---

### M2. Literature Review Superficial

**Issue**: Background section (2) cites only 5 papers, omitting vast redistricting literature:

**Missing**:
- Classic work: Polsby & Popper (1991) cited but not discussed in depth
- Partisan metrics: Efficiency gap (Stephanopoulos & McGhee 2015), mean-median (McDonald & Best 2015)
- Commission studies: Cain & Miller (2015), Masket et al. (2012)
- Algorithmic approaches: Altman & McDonald (2010), Chen & Rodden (2013), DeFord et al. (2019)
- VRA literature: Grofman & Handley (1989), Pildes (2002)

**Impact**: Paper appears uninformed by decades of redistricting scholarship. Readers will question whether authors understand the field.

**Recommendation**: Expand Section 2 to 2-3 pages. Add subsections:
- 2.1: Redistricting criteria (compactness, contiguity, VRA, COI)
- 2.2: Measuring gerrymandering (geometric vs partisan metrics)
- 2.3: Reform interventions (commissions, courts, algorithms)
- 2.4: Longitudinal studies (position this paper in that tradition)

---

### M3. Representation Implications Unexamined

**Issue**: The paper analyzes redistricting but never asks whether compactness affects representation quality. Does compactness improve:
- Voter turnout?
- Constituent-legislator contact?
- Responsiveness to local issues?
- Minority representation?

**Evidence**: Discussion mentions "fair representation" (abstract, conclusion) but never defines or measures it. Compactness is treated as an end in itself, not a means to better representation.

**Impact**: The "so what?" question is unanswered. Why should voters care about compactness? The paper assumes compactness = fairness without justification.

**Recommendation**:
1. Add Section 5: "Representation Outcomes" analyzing:
   - Turnout trends in high vs low compactness states
   - Election competitiveness (safe vs swing districts)
   - Majority-minority district preservation
2. Or: Explicitly acknowledge representation outcomes as beyond scope, but discuss implications speculatively

---

## Minor Issues

### m1. Commission Case Studies Needed

You cite California (+8.2% compactness) and Michigan (+6.4%) as successes but don't explain *why*. What did these commissions do differently?

**Recommendation**: Add case study boxes: CA 2010 commission process, MI 2018 ballot initiative. Explain causal mechanisms.

---

### m2. Comparative Context Missing

How does U.S. redistricting compare internationally? Other democracies use independent commissions (Canada, UK, Australia). Does their experience inform U.S. reform?

**Recommendation**: Add paragraph on comparative context, citing international literature.

---

### m3. Counterfactuals Unexplored

What would 2020 look like if *all* states adopted commissions in 2010? You have the data (algorithmic baseline ≈ commission upper bound). Model this counterfactual.

**Recommendation**: Add Section 7.4: "Counterfactual Analysis" projecting national compactness under universal commission adoption.

---

## Strengths

1. **Empirical rigor**: Longitudinal design with consistent methods is exemplary
2. **Policy relevance**: Commission effectiveness quantification useful for reform advocates
3. **Comprehensive scope**: 50 states, 20 years, large-N analysis
4. **Clear writing**: Paper is well-structured and accessible

---

## Questions for Authors

1. What is your definition of "fair representation"? How does compactness contribute?
2. Have you examined representation outcomes (turnout, competitiveness) associated with compactness?
3. Can you model counterfactual scenarios (e.g., universal commissions)?

---

**Bottom Line**: Solid empirical analysis but needs stronger theoretical framing and engagement with representation theory literature. The technical work is good, but scholarly contribution is limited without connecting compactness to representation quality.
