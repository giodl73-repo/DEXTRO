## Summary Assessment

The authors have made substantial improvements addressing my primary concerns from Round 0. The paper now explicitly frames itself as a geometric fairness study with appropriate caveats about partisan analysis, adds rigorous statistical evidence with effect sizes and confidence intervals, and tempers all causality claims with proper correlational language. These revisions meaningfully strengthen the work. I raise my score from 3.0 to 3.5.

## Assessment of P1 Revisions

### P1.1: Scope Reframing (Addressed ✓)

**Original concern**: Missing partisan metrics undermined claims about gerrymandering and reform effectiveness.

**Author response**: Reframed as "geometric fairness only" study with explicit scope statement in Introduction and strengthened limitations.

**Assessment**: **Fully addressed**. The new scope statement (Introduction, after methodology description) clearly delineates geometric vs partisan fairness, explicitly noting "geometric properties do not fully capture redistricting quality." The enhanced limitations section (Discussion, Section 7.4.2) provides excellent three-part framework distinguishing geometric/partisan/representational fairness. This is exactly what was needed. The paper no longer overpromises on partisan claims.

**Minor note**: The Abstract still mentions "gerrymandering worsened as technology improved" (line 8)—this is acceptable given the geometric interpretation, but consider whether "non-compact districts increased" would be more precise given the new framing.

### P1.2: Statistical Rigor (Addressed ✓)

**Original concern**: Commission effectiveness claim (+7.3pp) lacked effect size, confidence intervals, and robustness checks.

**Author response**: Added Cohen's d = 1.64, 95% CI [2.1pp, 12.5pp], comprehensive robustness checks subsection.

**Assessment**: **Excellently addressed**. The new robustness checks subsection (Section 5.4.1) is exemplary:
- Alternative metrics (Reock: +6.8pp, p=0.014) confirm metric-independence
- Outlier sensitivity tests show effect persists excluding CA (+2.7pp, p=0.041)
- Confound controls via multivariate regression (β=6.9pp, p=0.009)
- Pre-trend analysis rules out selection on pre-existing trajectory

The effect size (Cohen's d = 1.64) is correctly labeled as "large" and the 95% CI properly quantifies uncertainty. This is publication-quality statistical reporting. The interpretation ("strong correlational evidence... observational data cannot establish causation") strikes the right balance.

### P1.5: Causality Tempering (Addressed ✓)

**Original concern**: Language like "commissions improved outcomes" overstated causal claims from observational data.

**Author response**: Changed all "improved" to "associated with" throughout; added selection bias caveats.

**Assessment**: **Thoroughly addressed**. Every instance I spot-checked now uses appropriate correlational framing:
- Abstract: "were associated with... (though causality cannot be definitively established)"
- Section 5.4: "were associated with a 7.3 percentage point improvement"
- Discussion: "correlate with reduced partisan gerrymandering... causality cannot be definitively established"
- Causality limitation (7.4.3): "commission adoption is non-random... cannot establish causation"

The new language is scientifically appropriate for observational data.

## Remaining Issues

### Minor Issues

1. **Terminology consistency**: Abstract uses "gerrymandering worsened" while body now avoids this term in favor of "non-compact districts." Consider harmonizing.

2. **Data Availability**: The new section provides GitHub/Zenodo placeholders but says "to be published upon acceptance." Consider Zenodo pre-registration for reproducibility even before acceptance.

3. **Commission disaggregation**: Still treats all commissions equally. Independent commissions (CA, CO, MI) vs advisory (NY) likely have different effects. A 2-3 sentence acknowledgment in limitations would suffice.

## Verdict

**Accept with minor revisions**. The P1 improvements successfully address all blocking concerns. The paper now makes appropriate claims supported by rigorous evidence. The geometric-only scope is clearly communicated. The remaining issues are truly minor and would not block publication.

**Recommended venue shift**: With the geometric-only framing, this paper is now better suited to **Political Analysis** or **Political Geography** than Science. Science typically expects partisan analysis for redistricting papers. The authors should consider resubmitting to a specialized venue where geometric analysis is the primary focus.

**Congratulations** to the authors on a substantial revision that meaningfully improves the work.
