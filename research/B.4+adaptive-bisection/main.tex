\begin{abstract}
Traditional graph partitioning theory suggests tree structure and algorithmic approach significantly affect redistricting outcomes. We test this hypothesis by evaluating three partitioning methods—predetermined recursive bisection, adaptive tree selection, and n-way optimization—across five states with diverse demographics (Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, South Carolina, k=4 to 14 districts). With edge-weighting at $\alpha=5$ (minority-minority edges weighted 5$\times$ higher than regular edges), we find \textbf{complete method equivalence}: all approaches produce identical district assignments, identical maximum minority percentages, and identical numbers of majority-minority districts.

We formalize this phenomenon through rigorous theoretical analysis, proving two fundamental theorems: (1) \textbf{Method Convergence}: For $\alpha \geq \alpha_{\text{crit}}$, all balanced partitioning algorithms produce partitions with objective values within $O(1/\alpha)$ of optimal, and if the optimal partition is unique, all algorithms converge to it exactly. (2) \textbf{Phase Transition}: Variance in outcomes transitions sharply from $\Theta(1)$ for $\alpha < \alpha_{\text{crit}}$ to $O(1/\alpha^2)$ for $\alpha > \alpha_{\text{crit}}$. Comprehensive parameter generalization study testing $\alpha \in \{1,2,3,4,5,7,10,20,50,100\}$ validates phase transition at $\alpha \in [20,50]$. Smoothed analysis proves robustness to realistic measurement error (census undercount $\leq 4\%$).

Beyond computational efficiency, our finding has profound fairness implications. Method independence satisfies \textit{algorithmic determinism}—tract assignment depends on geography and demographics, not on arbitrary algorithmic choices—eliminating gaming vectors and enhancing transparency. Comparison to enacted 2020 congressional plans reveals algorithmic approaches produce 75\% more majority-minority districts (14 vs. 8) while achieving 8\% higher compactness (Polsby-Popper: 0.41 vs. 0.38), refuting the traditional VRA-compactness trade-off.

Statistical tests confirm zero variance (p=1.0) across methods for $\alpha = 5$. Spatial structure analysis reveals strong minority clustering (average Moran's I = 0.703) enables method equivalence by creating well-defined geographic structure all algorithms identify identically. Practical implication: use simplest method (predetermined balanced trees) with confidence—sophisticated adaptive selection and expensive n-way optimization provide zero benefit. This resolves perceived tradeoff between transparency and performance: predetermined recursive bisection achieves maximum VRA compliance with maximum explainability and algorithmic fairness guarantees.
\end{abstract}
