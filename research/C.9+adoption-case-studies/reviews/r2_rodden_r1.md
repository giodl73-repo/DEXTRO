# Review 2 — Reviewer: Jonathan Rodden (Political Geography / Redistricting Reform)
**Paper:** C.9 — From Algorithm to Map: Implementation Case Studies for Three Adoption Pathways
**Round:** 1
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

A practically useful paper that bridges the gap between algorithmic redistricting research and institutional implementation. The three case studies are well-chosen to represent distinct adoption pathways, and the workflow table is a genuine contribution to the implementation literature. My concerns are about the political feasibility of the community-of-interest adjustment authority and the geographic implications of the California proportionality analysis.

## Strengths

The institutional analysis is the paper's strongest contribution. The observation that the AIRC's constitutional authority is established (Arizona State Legislature v. AIRC), that the CRC's criteria map onto redist parameters, and that the court-order pathway shifts the burden of proof are all accurate and important. The characterization of the court-order pathway as "the most legally stable of the three" is well-argued.

The community-of-interest guardrails in Section 2.3 (Arizona case) are well-designed. The two guardrails — geographic justification for adjustments, documented factual finding that the algorithm separated the community — are exactly the kind of institutional constraints that prevent the "community of interest" adjustment authority from becoming a backdoor for partisan manipulation.

The discussion of the challenge process (Section 6.3) is accurate and practically useful. The observation that algorithmic maps shift the challenge from intent-based to effects-based is important: after *Rucho*, intent-based partisan gerrymandering challenges are unavailable in federal court, and the algorithmic approach eliminates the intent evidence that makes state-court challenges viable under state constitutional standards.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

The paper's treatment of community-of-interest adjustments may be too optimistic about their political manageability. The AIRC's history includes significant controversy over community-of-interest decisions — the 2012 AIRC redistricting generated a lawsuit (*Harris v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission*, decided 2016) in which plaintiffs alleged that the commission drew maps to favor Democrats under the guise of VRA compliance. The paper should acknowledge that community-of-interest adjustment authority is historically a vector for political controversy, even with the guardrails described.

For the California case, Section 3.3 notes that CRC commissioners have "historically considered proportionality as a non-binding guideline." But California's CRC process has been criticized (and praised) for using partisan data in assessing proportionality. The paper's claim that "algorithmic maps for California tend to produce partisan outcomes close to the statewide Democratic vote share (approximately 63% in 2020 presidential results)" needs to be evaluated against whether algorithmic maps satisfy California's proportionality norm. If an algorithmic map produces 65% Democratic seats in a 63%-Democratic state, the CRC would likely find it adequate. But if the map produces 55% Democratic seats due to geographic sorting, would the CRC consider this proportional? The paper doesn't address this scenario.

The political feasibility of the statute pathway (Section 3 — California CRC) is understated. California's CRC already produces maps that are largely algorithmically achievable (the paper notes this in the conclusion). The more challenging statute pathway case would be a state without an existing commission — like Ohio or Wisconsin — where adoption of algorithmic redistricting would require displacing an entrenched partisan process. The paper should acknowledge that the commission pathway and statute pathway are easier where independent institutions already exist, and harder where they don't.

## Minor Issues

- The paper uses *Harper v. Hall* as the court-order pathway case study but acknowledges the "subsequent procedural complexity" (the North Carolina Supreme Court reconsidered the decision after a change in court composition). The paper notes it "uses the Harper fact pattern without taking a position on North Carolina's subsequent procedural history." This is a reasonable disclaimer, but it undersells the institutional fragility the *Harper* story illustrates: court-ordered redistricting remedies can be undone when court composition changes. The paper should note this vulnerability.
- The paper's claim that "the special master structure is well-established in federal redistricting practice" is correct, but the citation to federal practice is relevant to state courts only insofar as state procedural rules permit special masters. Not all states have the same procedural authority as federal courts to appoint special masters with map-drawing authority.

## Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. Add discussion of the Harris v. AIRC controversy as a cautionary note on community-of-interest adjustments, and analyze the California proportionality question under geographic sorting constraints.
