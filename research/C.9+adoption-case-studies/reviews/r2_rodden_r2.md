# Review 2 — Reviewer: Jonathan Rodden (Political Geography / Redistricting Reform)
**Paper:** C.9 — From Algorithm to Map: Implementation Case Studies for Three Adoption Pathways
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

The revision addresses both of my principal concerns: the *Harris v. AIRC* cautionary note is now present in the Arizona section, and the California proportionality analysis is developed. The *Harper* reversal in the North Carolina section is now honestly disclosed (see Stephanopoulos's concern). I maintain my score at 3/4. The paper is a practical and legally honest contribution.

## Addressed Issues

The *Harris v. AIRC* note is now present in Section 2.3. The paper acknowledges that community-of-interest adjustment authority has been litigated as partisan manipulation in the Arizona context, and that the two guardrails proposed (geographic justification, documented factual finding) are designed to address the specific defect alleged in *Harris*. This is the cautionary note I requested, and it is well-framed — the paper does not undermine its own argument but rather strengthens it by showing awareness of the failure mode.

The California proportionality analysis is now present in Section 3.3. The paper addresses the scenario I raised: if geographic sorting means the algorithmic map produces fewer Democratic seats than the statewide Democratic vote share, would the CRC find it proportional? The answer is that the CRC's proportionality guideline is non-binding, and that the C.8 finding (algorithmic maps have near-zero efficiency gap) is the relevant response: if the map has no systematic partisan bias, the proportionality concern is answered.

The political feasibility discussion now acknowledges that the commission and statute pathways are easier where independent institutions already exist. The paper notes that Ohio and Wisconsin would face different adoption challenges than Arizona and California, and identifies institutional barriers for each.

## Remaining Concerns

The generalizability claim remains somewhat overstated. The paper now qualifies it ("the three pathways analyzed here provide institutional templates") but the qualification is brief. A more systematic treatment of which states are plausible early adopters, based on their current redistricting frameworks, would strengthen the claim. The paper mentions Michigan, Colorado, and Virginia as candidates but does not analyze their specific institutional situations.

The *Harper* reversal treatment (Section 4) is present and honest (see also Stephanopoulos's analysis). The paper correctly reframes the case study as illustrating the court-order pathway pattern rather than the current North Carolina situation. From a geographic perspective, the North Carolina case is still the right example: the I-35 corridor argument I raised in B.18 is less relevant here (C.9 is about institutional process, not geographic distribution of disruption), but the reframing is appropriate.

## Minor Issues

- The redistricting timeline concern I raised (60-day public comment period plus adjustment plus challenge time) is now addressed in Section 6.1: the paper acknowledges the timeline pressure and notes that expedited procedures are available in some states. This is adequate.
- The "California CRC resembles algorithmic output" observation is now more developed (cross-referencing C.8's finding), which is the right connection.

## Recommendation

Accept. The *Harris* cautionary note and the California proportionality analysis are genuine improvements. The paper is now a balanced and practically useful implementation guide.
