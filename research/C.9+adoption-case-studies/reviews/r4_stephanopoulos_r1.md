# Review 4 — Reviewer: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law / Constitutional Redistricting)
**Paper:** C.9 — From Algorithm to Map: Implementation Case Studies for Three Adoption Pathways
**Round:** 1
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

This is the paper in the portfolio most directly relevant to constitutional law and redistricting jurisprudence. The legal analysis is generally accurate, but there are several important legal errors and omissions that must be corrected before publication. The treatment of *Harper v. Hall* is the most significant issue. The paper also underestimates the constitutional risks of the court-order pathway.

## Strengths

The Arizona analysis (Section 2) is legally accurate. The AIRC's constitutional authority is correctly established (*Arizona State Legislature v. AIRC*, 576 U.S. 787 (2015)), and the criteria priority ordering is correctly stated. The two community-of-interest guardrails are well-designed and legally defensible.

The California criteria analysis (Section 3.1) correctly notes that California's criteria do not include partisan fairness as a statutory criterion, distinguishing it from Arizona. This distinction is legally significant: a court reviewing a California algorithmic map would apply only the six statutory criteria, not a partisan proportionality standard.

The burden-shifting argument for the court-order pathway (Section 4.3) is legally sound. The observation that algorithmic maps shift the challenge from intent-based to effects-based is consistent with the *Washington v. Davis* framework: discriminatory effect without discriminatory intent requires a much stronger showing for constitutional violation.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

The *Harper v. Hall* analysis is legally incomplete and potentially misleading. The paper says it "uses the Harper fact pattern without taking a position on North Carolina's subsequent procedural history." But the subsequent history is not peripheral — it is the entire point. After Justice Anita Earls was briefly suspended pending an ethics investigation (related to criticism of the majority's political leanings), the reconstituted North Carolina Supreme Court reversed *Harper v. Hall* in 2023, holding that there is no judicially manageable standard for partisan gerrymandering under the North Carolina Constitution. This reversal means that the *Harper* legal framework — state-court partisan gerrymandering review leading to algorithmic remedial maps — is currently *not viable* in North Carolina. Using *Harper* as the case study for the court-order pathway without explaining this procedural history is misleading.

The paper should either (a) update the case study to acknowledge the *Harper* reversal and argue that the court-order pathway remains available in *other* states (e.g., Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin) that have maintained state-court partisan gerrymandering review, or (b) reframe the North Carolina case study as a model for what the court-order pathway could look like in a favorable state-court jurisdiction, not specifically North Carolina as of 2026.

The paper overstates the legal stability of the court-order pathway in one specific way. Section 4.3 states that "because the algorithm does not access partisan data, no partisan intent can be attributed to the mapmaker; partisan-outcome challenges must rest on effects rather than intent." But effects-based partisan gerrymandering claims are available under some state constitutional frameworks. In Pennsylvania (*LWV v. Commonwealth*, 2018), the state supreme court applied an effects-based test for partisan gerrymandering under the state constitution's free and equal elections clause — and this effects test does not require proof of intent. An algorithm that produces Republican-leaning outcomes (even without Republican intent) could still be challenged under a Pennsylvania-style effects test. The paper should acknowledge this vulnerability.

The treatment of the VRA constraint on adjustments is legally underdeveloped. Section 4.2 notes that VRASection weighting is applied "to protect the Black-majority district in the northeastern part of the state (the historical Gingles area)." But *Allen v. Milligan* (2023) held that Section 2 of the VRA requires Alabama to draw a *second* Black-opportunity district, not merely protect the existing one. The court-order pathway for North Carolina (or any state with a comparable Black population distribution) must grapple with whether VRASection produces the *number* of majority-minority districts required by Section 2, not just whether it protects existing ones. This is a substantive legal question the paper does not address.

## Minor Issues

- The paper's reference to "post-*Harper*" conditions should be updated to reflect the *Harper* reversal.
- The public transparency section (6.2) correctly identifies the challenge of explaining algorithmic outputs to communities. But the paper should note that transparency requirements may have legal force in some states: California's redistricting criteria include a public explanation requirement, and California courts have found redistricting decisions inadequately justified when commissioners could not explain specific boundary choices. This is a real legal risk for the "geometric consequence" explanation the paper offers.
- Citation: the paper cites *Harper v. Hall* for the NC case but should also cite *Moore v. Harper* (2023), which addressed the related independent-state-legislature theory and is relevant to the Elections Clause analysis underlying the commission pathway.

## Recommendation

Major revisions required. Correct the *Harper v. Hall* case study to account for the 2023 reversal. Add analysis of effects-based state-court partisan gerrymandering claims as a vulnerability for the court-order pathway. Develop the *Allen v. Milligan* implication for the number of VRA-required districts in the court-order pathway.
