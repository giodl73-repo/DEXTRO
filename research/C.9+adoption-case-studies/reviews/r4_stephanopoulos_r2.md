# Review 4 — Reviewer: Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Election Law / Constitutional Redistricting)
**Paper:** C.9 — From Algorithm to Map: Implementation Case Studies for Three Adoption Pathways
**Round:** 2
**Score:** 3/4

## Summary

My Round 1 score of 3/4 with a "major revisions required" note was driven by the *Harper v. Hall* reversal — the paper used *Harper* as the primary case study for the court-order pathway without disclosing that the 2023 reversal means the *Harper* legal framework is currently not viable in North Carolina. The revision has corrected this directly and clearly. I maintain 3/4 as an acceptance score. The remaining concerns are about depth, not accuracy.

## Addressed Issues

The *Harper* reversal treatment (Section 4) is now fully and honestly disclosed. The paper includes a dedicated note explaining the 2023 reversal (reconstituted Republican majority reversed the earlier holding, finding no judicially manageable standard for partisan gerrymandering under the NC Constitution). The case study is now explicitly framed as illustrating "the *Harper* model — what the court-order pathway looks like when state courts maintain partisan gerrymandering jurisdiction." The alternative states where the framework currently applies (Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin) are named. This is exactly the reframing I required.

The *Allen v. Milligan* VRA implication is now addressed in Section 4.2: the paper explains that VRASection must produce the *number* of majority-minority districts required by Section 2, not merely protect existing ones. The connection to the post-*Allen* remedial map context (where Alabama needed a second majority-Black district) is made explicit, and the paper explains how VRASection's optimization across the full state generates the required number of minority-opportunity districts. This is the substantive legal update I requested.

*Moore v. Harper* (2023) is now cited in the Arizona AIRC section with the sentence I suggested: the Court's rejection of the independent-state-legislature theory reinforces *AIRC*'s holding that state legislatures may delegate redistricting authority to independent commissions.

The effects-based state-court challenge vulnerability is now acknowledged in Section 4.3: the paper notes that Pennsylvania-style effects tests (under the free and equal elections clause) could challenge algorithmic maps that produce partisan outcomes, even without intent evidence. The response — that the C.8 finding of near-zero efficiency gap for algorithmic maps is the primary defense — is correct.

The *Karcher v. Daggett* citation is present in the constitutional analysis, cross-referencing B.18's analysis.

## Remaining Concerns

The *Allen v. Milligan* response, while present, is somewhat brief. The paper says VRASection's optimization "generates the required number of minority-opportunity districts" — but the evidence for this claim is a cross-reference to D.5. The D.5 paper demonstrates the Gingles methodology for Alabama specifically, but does not systematically demonstrate that VRASection produces the required number of minority-opportunity districts in every state with a Section 2 obligation. This remains an empirical claim that is not fully supported in either paper.

The transparency requirement for California courts (my minor concern from Round 1 — that California courts have found redistricting decisions inadequately justified when commissioners could not explain specific boundary choices) is addressed in a footnote. This is adequate.

The effects-based challenge discussion could note that the Alabama *Allen* context provides the strongest test: in a state where racial composition and partisan alignment are highly correlated, an algorithmically drawn remedial map would face the most searching effects-based scrutiny, and the near-zero efficiency gap finding from C.8 may not carry over directly to the effects-based framework.

## Minor Issues

- The paper now explicitly identifies Michigan, Colorado, and Wisconsin as candidate early-adoption states, noting the institutional barriers each would need to overcome. This is the specificity I requested.
- The *Harper* procedural note correctly describes the Justice Earls suspension/ethics investigation context that preceded the reconstitution. The paper's description is accurate and adequate.

## Recommendation

Accept. The *Harper* reversal treatment is now complete and legally accurate. The *Allen v. Milligan* update addresses the VRA district count question. The paper is now ready for publication as a legal implementation guide.
